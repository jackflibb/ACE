Volume 25, Issue 4, 1 May 2005, Pages 1279–1291

Amygdala and nucleus accumbens in responses to receipt and omission of gains in adults and adolescents

  • Monique Ernst, ,
  • Eric E. Nelson,
  • Sandra Jazbec,
  • Erin B. McClure,
  • Christopher S. Monk,
  • Ellen Leibenluft,
  • James Blair,
  • Daniel S. Pine
  • Mood and Anxiety Disorders Program, National Institute of Mental Health, National Institutes of Health, Department of Health and Human Services, 15K North Drive, Bethesda MD 20892-2670, USA
Received 25 October 2004, Revised 3 December 2004, Accepted 8 December 2004, Available online 17 February 2005

Abstract

Adolescents' propensity for risk-taking and reward-seeking behaviors suggests a heightened sensitivity for reward, reflected by greater feedback-related activity changes in reward circuitry (e.g., nucleus accumbens), and/or a lower sensitivity to potential harm reflected by weaker feedback-related activity changes in avoidance circuitry (e.g., amygdala) relative to adults. Responses of nucleus accumbens and amygdala to valenced outcomes (reward receipt and reward omission) were assayed using an event-related functional magnetic resonance imaging procedure paired with a monetary reward task in 14 adults and 16 adolescents. Bilateral amygdala and nucleus accumbens showed significantly greater activation when winning than when failing to win in both groups. Group comparisons revealed stronger activation of left nucleus accumbens by adolescents, and of left amygdala by adults. When examining responses to reward receipts and to reward omissions separately, the most robust group difference was within the amygdala during reward omission. The reduction of the fMRI BOLD signal in the amygdala in response to reward omission was larger for adults than for adolescents. Correlations showed a close link between negative emotion and amygdala decreased BOLD signal in adults, and between positive emotion and nucleus accumbens activation in adolescents. Overall, these findings support the notion that the signal differences between positive and negative outcomes involve the nucleus accumbens more in adolescents than in adults, and the amygdala more in adults than in adolescents. These developmental differences, if replicated, may have important implications for the development of early-onset disorders of emotion and motivation.

Introduction

Adolescence is a critical period characterized by high levels of risk-taking, impulsivity, and novelty-seeking (Arnett, 1999). Adolescents present vulnerability for gambling (Chambers and Potenza, 2003) and addiction (Chambers et al., 2003). Their propensity for risk-taking may relate to increased responses to reward (Laviola et al., 2003), and reduced harm avoidance (Maggs et al., 1995 and Wills et al., 1994). Consummatory processing (responses to outcomes) influences the motivation to select specific courses of actions, and subsequently can indirectly affect pattern of decision-making.

A growing body of work in animals is being conducted to better understand the biological basis of reward-related processing. Single-cell recording studies reveal that dopamine neurons in non-human primates are activated at the receipt of unpredicted rewards, emit no signal after a predicted reward, and emit a negative signal (decreased spike production from baseline) when a predicted reward fails to occur (Schultz et al., 1997).

Similarly, neuroimaging studies in human adults (Breiter et al., 2001, Delgado et al., 2000, Knutson et al., 2001 and O'Doherty et al., 2001) reveal that reward receipt enhances activity of subcortical structures (i.e., striatum and amygdala), and of cortical regions (i.e., mesial prefrontal cortex), whereas reward omission, or exposure to losses, tends to reduce the fMRI BOLD signal in these same structures.

At present, two neuroimaging studies have examined reward circuitry in adolescents (Bjork et al., 2004 and May et al., 2004), only one with an adult comparison group (Bjork et al., 2004). Bjork et al. (2004) found less activation of the right nucleus accumbens (NAcc) during anticipation of gains in adolescents than in adults. They interpreted this finding as indicating a selectively weaker recruitment of motivational, but not consummatory, components of reward-directed behavior in adolescents. Here, we propose that age-related differences in consummatory behavior and underlying neural substrates may contribute to risk-taking in adolescence.

Whereas increased risk-taking in adolescents can relate to greater reward responses, through enhanced appetitive value of positive outcomes (Arnett, 1992, Gardner and Herman, 1991 and Laviola et al., 2003), it can also indicate decreased response to danger or threat, through a decreased aversiveness of negative outcomes, leading to reduced harm avoidance behavior (Maggs et al., 1995, Wills et al., 1994 and Wilson and Daly, 1985). The amygdala circuitry is critically involved in anticipation of negative outcomes but also in the response to negative stimuli (LeDoux, 2000). Although participating in the emotional coding of both appetitive and aversive stimuli (Baxter and Murray, 2002 and Zald, 2003), the amygdala can function as a behavioral “brake” (Zald, 2003). Its functional characteristics during adolescence are virtually unknown (Killgore et al., 2001 and Yurgelun-Todd et al., 2003), although neuroimaging studies suggest distinct patterns of amygdala modulation in affective paradigms during adolescence and adulthood (Monk et al., 2003). These studies have focused on various aspects of the amygdala, including its modulation by attention during processing of fearful facial expression (Monk et al., 2003 and Thomas et al., 2001), and its response to passive viewing of fearful and neutral faces (Thomas et al., 2001). Adolescents seemed to be more sensitive to the emotional content than to the attentional demands of the task in Monk et al.'s (2003) study, and to be more responsive to neutral vs. fearful expressions than adults in Thomas et al.'s (2001) study. None of these studies have examined reward-related processes per se.

Here, we test whether adults and adolescents differ in the NAcc's and amygdala's involvement in reward-related processes, specifically in response to reinforcers. Converging data emerge from three different perspectives: the approach/avoidance model of adolescent behavior (Chambers et al., 2003 and Spear, 2000), the available neuroimaging findings in adults (Breiter et al., 2001, Delgado et al., 2000, Knutson et al., 2001 and O'Doherty et al., 2001), and the electrophysiological work in primates (Schultz, 2002 and Schultz et al., 1997). Taken together, this work generates the hypotheses that (1) responses to outcome will affect the NAcc more in adolescents than in adults, and (2) the amygdala more in adults than in adolescents, and (3) that self-ratings of affective responses will correlate with regional activation.

Methods

Sample

Eighteen healthy adolescents and 16 healthy adults participated in the study. Data from four subjects (2 adolescents and 2 adults) were excluded from the analysis because of excessive head movement (greater than 1.5 mm in any one direction). Subjects were recruited through newspaper advertisements and were financially compensated for their participation. Inclusion criteria were right-handedness, age between 9 and 17 years for adolescents and 20 and 40 years for adults, absence of past and present psychiatric disorders on the basis of a psychiatric diagnostic interview [Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia of School-Age Children-Present and Lifetime version for the adolescents (Kaufman et al., 2000 and Spitzer et al., 1992), and Structured Clinical Interview for DSM-IV for adults (Segal et al., 1994)], and absence of acute or chronic medical illnesses on the basis of medical history and physical examination. All adult participants and parents of adolescent participants signed a consent form after having been explained the study in detail. All adolescents signed an assent form. The study was approved by the NIMH Institutional Review Board.

Task (Fig. 1)

The Wheel of Fortune (WOF) task is a computerized two-choice decision-making task involving probabilistic monetary outcomes (Ernst et al., 2004). Subjects were instructed that they could take home the money they won and that they should try to win as much money as possible. In each trial, a wheel of fortune (circle divided into two slices of different colors, blue and magenta) was presented to the subjects (Fig. 1).

Fig. 1. 

Wheel of fortune task. Presentation of 129 trials, including 18 trials with 50% chance of winning $4.00 (W4) or not winning $4.00 (N4), and 18 trials with 50% chance of winning $0.50 (W.5) or not winning $0.50 (N.5). Each trial consists in a selection phase (3 s) during which subjects select one color, an anticipation phase (4 s) during which subjects rate how confident they are in winning, and a feedback phase (4 s) during which subjects are shown whether they won or not and are asked to rate how they feel on a 5-point scale.

Four types of monetary wheels of fortune, differing on probability and magnitude of reward, were presented in random order throughout the task. Two of these wheels were used in this report. They included a high-reward and a low-reward condition. They were each divided into equal slices, and thus represented a 50% chance of choosing the winning slice. For the high-reward 50% wheel, the dollar amount was $4.00, and for the low-reward 50% wheel, the dollar amount was $0.50. This report is focused on the effects of magnitude, and not probability, of outcomes. Differences in probability of outcomes associated with variable levels of uncertainty have been shown to modulate regional neural activity driven by dopaminergic transmission (Berns et al., 2001, Fiorillo et al., 2003 and O'Doherty et al., 2004). This complicates the interpretation of the effects of magnitude of potential gains. Thus, only the trials involving 50% chance of winning were used for the analysis, to avoid the potential confound of varying probability. The examination of the effects of risk and probability of outcome on reward circuits will be presented in a separate report. The other two wheels were divided into unequal slices (10% slice and 90% slice in the high-conflict wheel, and 30% and 70% in the low-conflict wheel), with the smaller slice paired with the higher dollar amount ($4.00–10% and $0.50–90%; $2.00–30% and $1.00–70%).

Subjects were asked to select one of the slices by its color (blue or magenta) using a button press. For example, when the magenta slice was on the left, and subjects opted for this color, they pressed the left button. If the computer randomly selected the same color as the subject, the subject won the designated amount of money (receipt of reward); if the computer randomly selected the other color, the subject won nothing (omission of reward). Order of presentation for all conditions was fully randomized (Fig. 1).

Subjects were trained prior to the fMRI session on a real wheel of fortune that matched the computer game. During training, subjects selected one slice; the wheel was spun; and the subject won the dollar amount paired with the selected slice, if the slice stopped under the pointer. Otherwise the subject won nothing. The subject was told that the computer version of the task operated under the same principle, except that the spinning of the wheel was not visible.

The task comprised three runs of 7.2 min and 43 trials each. Of a total of 129 trials, 18 were high-reward 50% wheels, 18 were low-reward 50% wheels, and 18 were fixation trials to serve as baseline. They were distributed randomly across the three runs. Each wheel trial lasted 11 s and included a selection phase (3 s), an anticipation phase (4 s), and a feedback phase (4 s) (Fig. 1). Only the feedback phase was analyzed, which comprised responses to positive or negative outcomes. During feedback, subjects viewed the dollar amount won ($0 if not won), and a 5-point rating scale along which they were asked to rate how they felt. A positive 5-point scale was shown for favorable outcomes (1 neutral, 5 very good), and a negative 5-point scale was used for unfavorable outcomes (1 neutral, 5 very bad). All responses were recorded on a 5-key button-box in the fMRI scanner (MRI Devices; Waukesha, WI), and all button presses were done using the right hand. After leaving the scanning room, subjects completed a questionnaire, which consisted of questions related to their experience of the task, such as “how hard was it to choose between the two slices?”; “did you have a strategy?”; “did you have trouble lying still?”. Answers were made on a 4-point rating scale (1 not at all, 4 extremely). Since none of these questions yielded significant differences between adults and adolescents, they were not used in the analysis.

fMRI

A General Electric Signa 3 T scanner was employed. Head movement was restricted by the use of foam padding. Visual images were presented via Avotec Silent Vision Glasses (Stuart, FL) located directly above subjects' eyes. All participants were acclimated to the MRI environment with an MRI simulator prior to the study.

Gradient echo planar (EPI) images were acquired after sagittal localization and a manual shim procedure. EPI images were acquired in 23 contiguous 5 mm axial slices per brain volume positioned parallel to the AC–PC line. Images were acquired using echo planar single shot gradient echo T2* weighting. The following imaging parameters were used: matrix was 64 × 64 mm; TR = 2000 ms; TE = 40 ms; Field of View = 240 mm; voxels were 3.75 × 3.75 × 5 mm. After EPI acquisition, a high resolution T1-weighted anatomical image was acquired to aid with spatial normalization. A standardized magnetization prepared gradient echo sequence was used (180, 1 mm sagittal slices, FOV = 256, NEX = 1, TR = 11.4 ms, TE = 4.4 ms, matrix = 256 × 256, TI = 300 ms, bandwidth = 130 Hz/pixel, 33 kHz/256 pixels).

Analysis of the results

Behavioral performance

Performance on the WOF is presented for each group (adults vs. adolescents) as means and standard deviations of the reaction times and the rating scores of “how do you feel” for favorable and unfavorable outcomes separately.

Imaging data

For each subject, reconstructed fMRI images were analyzed using Medx software to check for excessive motion. Data from subjects moving more than 1.5 mm in any plane were discarded. All subsequent analyses were conducted with SPM software (SPM99, Welcome Department of Neurology) and other routines written in Matlab 5.3. Preprocessing of data included, in turn, correction for the sequence of slice acquisition, motion correction, and spatial normalization to the Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) T1-weighted template image supplied with SPM99.

The analysis of the neuroimaging data was based on the assumption that the transform of neural signal to fMRI signal is linear and time-invariant, with a known impulse response function (Zarahn, 2000). This assumption has been shown to be acceptable for events of duration greater than 2 s (Buckner, 1998). At the individual subject level (i.e., time series), event-related response amplitudes were estimated using the General Linear Model (GLM) for each crossing of the 2 (two processes: positive feedback and negative feedback) by 2 (two conditions: high-reward 50% wheel and low-reward 50% wheel) factorial design. In other words, each event (total number of events = 4: 2 processes × 2 conditions) was coded separately. The waveform used to model each type of event-related response in the GLM was a rectangular pulse of the duration of the event (4 s) convolved with the synthetic hemodynamic response function provided by SPM. Contrast images were generated for each subject using pairwise comparisons of the event-related BOLD responses across event types. Prior to group-level analysis, each contrast image was divided by the subject-specific voxel time series means, yielding values proportional to percentage fMRI signal change. These normalized contrast images were then smoothed with an isotropic Gaussian kernel (FWHM = 11.4) to mitigate any non-stationarity in spatial autocorrelation structure introduced by the previous step.

For all group-level analyses, a random effects model was employed to permit population-level inferences (Holmes and Friston, 1998). The main analysis was restricted to four a priori defined regions of interest (ROIs), right and left NAcc, and right and left amygdala. These regions were ascertained from standard anatomical criteria (Duvernoy, 1999, Szeszko, 1999, Szeszko et al., 2002 and Talairach and Tournoux, 1988) on a single MNI template and applied to all normalized brains at the group level. The boundaries of the amygdala were defined anteriorly by the white matter of the parahippocampal gyrus (which provided the anterior, lateral, and inferior borders of the anterior part of the amygdala), and posteriorily by the temporal horn of the lateral ventricle (inferior border), the basal ganglia (superior border), the uncus (medial border), the white matter of the temporal lobe (lateral border), and the alveus of the hippocampus (posterior border). The NAcc was identified at the inferior junction between the head of the caudate and putamen. It was delimited superiorly by a line connecting the inferior corner of the lateral ventricle and the inferior point of the internal capsule abutting the NAcc and laterally by a vertical line passing from the latter point (Breiter et al., 1997). We performed voxel-wise tests in these anatomically defined volumes of interest. Consistent with the current standard (Hariri et al., 2002 and Winston et al., 2002), we utilized the Gaussian random field threshold (α = .05, corrected) with small volume correction implemented in SPM99. In other words, statistical maps were thresholded at P < 0.05 uncorrected and small volume corrections were applied to a priori ROIs (right and left NAcc and right and left amygdala) ( Worsley et al., 1996). Statistical significance of activation in regions of interest was set at P < 0.05 corrected for multiple comparisons.

Four sets of analyses were conducted in these ROIs. These sets compared (1) win $4.00 events with no-win $4.00 events, (2) win $0.50 events with no-win $0.50 events, (3) win $4.00 events with win $0.50 events, and (4) no-win $4.00 events with no-win $0.50 events. Each set of analyses included three types of tests, (1) combined group activation (N = 30), (2) adolescent vs. adult comparison (i.e., interaction of group by activation), and (3) adolescent group and adult group activation separately. These analyses followed the scheme described below.

Significant ROI activations found in the combined group analysis were submitted to direct group comparisons (adolescents vs. adults) using a small volume correction for a 3 mm sphere around the activation peak (19 voxels). We used a more lenient statistical threshold for group comparisons because of the expected reduced power in between-group comparisons. Authors have used non-corrected P < 0.05 for analyzing activation in small structures based on a priori hypotheses ( Killgore and Yurgelun-Todd, 2004 and Pessoa et al., 2002). We opted for a slightly more conservative approach by setting the threshold at a P < 0.05 corrected for a standard volume of 19 voxels. However, because of controversies in the area of statistical thresholds, we also present results after correction for the whole ROI volume. In addition, peak values of ROI activations in the [adolescent vs. adult] comparisons were extracted for each group and each condition (win $4.00, win $0.50, no-win $4.00, and no-win $0.50) to permit examination of the absolute changes (vs. baseline fixation, e.g., [win $ 4.00 vs. baseline]) for each element of these comparisons, and better understand the nature of these [group × activation] interactions (see Fig. 2, Fig. 3 and Fig. 4). Finally, when a group difference was significant, post-hoc ROI analyses were conducted for each group separately.

Correlation analyses were also performed between ROI activations and ratings of satisfaction and dissatisfaction. Lastly, because of the scarcity of studies of developmental aspects of reward-system function, we report results of exploratory analyses of whole brain activation using a threshold of Puncorrected < 0.001 for main effects and Puncorrected < .005 for interaction (between group comparisons). These findings are only exploratory, and are included for completeness, as hypotheses generating rather than hypotheses testing.

Finally, it is important to underscore the controversies regarding the interpretation of negative changes from baseline of BOLD fMRI signal. Whereas a positive change from baseline of the BOLD fMRI signal is recognized as reflecting increased synaptic activity (activation), a negative change from baseline is less well understood, and may not represent reduced neural activity (deactivation) (Czisch et al., 2004, Harel et al., 2002 and Heeger and Ress, 2002). Proposed interpretations, outside the inhibition or suppression of neuronal firing (Raichle, 1998), include vascular stealing phenomena and reversed baseline state (Czisch et al., 2004). Thus, the caveat regarding the uncertainty of the nature of a negative BOLD signal change needs to be kept in mind.

Results

Sample and task performance

Fourteen adults (6 females/8 males; 26.7 ± 5.0 years old) and sixteen adolescents (9 females/7 males; 13.3 ± 2.1 years old) completed the study. Mean IQ (adults 109.1 ± 12.1; adolescents 107.6 ± 12.4) and mean socioeconomic status (adults 69.4 ± 31.2; adolescents 55.3 ± 17.8) measured using the Hollingshead's Four Factor Index of Social Status (Hollingshead, 1975) were similar between groups.

On the 5-point self-rating scales (feel: 1 ‘neutral,’ 5 ‘very good’ in win outcome, and ‘very bad’ in no-win outcome), both groups were significantly happier to win $4.00 (adults 4.2 ± 0.7; adolescents 4.7 ± 0.3) than $0.50 (adults 3.6 ± 0.9, adolescents 4.1 ± 0.8) (F = 33.53, df = 1,28, P = 0.0001), and they tended to be more dissatisfied when not winning $4.00 (adults 3.3 ± 1.0; adolescents 2.9 ± 1.2) than when not winning $0.50 (adults 3.1 ± 1.0; adolescents 2.7 ± 0.9) (F = 3.99; df = 1,28, P = 0.06). Absolute ratings were also higher in the win outcomes than in the no-win outcomes in both groups (F = 47.6, df = 1,28, P < 0.0001). In addition, adolescents differed from adults in their self-rated feeling level across event types (interaction [group × outcome]: F = 7.4, df = 1,28, P = 0.01). Post-hoc tests showed that this interaction resulted from a greater intensity of positive feelings in adolescents than adults in the win condition (F = 5.1, df = 1,28, P = 0.03). These findings suggest that both groups found the win outcomes more salient than the no-win outcomes, and that adolescents experienced positive outcomes as more pleasurable than did adults. Adolescents did not differ from adults in their experience of negative outcomes.

The cumulative dollar amount won during the task was similar between groups (adolescents: $ 60.3 ± 6.4; adults: $58.9 ± 10.8).

Whole brain analyses

To facilitate hypothesis generation, results of the exploratory whole brain analyses are presented in Table 5 and Table 6. Threshold for statistical significance were set at P < 0.001 uncorrected for multiple comparisons for the main effect analyses, and at P < 0.005 uncorrected for the interaction analyses (group × outcome). These results are for completeness and are not addressed in the discussion. The most notable findings were as followed:

In the comparison of win $4.00 vs. no-win $4.00, adults showed activation in three main clusters, amygdala, visual cortex, and cerebellum ( Fig 2a), and adolescents in two main clusters, ventral striatum and left primary sensory area ( Fig 2c). Adults showed greater activation of inferior frontal cortex than did adolescents, and adolescents showed greater activation of posterior cingulate than did adults.

Focusing on positive feedback, in response to win $4.00 vs. win $0.50, adults engaged two main areas, left frontoparieral cortex and cerebellum, and adolescents two main areas, anterior and posterior cingulate. Cerebellum was activated more strongly in adults than in adolescents.

Finally, in response to negative feedback, greater negative fMRI signal in no-win $4.00 than in no-win $0.50 was found in right hippocampus for adults and in precuneus for adolescents. These fMRI signal changes differed between groups. Finally, adolescents showed greater activation of the anterior cingulate in no-win $4.00 than in no-win $0.50.

Discussion

We examined, in adolescents and in adults, brain regions sensitive to reward receipt/reward omission. Based on the propensity for risk-taking and novelty-seeking in adolescents, we predicted a more active reward-related system (i.e., NAcc), and a less active harm avoidance-related system (i.e., amygdala) during responses to outcomes in adolescents than in adults. Overall findings supported this prediction.

General pattern of activation in adolescents

In general, patterns of neural responses to feedback were consistent with the adult literature, i.e., increased activity (activation) in response to rewards, and reduced BOLD fMRI signal in response to omission of rewards. This was reflected by significant regional activations in the direct comparison of responses to reward receipt and responses to reward omission of the large dollar amount ($4.00) in ventral striatum and amygdala. These significant changes indexed the net difference between activation due to a $4.00 receipt and reduced fMRI signal, possibly indexing reduced neural activity, due to a $4.00 omission. While this finding established the modulation of these regions by the manipulation of outcome valences (positive vs. negative), it begged the question of the specific sensitivity of these regional changes to positive outcomes and negative outcomes separately. This question was addressed in the comparisons of responses to small ($0.50) vs. large gains ($4.00).

With respect to reward receipt, the ventral striatum, particularly NAcc, and the medial temporal cortex, including amygdala, have been shown to be activated by positive feedback (i.e., rewards) in a number of adult paradigms (Breiter et al., 2001, Delgado et al., 2000, Elliott et al., 2003, Knutson et al., 2001 and O'Doherty et al., 2001). We found activation to be significant in the amygdala and at a trend level in the NAcc for the combined sample in response to the receipt of large vs. small gains. These activations were relatively weak, and did not reach significance in each group separately. We propose two possible explanations for this finding.

The first interpretation is based on the temporal difference model proposed by Schultz (2002) and Schultz et al. (1997). This model suggests that dopamine, a prominent neuromodulator of NAcc function, signals the difference between the expected value and the actual value of an outcome. The task was designed to favor expectation of gains, since losses were not included. In this way, subjects expected mainly to win, and our paradigm may have minimized the difference between expected and actual values of outcome, and weakened our ability to detect activation of the NAcc.

The second possibility concerns the valence of the reward magnitudes. The difference between the reward value of $4.00 and $0.50 might have been too small to generate a reliable signal difference, i.e., that exceeds the variability in activation of both groups. However, $0.50 was clearly a too small amount to produce significant neuronal activations in the [win $0.50 vs. no-win $0.50] comparison, in contrast to the $4.00 amount which generated strong activations. A task that includes losses and larger dollar amounts might be associated with greater recruitment of ventral striatal regions during gain receipts.

With respect to negative outcomes, reduced BOLD signal in these regions has been reported in adults when potential rewards were omitted or when losses were incurred (Breiter et al., 2001, Delgado et al., 2000, Knutson et al., 2001, McClure et al., 2003 and O'Doherty et al., 2003). We found similar findings in both NAcc and amygdala in the combined group. These signal reductions reached significance in the NAcc in adolescents, and in the amygdala in adults (Table 1 and Table 2). These negative fMRI signal changes may reflect reduced neural activity, or perhaps more specifically reduced dopaminergic activity, based on the temporal difference model of reduction of dopamine neuronal activity in response to the omission of reward that was expected or a reward smaller than expected (Schultz, 2002 and Schultz et al., 1997). Thus, neural responses of the NAcc and amygdala to feedback were in the same direction in adolescents as those reported in adults.

Adolescents compared to adults

In this study, we tested specifically the hypothesis of quantitative differences in the engagement of these subcortical structures between adults and adolescents. This hypothesis was proposed as a possible mechanism underlying the well-recognized peak of risk-taking behavior during adolescence. Risk-taking is a manifestation of both enhanced approach behavior and reduced harm avoidance behavior. The role of the NAcc is to ultimately permit (motivate) animals to choose (approach) the most advantageous course of action (Cardinal et al., 2002a). The amygdala codes the affective values of stimuli and dispatches this information to different systems, including NAcc, but more specifically to brainstem arousal and response systems, which warn animals to avoid or move away from negative situations (Cardinal et al., 2002a and LeDoux, 2000). Thus, NAcc and amygdala use representations of the utility or affective value of reinforcers to inform behavioral responses, towards and away from stimuli respectively. These representations originate from the processing of responses to outcomes. Therefore, changes in the processing of outcomes can directly affect approach/avoidance behavior, and contribute to risk-taking in adolescents. Based on this formulation, adolescents were expected to show a more responsive NAcc and a less responsive amygdala than were adults during the presentation of feedback.

At present, only one fMRI study directly compared reward circuitry in adolescents and adults (Bjork et al., 2004). Bjork et al. (2004) examined anticipation and response to outcomes in a monetary reaction time task. Subjects had to hit a target within a specified time window to win or to avoid losing money. The authors found that regional activations in the [win vs. no-win] feedback comparison did not differ between adults and adolescents. However, during the preceding phase of anticipation to action, i.e., between the presentation of a cue informing the type of trial (gain, loss, or neutral) and the presentation of the target, adolescents showed weaker activation of the NAcc than adults in the comparison of [possible gain vs. no incentive] anticipation. These findings were interpreted as a weaker reward system in adolescents than in adults.

In our study, the [win vs. no-win] contrast revealed stronger activation of ventral striatum in adolescents than in adults. Our results are in contrast to Bjork et al.'s findings of no age-related differences during feedback. Although both paradigms recorded neural activity during feedback, the nature of the paradigms was substantially different with regards to cognitive processes (decision-making vs. reaction time task), which makes the direct comparisons of these studies difficult.

To examine the relative contribution of processing positive feedback (win) vs. processing negative feedback (no-win), we examined separately positive [win $4.00 vs., win $0.50] outcome and negative outcome [no-win $0.50 vs. no-win $4.00]. We found no group difference during reward receipt [win $4.00 vs. win $0.50], except for a trend of the right amygdala to be more active in adults than in adolescents (P < 0.06). This suggested that the significant age-related differences in regional activations found in the [win vs. no-win] comparison were explained by the responses to negative feedback more than by the responses to positive feedback. Indeed, during reward omission (no-win $0.50 vs. no-win $4.00), the amygdala was significantly more affected in adults than in adolescents, and the NAcc tended to be more affected in adolescents than in adults (P < 0.06). The heightened sensitivity of negative feedback to age differences may be a reflection of our paradigm, which did not optimize the salience (magnitude of the $ amount, and inclusion of real losses) of the rewards, yielding weaker responses to positive feedback. However, taken together, these findings, in line with our predictions, support less amygdala and more NAcc activity changes in adolescents than in adults during processes of outcomes.

One other study recently examined reward circuitry in adolescents, but no adults were included (May et al., 2004). May et al. (2004) used an event-related fMRI paradigm to examine neural responses to monetary outcomes (gain, loss, and neutral) in a simple card-guessing task. Changes of BOLD signal in left ventral striatum, left medial orbitofrontal cortex, and anterior cingulate were found to be more positive in rewarded trials than in neutral or punished trials. These findings concurred with our results of ventral striatum being modulated by outcomes in adolescents. Of note, May et al. (2004) did not report involvement of the medial temporal cortex in adolescents, in contrast to their previous work in adults using the same paradigm (Delgado et al., 2000). This observation follows the same trend as our work, i.e., lesser involvement of medial temporal structures during feedback in adolescents than in adults.

Although we designed this study to address possible ontogenic changes in the NAcc's and amygdala's responses to outcomes, a number of other structures are involved in this process, particularly at the cortical level. The amygdala and NAcc are involved in extensive distributed neural networks that are partly overlapping and fulfill a number of specific functions, such as associative learning and reward processes. In particular, the medial prefrontal cortex emerges as a possibly significant region in the modulation of subcortical activity in responses to outcomes (Knutson et al., 2000, Knutson et al., 2001, Knutson et al., 2003, O'Doherty et al., 2001 and Phillips et al., 2003). Both amygdala and NAcc receive substantial afferents from the medial prefrontal cortex, which is also a major dopaminergic projection area. The anterior cingulate and the orbitofrontal cortex have been consistently implicated in processes related to responses to outcomes and decision-making (Bechara, 2004, Bush et al., 2002, Cardinal et al., 2002a, Cardinal et al., 2002b, Dalley et al., 2004, Ernst et al., 2002, Ernst et al., 2004, Holroyd et al., 2003, Rogers et al., 2004 and Rolls, 2004). Distinct functions have been proposed for these latter regions, such as conflict and error monitoring in the case of the anterior cingulate (Carter et al., 1998), and processing the emotional valence of stimuli in the case of the orbitofrontal cortex (Rolls, 2004) At present, a number of theoretical models have attempted to integrate anatomical, particularly connectivity, and functional findings into cohesive frameworks (Blair, 2004, Damasio, 1996 and LeDoux, 2000). The present study represents a first step toward understanding how brain maturation shapes functional networks to achieve optimal efficiency in adulthood. Such understanding is critical for identifying vulnerability factors of behavioral disorders. The contribution of other structures to this process will need to be examined in future work.

Overall, findings of this study need to be considered in view of the following caveats. First, the spatial resolution of our image analysis exceeded the sizes of the structure under scrutiny. We cannot ignore the possibility that the regional activations could originate from nearby structures. However, we have used this method in previous studies showing amygdala activation in response to the viewing of facial expressions, highly consistent with the literature (Monk et al., 2003). In addition, the whole brain maps show clearly circumscribed activation clusters around our areas of interest (Figs. 2a,c). Finally, we opted for acquiring whole brain coverage to maximize spatial normalization process, and optimize its reliability, as well as to permit to assess the regional specificity of our findings. Second, ratings of satisfaction at winning were higher in adolescents than in adults, suggesting that they found this feedback more pleasurable. Adolescents could have been more sensitive to the dollar amount than adults because they have less “pocket money”. We ran post-hoc analyses of variance of peak ROIs values using affective ratings as covariates (not shown), and found no changes in the results. In addition, if the dollar value were higher for adolescents than for adults, adolescents would also have felt more distressed at not winning, which was not the case. Third, the age range of the adolescent group spanned early and late adolescence. Future studies should examine these two phases of adolescence separately. Finally, we did not include in our model the developmental changes in prefrontal cortex. The present study was deliberately focused on the two main structures with known functional specificity in the processes of approach and avoidance behavior. It will be important in future work to understand the potential role of the prefrontal cortex in the proposed model.

In conclusion, our findings suggest that, in response to outcomes, adolescents showed weaker involvement of the amygdala, and a greater contribution of the NAcc compared to adults. This age-related pattern of regional brain activation may explain the propensity for risk-taking and novelty-seeking behaviors in adolescents. In addition, perturbations in the developmental changes of the function of these structures, either in their synchronicity or magnitude, may underlie onset of psychopathology, such as disorders of mood or motivation, for which the prime time of onset is adolescence (Chambers et al., 2003 and Pine et al., 1998).

Future developmental studies are warranted to examine the different stages of a motivated action, including for example decision-making, execution, and anticipation, and the factors that could influence the pattern of activation and maturational trajectories of reward-related circuits. These factors include individual characteristics such as gender, pubertal stage in adolescents, and personality traits characterizing sensation-seeking, harm avoidance, and reward sensitivity. The present study did not provide sufficient statistical power to examine gender or pubertal effects. Such information would be tremendously useful to help better understand how the maturational changes of the various processes that contribute to decision-making are orchestrated in normal development.
