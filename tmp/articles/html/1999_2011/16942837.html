Fear-related activity in the prefrontal cortex increases with age during adolescence: A preliminary fMRI study

  • Deborah A. Yurgelun-Todd, ,
  • William D.S. Killgore
  • Cognitive Neuroimaging Laboratory, Brain Imaging Center, McLean Hospital, Harvard Medical School, Belmont, MA 02478, USA
Received 17 March 2006, Revised 7 July 2006, Accepted 18 July 2006, Available online 30 August 2006

Abstract

An emerging theory of adolescent development suggests that brain maturation involves a progressive “frontalization” of function whereby the prefrontal cortex gradually assumes primary responsibility for many of the cognitive processes initially performed by more primitive subcortical and limbic structures. To test the hypothesis of developmental frontalization in emotional processing, we analyzed the correlation between age and prefrontal cortex activity in a sample of 16 healthy adolescents (nine boys; seven girls), ranging in age from 8 to 15 years, as they viewed images of fearful and happy faces while undergoing functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI). During fear perception, age was significantly positively correlated with greater functional activity within the prefrontal cortex, whereas no significant relationship was evident between age and activity in the amygdala. Consistent with previous gender-related findings, age was significantly correlated with bilateral prefrontal activity for the sample of females, but was only significantly related to right prefrontal activity for the males. In contrast, similar age-related correlations were not evident during the perception of happy faces. These results suggest that the maturation of threat-related emotional processing during adolescence is related to the progressive acquisition of greater functional activity within the prefrontal cortex. The hypothesis of age related decreases in amygdala activity was not supported, but may have been due to low signal-to-noise and inadequate power in the present sample to resolve subtle changes in this small structure.

Within the human brain, the prefrontal cortex mediates the highest cognitive capacities, including reasoning, planning, and behavioral control [13]. This relatively large and complex associative brain region continues to develop along with other higher order association regions as children mature from adolescence into adulthood [13], [15] and [37]. Structural neuroimaging studies using growth mapping techniques suggest that the prefrontal cortex matures more slowly than other regions of the brain [5] and [15] and that its development parallels the improvements in cognitive control and behavioral inhibition that emerge during the adolescent transition into adulthood [5] and [39]. As children grow and acquire greater capacities to think abstractly, evaluate potential courses of action, and inhibit inappropriate or non-advantageous behavior, there appears to be progressive change in the organization of brain activity. Recent evidence suggests that this re-organization follows a systematic trajectory of “frontalization,” whereby the prefrontal cortex assumes progressively greater control over functions previously mediated by phylogenetically more primitive subcortical structures and the limbic system [36]. The period of adolescence is a time of considerable physiological change, as sex specific pubertal hormones bring about changes in physical stature, reproductive organs, and other secondary sexual characteristics [38]. Neuroendocrine changes during puberty also appear to be associated with changes in brain organization and cognitive function [17] as well as cerebral metabolism [11]. Furthermore, with increasing age, prefrontal activity becomes progressively more focal and specialized while irrelevant and diffuse activity in this region is reduced [3], [8] and [40]. Thus, adolescent development appears to involve significant functional re-organization of frontal and limbic systems.

During adolescence, social relationships take on particular importance as the adolescent child becomes adept at reading social and emotional cues, and modulating his own affective responses [20]. To date, very little research has examined the neurobiological basis of these developmental changes in affective processing during adolescence. Younger children have difficulty managing interference from competing distractions and the level of difficulty appears to be correlated with the immaturity of posterior and frontal association cortices [8]. As children mature, they show progressively greater capacity to selectively attend to incoming information and control their behavior in a goal directed manner [1], [5], [29] and [40]. This development appears to emerge in conjunction with a progressive frontalization of functional activity associated with inhibitory processing [36]. Frontalization of inhibitory capacity could presumably permit greater top down modulation of activity within more primitive subcortical and limbic emotion processing regions, such as the amygdala. Hariri et al. [18] and [19] have demonstrated that increased functional activity within the prefrontal cortex is associated with significant modulation of amygdala responses to affective stimuli, particularly with regard to fearful and angry faces. It is possible that these processes emerge developmentally. Some functional neuroimaging evidence suggests that adults show greater functional activity in the orbitofrontal cortex than adolescents when directing attention in a goal oriented manner toward emotional stimuli [32]. In a previous study, we examined whether older children modulated amygdala responses to fearful stimuli via greater activation within the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex. The findings indicated a significant age-related increase in left dorsolateral prefrontal activation relative to left amygdala activity in adolescent girls during the perception of facial expressions of fear, but this difference was negligible in adolescent boys [23]. That study included extremely limited search regions and data were not collected across the entire brain. Therefore, in the present study, utilizing a different sample of adolescent children and more sophisticated data analytic techniques, we more extensively examined the relationship between age and prefrontal/amygdala activity in response to facial expressions of fear. We hypothesized that during the perception of facial expressions of fear, brain activity within inferior-lateral and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex would be positively correlated with age, while activity within the amygdala would correlate negatively with age. A happy face condition was also included for comparison condition.

Sixteen healthy children (nine boys; seven girls), ranging in age from 8 to 15 years (M = 11.6, S.D. = 2.1) underwent fMRI while completing fear and happy face perception tasks. The participants were healthy and free of medical, neurological, or psychiatric problems as determined by a structured clinical interview (i.e., the Kiddie–Schedule for Affective Disorders and Schizophrenia) administered by a trained psychometric technician. All parents provided written informed consent for their children whereas verbal and written assent was obtained from all minor participants prior to involvement in the study. Previously, clinically relevant affective symptoms from this sample were correlated with brain activity during fear perception. In one report [27], we showed that higher scores on a depression inventory were positively correlated with activity in the ventromedial prefrontal cortex, and in the other report [26], we showed that amygdala activity was significantly correlated with dimensions of social anxiety but not with other aspects of anxiety in adolescents. Here we present new, previously unreported data from this sample specifically addressing the age-related changes in functional activity during fear perception.

As described in greater detail elsewhere [26], the children were scanned while engaged in two passive visual affective tasks, each involving static presentation of six photographs of actors expressing either fear or happiness. The two tasks were identical in design, except for the specific affect shown on the face, and order was counterbalanced across participants. The stimuli included photographs of males and females from the standard set of Ekman and Friesen [10]. In a blocked paradigm, faces were presented sequentially (9.5 s each; 0.5 s inter-stimulus interval) during two 30-s stimulus periods that alternated with three 30-s fixation rest periods (black screen with a small white circle as a fixation point). All participants received the same sequence of stimuli within each task. Stimuli were back-projected onto a screen at the end of the scanning table and viewed from a mirror mounted on the head coil. Participants were asked to keep their eyes fixed on the center of the faces and were informed that they would be quizzed about the emotion depicted on the faces at the completion of the scan.

For each 150 s scan, 50 echoplanar images (10 per alternating block) were collected on a GE LX 1.5 Tesla MRI scanner equipped with a quadrature RF head coil (TR = 3 s, TE = 40 ms, flip angle = 90°). Twenty coronal slices were collected (7, 1 mm gap), with a 20 cm field of view and a 64 × 64 acquisition matrix. In-plane resolution was 3.125 mm × 7 mm × 3.125 mm.

Data were preprocessed and analyzed in SPM99 [12]. Images were motion corrected, normalized, realigned, spatially smoothed using an isotropic Gaussian kernel (full width half maximum [FWHM] = 10 mm), and resliced to 2 mm × 2 mm × 2 mm using sinc interpolation. Head motion exceeding 2 mm in any direction was used as an exclusion criterion. No participants were excluded on this basis.

Data were analyzed using a two-step random-effects approach [34]. Initially, contrast images were generated using the general linear model within SPM99, employing a box-car waveform (i.e., five 30 s alternating conditions) with a six-second time-shifted delay convolved with the hemodynamic response function as the reference paradigm. This approach yielded SPM contrast maps reflecting the activation associated with viewing the affective faces versus the fixation control baseline. These contrast images were then entered into a random effects correlation analysis in SPM99 [34] that highlighted the voxels showing a significant correlation between the age of the children and the intensity (i.e., height) of task-related BOLD activity. In accordance with our a priori hypotheses, we restricted the analysis to the prefrontal cortex and amygdala. Prefrontal regions included the inferior, middle, superior, and medial orbitofrontal gyri, gyrus rectus, superior medial frontal gyrus, inferior, middle, and superior frontal gyrus (see Fig. 1). The prefrontal ROI comprised a total of 45,283 voxels. The left amygdala ROI was 220 voxels and right amygdala search region was 248 voxels. Regions of interest were constructed from the published anatomical atlas of Tzourio-Mazoyer et al. [42] as implemented in the Wake Forest Pickatlas Utility [30]. Because we focused our hypothesis on specific regions, hypothesized a priori, the statistical threshold within the ROIs was set at p < .005, uncorrected, with a spatial extent of (k) = 10 contiguous voxels. These thresholds have been used extensively in previous work using similar pre-defined hypothesized ROIs and were retained to permit direct comparison across studies while maintaining a reasonable balance between Type I and II error for hypothesized ROIs [24], [26] and [27]. According to the proposed hypotheses age-related positive correlations were examined within the prefrontal cortex and negative correlations within the amygdala. Activation images were superimposed on an average template brain normalized to the standardized coordinate space of the Montreal Neurological Institute (MNI) for visualization.

As hypothesized, we found significant positive correlations between the age of the adolescent children and the intensity of BOLD prefrontal cortex responses to facial expressions of fear (see Table 1 and Fig. 1). Activity within the left (Pearson r = .74, p = .001, two-tailed) and right (r = .82, p < .0001) prefrontal cortex increased significantly with greater age of the adolescent children. In contrast, age was not significantly correlated with activity in either the right or left amygdala during fear perception. We also examined the correlations for each gender separately at the same local maxima identified within the total sample analysis. For the males, age was not significantly correlated with the intensity of BOLD activity in the left prefrontal cortex (r = .51, p = .16), but was correlated with activity in the right (r = .80, p = .009). For females, age showed even stronger correlations with prefrontal activity in both the left (r = .89, p = .007) and right (r = .90, p = .006) prefrontal ROIs. Correlations were compared between genders using Fisher's r-to-z transformations, with no significant differences emerging between males and females for age correlations in the left (z = −1.36, p = .17) or right (z = −0.55, p = .58) prefrontal cortex. There was no significant correlation between age and activity within the left or right amygdala for either gender.

For happy faces, age was not positively correlated with activity in the prefrontal cortex or negatively correlated with activity in either left or right amygdala (see Table 1).

The findings from the present study were mixed with regard to our conceptual hypothesis, as we found a positive linear relationship between age and prefrontal cortex activity during fearful face processing, but did not find the expected negative relationship between age and amygdalar activity. The results indicated that, with increasing age, processing of fear-related face stimuli was associated with progressively greater brain activity bilaterally within the inferior, middle, superior, and superior medial frontal gyri. These data are consistent with the general hypothesis that adolescent development is associated with a progressive “frontalization” of many functions that are initially mediated by other regions, particularly those involving subcortical structures [36], but do not support the notion that limbic activity is concomitantly reduced with greater adolescent age. Previous research has suggested that cognitive development through the adolescent years is associated with progressively greater efficiency of executive control capacities with this efficiency paralleled by increased activity within focal prefrontal regions [36] and [40]. Alternatively, the task may be perceived differently by younger and older children, whereby the former recruit additional prefrontal regions for reasons unexplored in the present study. Further, the interpretation of fMRI studies is heavily dependent on the control task used. Age-related reductions in baseline responses during the control task (i.e., fixation) could produce the appearance of activation increases with age. Neuroimaging studies using positron emission tomography (PET) suggest that, even at rest, there is a positive correlation between age and prefrontal glucose metabolism from early childhood through early adolescence [7] and [22]. Future studies should address the possibility of altered baseline responses with age.

Structural volumetric neuroimaging studies suggest that, while total brain volume is relatively established by the early school age years [35], significant remodeling of gray and white matter occurs throughout adolescence and into early adulthood [9]. Moreover, recent structural volumetric studies report that the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex does not reach its full volume until the early twenties [14]. Many complex cognitive processes continue to develop even into the early adult years [21] and it is, therefore, unlikely that fear processing is completely mature by adolescence. The possibility of ongoing development beyond early adolescence was supported by a recent study of brain activity during an emotional processing task that found significantly greater activity within the orbitofrontal cortex of adult participants relative to adolescents [32]. Together, the emerging findings suggest that the development of prefrontal modulation over emotional processing continues to develop throughout the adolescent years and into early adulthood.

Contrary to our hypothesis we did not find a negative correlation between age and activity in the left amygdala [23], as none of the voxels in amygdala in the current study showed significant linear associations with age. Amygdala activation is a fairly consistent finding across age groups for tasks of fear and face perception that are similar to those used in the present study [25], [33] and [41]. It is possible that the failure to replicate the age-related amygdala results in the present study may reflect methodological limitations, including difficulties associated with magnetic field inhomogeneity and susceptibility artifacts near the air-filled sinuses [6] and [28], a relatively small region of interest, limited sample size, or habituation to an affective task presented in a block paradigm [2].

The present results are consistent with our previous study data indicating that age was significantly related to prefrontal development for females, but not for males [23]. In the current investigation, age was highly correlated with prefrontal activity in both the left and right hemispheres for females, but the association only reached significance for the right hemisphere in the sample of males. Given the small sample size in the present study, statistical power was severely limited when the groups were split by gender. Thus, our gender findings may reflect low power to detect sex differences, or they may reflect the earlier maturation of girls and the generally greater capacity of females to interpret, regulate, and contextualize emotions [16], [20] and [31]. A recent review of the neuroscience literature has documented the rapidly growing evidence supporting sex-related differences in neuroanatomy, neurochemistry, and neurocognitive functioning [4]. The present data underscore the importance of considering sex differences when studying adolescent brain development.

The present study was limited by several factors. First, we utilized a passive perception task that simply required the participants to view static photographs. No behavioral data were collected during scanning. Consequently, we cannot equate participant's variables that may be of importance, such as level of attention or subjective emotional responses. Future studies would benefit from the collection of behavioral data during emotional perception. Second, while the present data clearly suggest a linear relationship between age and prefrontal responsiveness to fearful faces, the age range was restricted to late childhood through the middle years of adolescence. It is quite possible that these relationships would not remain linear if a broader developmental age range was examined. Further research that includes a more comprehensive age spectrum from the early school age years through adulthood will be necessary to effectively map the developmental trajectory of prefrontal activity. It is also important to keep in mind that the present study focused its hypotheses on developmental changes in prefrontal activity in response to fearful stimuli. The emotion of fear and processing of fear-related stimuli have been the most extensively studied of the various affective processes, but it will also be important to determine the extent to which age-related changes in prefrontal activity occur with other types of emotional stimuli. Our preliminary data suggest that response to happy faces is not associated with a strong age-related frontal activation pattern.

During processing of fear-related stimuli, there was a positive linear relationship between age and greater functional activity within the prefrontal cortex in a group of adolescents, whereas no significant relationship was evident between age and activity within the amygdala. These preliminary results suggest that the maturation of emotional processing during adolescence is primarily related to the progressive acquisition of greater functional activity within the prefrontal cortex. In contrast, no evidence of systematic age-related change in functional activity of the amygdala was observed across this same developmental period. Future research may build upon these preliminary findings by exploring the possibility of changes in functional connectivity between prefrontal and limbic regions with adolescent development. Furthermore, additional studies are needed to determine whether these relationships are related to measurable changes in emotional behavior.
