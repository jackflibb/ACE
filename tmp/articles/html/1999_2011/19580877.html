Sex-dependent age modulation of frontostriatal and temporo-parietal activation during cognitive control

  • Anastasia Christakoua,
  • Rozmin Halaria,
  • Anna B. Smitha,
  • Eve Ifkovitsa,
  • Mick Brammerb,
  • Katya Rubiaa, ,
  • a Department of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, Institute of Psychiatry, King's College London, Box P046, De Crespigny Park SE5 8AF, UK
  • b Department of Biostatistics and Computing, Institute of Psychiatry, King's College London, UK
Received 29 August 2008, Revised 14 January 2009, Accepted 29 June 2009, Available online 4 July 2009

Abstract

Developmental functional imaging studies of cognitive control show progressive age-related increase in task-relevant fronto-striatal activation in male development from childhood to adulthood. Little is known, however, about how gender affects this functional development. In this study, we used event related functional magnetic resonance imaging to examine effects of sex, age, and their interaction on brain activation during attentional switching and interference inhibition, in 63 male and female adolescents and adults, aged 13 to 38. Linear age correlations were observed across all subjects in task-specific frontal, striatal and temporo-parietal activation. Gender analysis revealed increased activation in females relative to males in fronto-striatal areas during the Switch task, and laterality effects in the Simon task, with females showing increased left inferior prefrontal and temporal activation, and males showing increased right inferior prefrontal and parietal activation. Increased prefrontal activation clusters in females and increased parietal activation clusters in males furthermore overlapped with clusters that were age-correlated across the whole group, potentially reflecting more mature prefrontal brain activation patterns for females, and more mature parietal activation patterns for males. Gender by age interactions further supported this dissociation, revealing exclusive female-specific age correlations in inferior and medial prefrontal brain regions during both tasks, and exclusive male-specific age correlations in superior parietal (Switch task) and temporal regions (Simon task). These findings show increased recruitment of age-correlated prefrontal activation in females, and of age-correlated parietal activation in males, during tasks of cognitive control. Gender differences in frontal and parietal recruitment may thus be related to gender differences in the neurofunctional maturation of these brain regions.

Introduction

Mature, goal-directed and adaptive behaviour necessitates the deployment of effective cognitive control, including the ability to shift away from a previously formed behavioural set with changing environmental contingencies, and to selectively attend information, while suppressing interference from distracting, task-irrelevant stimuli or events. Such processes of cognitive control mature late in adolescence (Levin et al., 1991), and have been associated with activation increases in task-relevant brain regions (Bunge et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2000 and Rubia et al., 2007). Specifically, functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) studies of cognitive flexibility and interference inhibition describe linear increases with age in fronto-striatal and temporo-parietal activation well into the third and fourth decade of life (Adleman et al., 2002, Crone et al., 2006, Marsh et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2006 and Sowell et al., 2003). This progressive functional maturation parallels the late structural maturation of these systems during adolescence and early adulthood, through processes of synaptic remodelling, activity dependent myelination and programmed cell death, affecting both grey and white matter organisation (de Graaf-Peters and Hadders-Algra, 2006 and Liston et al., 2006). Structural imaging studies show a linear increase with age in white matter that peaks at around age 50 (Sowell et al., 2003), and a decrease in grey matter density up to age 40; together these findings are thought to reflect progressive myelination well into the 40s (Sowell et al., 2003 and Sowell et al., 2007). Hardly anything is known, however, on how gender affects this linear age correlation of task-relevant brain activation during tasks of cognitive control.

In neuropsychological studies, there is evidence for gender differences in cognitive development. There is evidence for gender differences in early cognition (Bjorklund and Pellegrini, 2000), however sex differences in higher-cognitive, executive functions appear to become more pronounced with puberty, during adolescence, concomitant with hormonal changes (Sisk and Zehr, 2005): girls appear to become more efficient than boys in tasks of selective attention, verbal fluency and conductive reasoning (Anderson, 2001, Anderson et al., 2001, Klenberg et al., 2001 and Schaie, 1994), while males outperform females in executive functions that rely on visual–spatial processing, in particular mental rotation (De Luca et al., 2003). The evidence for superior performance of females in some tasks of selective attention such as the Colour-Word Stroop task, however, is controversial, with both positive and negative findings (Laeng et al., 2005, MacLeod, 1991 and Van der Elst et al., 2006). There is furthermore evidence for domain-dependent sex differences in cognitive functions, for example in cognitive flexibility, with females showing greater flexibility in the verbal domain (Weiss et al., 2006) and males showing greater flexibility in the visual–spatial perceptual domain such as mental rotation (Weiss et al., 2003).

At the level of brain structure, morphological imaging studies have shown gender differences in brain development, with males exhibiting steeper developmental slopes in grey matter reduction and white matter increase than females during adolescence (De Bellis et al., 2001, Giedd et al., 1999 and Giedd et al., 2006). This is thought to be partly explained by earlier maturation peaks in females in frontal, striatal, temporal and parietal areas (Giedd et al., 1999 and Giedd et al., 2006).

Only relatively recently have fMRI studies investigated sex differences in the neurofunctional substrates of executive functions. These by and large show sex differences in task-relevant brain function. For example, during tasks of working memory and mental rotation, findings seem to converge in that females show increased prefrontal, and males increased parietal activation (Bell et al., 2006, Goldstein et al., 2005, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003). During tasks of motor response inhibition, however, imaging findings have been more inconsistent, showing either increased activation in males in cingulate and striato-thalamic regions (Li et al., 2006), increased activation in females in frontal, parietal, temporal, and striato-thalamic regions (Garavan et al., 2006), or no gender differences (Halari et al., 2005). No gender differences in activation were observed during a simple reversal task (Crone et al., 2006).

No modern developmental functional neuroimaging study, however, has investigated sex differences or sex by age interactions on the neurofunctional substrates of higher level tasks of cognitive control, such as the inhibition of irrelevant stimulus–response associations in visual–spatial switching tasks and interference inhibition in the Simon task. Performance on these tasks develops relatively late in adolescence (Davidson et al., 2006 and Levin et al., 1991), presumably after the impact of sex hormones has differentiated neurofunctional brain networks (Neufang et al., 2008, Ostatnikova et al., 2007, Romeo, 2003 and Sisk and Zehr, 2005).

To our knowledge, there are only two developmental imaging studies that have investigated sex by age interactions on brain activation during tasks of cognitive inhibition. Crone et al. have shown no sex by age interaction effects during a reversal task (Crone et al., 2006). Marsh et al. have shown exclusively female contributions to age-related increases in lenticular nucleus activation during the Colour-Word Stroop task, with males showing more pronounced progressive deactivations with age in the anterior cingulate cortex (Marsh et al., 2006).

The aim of this study was therefore to investigate the effects of gender on brain activation and, in particular, on developmental changes therein, from adolescence to mid-adulthood, during two tasks of cognitive control. We used fMRI in 63 male and female participants during performance of a visual–spatial Switch task, measuring cognitive flexibility, and a Simon task, measuring selective attention and interference inhibition. These two tasks were chosen because they measure fundamental processes of cognitive control, important for mature goal-directed behaviour. The Simon task measures the ability to selectively attend relevant information and inhibit irrelevant and distracting information, while the Switch task measures cognitive flexibility. Both processes form part of higher level executive functions that are known to develop late in adolescence (Davidson et al., 2006 and Levin et al., 1991), and are mediated by late developing discrete fronto-striato-parietal neural networks (Rubia et al., 2006). In closely related functions, there is evidence for sex differences in performance (Laeng et al., 2005, MacLeod, 1991, Van der Elst et al., 2006 and Weiss et al., 2003), as well as brain function (Bell et al., 2006, Garavan et al., 2006, Goldstein et al., 2005, Li et al., 2006, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003). Their late neurofunctional development makes these processes particularly sensitive to developmental gender differences (Giedd et al., 1997, Sowell et al., 2003 and Sowell et al., 2007).

During male development between childhood and adulthood, predominantly right inferior prefrontal, caudate and temporo-parietal activation has been shown to increase linearly with age during the Switch task, and predominantly left inferior prefrontal, putamen and temporo-parietal activation during the Simon task (Rubia et al., 2006). Based on evidence for increased activation in females in prefrontal, and in males in parietal brain areas during perceptual flexibility in mental rotation tasks (Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003), and during motor response inhibition (Garavan et al., 2006), as well as evidence for an impact of gender on the relationship of striatal activity with age (Marsh et al., 2006), we hypothesised that gender would affect prefrontal, striatal and parietal brain activation, as well as the age modulation of these regions. Two different tasks of cognitive control were used in order to elucidate task-specific and task-independent effects.

Methods

Participants

Sixty three right-handed healthy adolescents and adults in the age range of 13 to 38 (mean age in years (SD) = 21.9 (6)) were recruited through advertisement (38 males (mean age = 15.7 (1) and 25 females (mean age (SD) = 16 (1)). All participants scored above the 10th percentile (IQ estimate over 80) on the Raven's Standard Progressive Matrices test of performance intelligence quotient (IQ) (Raven, 1960). (Mean Performance IQ estimate (SD) = 106.2 (11.5); Male IQ estimate = 105.4 (11); female IQ estimate = 107.7 (13)). Exclusion criteria were current or past substance abuse, head injury, mental retardation, or mental, endocrine or neurological disorder. There were no differences between males and females in age (t = − 0.9; df =34; p = n.s.) or converted performance IQ estimates across the whole group (t = − 0.6; df = 34, p = n.s.), or within adolescent and adult subgroups of participants (p > 0.05 in all cases). The study was approved by the local Ethics Committee and all participants gave informed consent. They all received £30 for their participation.

Paradigms

Task administration

The tasks were presented using a video projector viewed through a mirror within the MRI scanner. Behavioural response data were recorded through a key pad onto a time-locked automated PC system. All subjects were trained once on the tasks. A randomised presentation, rapid event related fMRI design was used with jittered inter-trial intervals (ITI) and random events to optimise statistical efficiency (Dale, 1999).

Switch task

An fMRI adaptation of the Meiran switch task (Meiran, 1996) was used and has been described in detail previously (Rubia et al., 2006, Smith et al., 2004 and Smith et al., 2006). Subjects are presented with a grid divided into four squares, with a double-headed arrow in the middle for 1600 ms. A red dot appears in one of the four quadrants for 1400 ms (mean inter-trial interval (ITI): 2.4 s). If the central arrow is horizontal, the subject has to indicate whether the target is on the left or right side of the grid (left or right button); if the central arrow is vertical, subjects have to indicate whether the target is in the lower or upper half of the grid (up or down button). During “switch” trials (32, 21%) the central arrow changed position; switch trials occur pseudorandomly after every 4–6 “repeat” trials, during which the central arrow remains in the same plane (120, 79%); there is a total of 152 trials and the task lasts 6 min. Subjects record their response by pressing the appropriate button on a keypad with four buttons arranged in a “diamond” configuration (Fig. 1A).

Simon task (motor Stroop)

We used an fMRI adaptation of the Simon task, also called the directional or motor Stroop task, which has been described in detail elsewhere (Rubia et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2009 and Smith et al., 2006). The task involves a stimulus–response incompatibility effect, and measures selective attention and inhibition of interference.

The task consists of 208 trials, including 24 pseudorandomly presented incongruent (“Simon”) trials. Congruent stimuli consist of arrows either on the left of the screen pointing left, or the right of the screen pointing right, appearing for 600 ms. The subject is told to press the left or right button, respectively. The incongruent (“Simon”) stimuli consist of arrows pointing left but appearing on the right side of the screen, or vice versa. The distribution of the incongruent trials is predetermined, such that they are interspersed among the congruent trials at least 3 repetition times (or 3 ITIs) apart, for adequate separation of the haemodynamic response. The subject is told to make a button response according to the direction of the arrow (iconic information), and to inhibit the tendency to respond according to the side of the screen upon which it appears (spatial information, Fig. 1B). The tendency to respond to the spatial information is predominant over the iconic information and needs to be inhibited during incongruent trials, resulting in increased response latencies and errors; this has been documented as the spatial incompatibility or “Simon” effect (Simon and Berbaum, 1988).

Behavioural variables are mean reaction times (MRT) and number of errors for the congruent and incongruent conditions, and the magnitude of the Simon effect on speed (MRTINCONGRUENT–MRTCONGRUENT) and accuracy (errorsINCONGRUENT–errorsCONGRUENT). Event-related brain activation analysis for the Simon task contrasts activation associated with incongruent trials with that of congruent trials (incongruent > congruent).

Performance data analysis

Sex differences on the dependent variables were analysed using multivariate analysis of covariance (MANCOVA) with sex as factor and age as covariate. Linear effects of age on performance variables were tested using two-tailed Pearson correlation analyses.

Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI)

Image acquisition

Gradient echo-planar magnetic resonance (MR) imaging data were acquired on a GE Signa 1.5 Tesla Horizon LX system (General Electric, Milwaukee, WI, USA) at the Maudsley Hospital, London, using a semi-automated image quality control procedure. A quadrature birdcage headcoil was used for RF transmission and reception. In each of 16 non-contiguous planes parallel to the anterior–posterior commissure, 152 (Switch task) or 208 (Simon task) T2âŽ-weighted MR images depicting BOLD (Blood Oxygen Level Dependent) contrast covering the whole brain were acquired with TE = 40 ms, TR = 2.4 s (Switch task) or 1.8 s (Simon task), flip angle = 90o, in plane resolution = 3.1 mm, slice thickness = 7 mm, slice skip = 0.7 mm.

fMRI data analysis

The method of fMRI analysis used (XBAM, Brammer et al., 1997) makes no normality assumptions, which are usually violated in fMRI data, but instead uses median statistics to control outlier effects and permutation rather than normal theory based inference. Furthermore the most common test statistic is computed by standardising for individual difference in residual noise before embarking on second level, multi-subject testing using robust permutation-based methods. This allows a mixed effects approach to analysis — an approach that has recently been recommended following a detailed analysis of the validity and impact of normal theory based inference in fMRI in large number of subjects (Thirion et al., 2007).

Individual analysis

fMRI data were realigned to minimise motion-related artefacts (Bullmore et al., 1999a), and smoothed using a Gaussian filter (full-width half maximum, 7.2 mm). Time-series analysis of individual subject activation was performed using XBAM v3.4, with a wavelet-based re-sampling method previously described (Bullmore et al., 2001). Briefly, we first convolved each experimental condition with two Poisson model functions (delays of 4 and 8 s). We then calculated the weighted sum of these two convolutions that gave the best fit (least-squares) to the time series at each voxel. A goodness-of-fit statistic (the SSQ-ratio) was then computed at each voxel consisting of the ratio of the sum of squares of deviations from the mean intensity value due to the model (fitted time series) divided by the sum of squares due to the residuals (original time series minus model time series). The appropriate null distribution for assessing significance of any given SSQ-ratio was established using the wavelet-based data re-sampling method (Bullmore et al., 2001) and applying the model-fitting process to the re-sampled data. This process was repeated 20 times at each voxel and the data combined over all voxels, resulting in 20 null parametric maps of SSQ-ratio for each subject, which were combined to give the overall null distribution of SSQ-ratio. The same permutation strategy was applied at each voxel to preserve spatial correlation structure in the data. Activated voxels, at a < 1 level of type I error, were identified through the appropriate critical value of the SSQ-ratio from the null distribution. Activation associated with repeat or congruent trials was subtracted from activation associated with switch or incongruent trials in the Switch and Simon tasks respectively (Switch: switch > repeat, Simon: incongruent > congruent). Individual maps were registered into standard Talairach space using rigid body and affine transformation.

Group analysis

A generic group activation map was produced for each task. Individual SSQ-ratio maps were transformed into standard space, first by rigid body transformation of the fMRI data into a high-resolution inversion recovery image of the same subject, and then by affine transformation onto a Talairach template (Talairach and Tournoux, 1988). A generic activation group map was produced for each experimental condition by calculating the median observed SSQ-ratio over all subjects at each voxel in standard space and testing them against the null distribution of median SSQ-ratios computed from the identically transformed wavelet re-sampled data (Brammer et al., 1997). The voxel-level threshold was first set to 0.05 to give maximum sensitivity and to avoid type II errors. Next, a cluster level threshold was computed for the resulting 3D voxel clusters such that the final expected number of type I error clusters was < 1 per whole brain. The necessary combination of voxel and cluster level thresholds was not assumed from theory, but rather was determined by direct permutation for each data set, giving excellent Type II error control (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Cluster mass rather than a cluster extent threshold was used, to minimise discrimination against possible small, strongly responding foci of activation (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Briefly, a voxel-wide significance threshold was set (p < 0.05), and surviving voxels were assembled into 3-D clusters using a contiguity criterion. The mass of each cluster was calculated by adding the statistical values of all cluster members and thresholded at p < 0.01. In all group activation analyses, less than one false positive activation locus was expected for p < 0.05 at voxel level and p < 0.01 at cluster level.

Analysis of covariance for sex differences in brain activation

Analysis of covariance (with age as a covariate) was conducted for between-group differences using a randomisation-based test for voxel- and cluster-wise activation differences (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Less than one false positive activation was expected at p < 0.05 at voxel level and p < 0.01 at cluster level.

First, the difference between the mean SSQ-ratio values in each group was calculated at each voxel. The mean ratio was then recalculated 1000 times at each voxel following random permutation of group membership and the difference in SSQ-ratios was calculated after each permutation. The same set of random numbers was employed for the permutation at each voxel to preserve spatial correlations in each permuted map. The probability of the original SSQ-ratio difference under the null hypothesis of no effect of group membership is the number of times we observed an SSQ-ratio difference as large or larger than the original difference during the permutation process, divided by the total number of permutations. If this value exceeded our threshold for voxel-level activation, activated voxels were used to identify activation clusters, which were subjected to cluster analysis.

For any given experimental contrast, information can be obtained not only about the size but also the direction of the activation from the General linear model fit to the time series of activation. The sign of BOLD response with respect to baseline can be either positive (in “phase” with the activation condition) or negative (in “phase” with the baseline condition). This estimation can then be taken into account to determine whether a group difference reflects a difference between positive or negative BOLD responses. This analysis was performed at a group level, and was based on the positive or negative average SSQ-ratios for each group in any of the between-group activation clusters. For all between-group activation differences for both contrasts, only BOLD responses were considered in both groups that were positive based on the average SSQ-ratio of each group relative to the activation condition of interest. This also applies to the correlation analyses.

Correlation between brain activation that showed gender effects and task performance

In order to examine whether the group activation differences between males and females were related to behavioural performance, the standardised BOLD response values (SSQ-ratios) were extracted from the ANCOVA activation clusters that differed significantly between genders. Pearson's correlation analyses were then conducted between the statistical BOLD response in these areas and the main performance measures (Simon/Switch RT and error effects).

Whole-brain correlations between brain activation and age across all subjects

To test for a linear correlation between whole-brain activation and age, the Pearson product–moment correlation coefficient was first computed at each voxel in standard space between age data and signal change over all subjects. The correlation coefficients were recalculated after randomly permuting the ages but not the fMRI data. Repeating the second step many times (1000 times per voxel, then combining over all voxels) gives the distribution of correlation coefficients under the null hypothesis that there is no association between specific ages and specific BOLD effects. This null distribution can then be used to assess the probability of any particular correlation coefficient under the null hypothesis. The critical value of the correlation coefficient at any desired type 1 error level in the original (non-permuted) data could be determined by reference to this distribution. Statistical analysis was extended to cluster level as described by Bullmore et al. (1999b).

Task-independent effects of age on brain activation: conjunction of the whole-brain correlations between activation and age for the Switch and Simon tasks

We wanted to examine age-correlated brain regions that were independent of the specific cognitive control task (i.e. common to the two tasks), and which, therefore, presumably reflect common underlying cognitive control processes that mature linearly with age. For this purpose, we conducted a conjunction analysis of the whole-brain correlations between activation and age for the Switch and Simon tasks (described above), by determining the voxels where both tests of correlation with age were significant (Nichols et al., 2005).

Age by sex effects: sex differences in whole-brain correlations between brain activation and age

In order to test whether gender had differential effects on the linear age correlations across the whole brain, sex differences were examined in the correlation coefficients of brain activation with age. For each group independently, the average Pearson product–moment correlation coefficient between subject age and fMRI response was computed, and the difference in correlation between the two groups calculated. To determine the significance of this difference, the appropriate null distribution was generated by randomly permuting subjects and their ages between the groups (without replacement), and scrambling any group differences. For each of the permutations, the difference in correlation between the scrambled groups was calculated, and the resulting values were combined over all voxels to produce a whole-brain null distribution of differences in correlation. Testing was then extended to cluster level, with the cluster probability under the null hypothesis chosen to set the level of expected type I error clusters at less than one. Areas where either sex showed exclusive significant progressive or regressive changes are reported.

Results

Task performance

Cost of cognitive load

We observed the “Switch cost” on both mean reaction time (RT) (switch trial RT–repeat trial RT: mean difference = 115.8 ms (69.6); paired t(62) = 13.2, p < 0.001) and accuracy (switch trial percentage errors–repeat trial percentage errors: mean difference = 2.0 (4.0); paired t(62) = 4.0, p < 0.001) across all subjects.

Similarly, we observed the “Simon effect” on both mean reaction time (RT) (incongruent trial RT–congruent trial RT: mean difference = 117.4 ms (65.4); paired t(62) = 14.2, p < 0.001) and accuracy (incongruent trial percentage errors–congruent trial percentage errors: mean difference = 15.8 (12.6); paired t(62) = 9.9, p < 0.001) across all subjects.

Performance variables

There were no significant differences between males and females in performance of either task (Switch F(5, 55) = 2.1, p = 0.08; Simon F(5, 55) = 0.6, p = 0.7), but there was an effect of age on the group as a whole in the Simon task (F(5, 55) = 2.7, p < 0.05) and a trend in the same direction in the Switch task (F(5, 55) =2.2, p = 0.06). There was no sex × age interaction (Switch: F(5, 55) =1.5, p = 0.2; Simon: F(5, 55) = 0.5, p = 0.8).

fMRI results

Task-related brain activation

Brain activation across all subjects

In the Switch task, activation in the switch > repeat contrast was observed in medial frontal cortex, anterior and posterior cingulate gyrus, bilateral inferior parietal and superior temporal gyrus, left putamen and thalamus. In the Simon task, activation across all subjects for the incongruent > congruent contrast was observed in a bilateral network comprising inferior and dorsolateral prefrontal and premotor cortices, anterior cingulate gyrus, caudate, putamen and thalamus, inferior and superior parietal, inferior temporal and occipital cortices and cerebellum. Detailed descriptions of these activation clusters can be found in the Supplementary material.

Sex differences in brain activation

Switch task: Females, compared to males, exhibited increased activation (switch > repeat contrast) in bilateral inferior prefrontal, anterior cingulate, caudate and inferior parietal areas. No areas showed increased activation in males compared to females in this task (Fig. 2A, Table 2).

Discussion

We investigated effects of age, gender, and gender by age interactions on brain activation during two cognitive control tasks, of cognitive flexibility and interference inhibition, between adolescence and mid-adulthood. At the performance level, increasing age was associated with a more reflective performance style, trading speed for accuracy, which, however, only reached significance in the Simon task. fMRI data showed progressive linear activation increase with age in bilateral inferior/dorsolateral prefrontal and cingulate cortico-striato-thalamic, and inferior parietal regions in the Switch task, and in predominantly left inferior/middle prefrontal cortico-striato-thalamic, and bilateral temporo-parietal regions in the Simon task. Sex differences analysis revealed that, despite comparable task performance, females showed enhanced activation in bilateral inferior fronto-striatal, cingulate, parietal and cerebellar brain regions during the Switch task. During the Simon task, laterality differences were observed: females relative to males activated more left inferior fronto-temporal areas, while males activated more predominantly right inferior fronto-parietal regions compared to females. Sex by age interaction analysis showed female-specific age correlations in task-relevant left and right prefrontal brain regions during both tasks, and male-specific age correlations in cingulate, parietal and temporal brain regions.

Interestingly, the prefrontal activation clusters that were increased in females relative to males were overlapping with prefrontal clusters that correlated with age across the whole sample (see Figs. 2 and 3 respectively), suggesting a more mature pattern of prefrontal brain activation. This was further supported by the sex by age interaction effects that showed female-specific age correlations in task-relevant left and right prefrontal brain regions during both tasks. In males, by contrast, the parietal activation differences relative to females, i.e. decreased activation during the Switch task and increased activation during the Simon task, resembled the more mature activation pattern, as demonstrated by the age correlations across the whole group (see Figs. 2 and 3 respectively). In the Simon task, these sex differences in activation, furthermore, were related to gender-specific measures of behavioural efficiency, suggesting strategy differences. Sex differences in brain activation patterns during higher level cognitive control tasks appear thus to be related to underlying differences in the age modulation and thus functional development of these brain regions, which may, in turn, be responsible for differences in cognitive strategies.

Age effects

Across all subjects, consistent with the literature on Simon and Switch tasks (Monsell, 2003 and Simon and Berbaum, 1988), speed and accuracy were reduced during Switch and Simon trials compared to repeat or congruent trials respectively. In the Simon task, increasing age was associated with a less impulsive performance style, favouring accuracy at the cost of speed. Thus, with increasing age, participants made fewer errors during incongruent trials at the cost of slowing down in speed, leading to a higher Simon RT effect. Similar developmental effects on the speed–accuracy trade-off have been observed previously in cognitive switching tasks (Davidson et al., 2006), although in this data, the speed–accuracy trade-off did not reach significance for the Switch task.

In line with previous findings, brain activation was more sensitive to age effects than performance (Marsh et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2006 and Rubia et al., 2007). The finding of progressive functional recruitment of bilateral inferior prefrontal and anterior cingulate cortex, caudate, putamen, thalamus and inferior parietal lobe during switching, extends previous findings from developmental imaging studies using the same switching task in male subjects (Rubia et al., 2006), and other switching tasks in younger age ranges (Casey et al., 2004 and Crone et al., 2006).

The finding of linear age correlations in predominantly left inferior prefrontal cortex, caudate, putamen, and temporo-parietal cortex activation in the Simon task, extends findings from other developmental imaging studies using similar interference inhibition tasks (Adleman et al., 2002, Booth et al., 2003, Bunge et al., 2002, Marsh et al., 2006 and Rubia et al., 2006). Left inferior prefrontal cortex is considered the key mediator of cognitive interference inhibition measured in these tasks (Derrfuss et al., 2005, Liu et al., 2004, Peterson et al., 2002 and Rubia et al., 2006), as opposed to the role of predominantly right hemispheric inferior prefrontal cortex for motor response inhibition and switching (Derrfuss et al., 2005, Garavan et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2003, Rubia et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2007 and Smith et al., 2004). The Simon task measures the cognitive inhibition of distracting information, mediated by predominantly left hemispheric fronto-striatal systems of interference inhibition; while the Switch task requires the inhibition of a previously valid motor response (right inferior prefrontal cortex) in order to switch to a new one (left inferior prefrontal cortex), thus eliciting activation in bilateral fronto-striatal networks (Rubia et al., 2006). Our findings therefore, show that post-adolescent development of cognitive switching and interference inhibition is associated with the progressive recruitment of task-specific neural networks for performance. They show an age-related increase in the functional recruitment of left inferior fronto-striato-thalamic networks for interference suppression, and bilateral inferior fronto-striato-thalamic networks for cognitive flexibility respectively, presumably reflecting progressive task-specific functional specialisation.

In addition to task-specific age correlations, we also observed common, task-independent linear age-correlated effects across the two tasks in left inferior/dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, in the junction of left superior temporal and inferior parietal gyri, and in a thalamic cluster, bordering the putamen. These brain regions presumably mediate progressively maturing cognitive control processes that are involved in the performance of both tasks and do not therefore show task-specificity. In fact, left inferior and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex has consistently been found to be activated during the performance of Simon and Switch tasks in this and previous studies (Liu et al., 2004, Rubia et al., 2006 and Smith et al., 2004), including a meta-analysis on both tasks (Derrfuss et al., 2005), and has been suggested to be crucial for updating task representation and integrating attentional and motor information (Derrfuss et al., 2004 and Derrfuss et al., 2005). Left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex is thought to be crucial for action selection and performance monitoring in tasks of cognitive control, independent of the specific task (Gehring and Knight, 2000, MacDonald et al., 2000 and Rubia et al., 2001). Commonly activated thalamic and striatal regions may be part of task-specific fronto-striato-thalamic networks that pass through common thalamic and striatal nodes. The left superior temporal/inferior parietal junction is known to mediate visual–spatial executive attention (Rushworth et al., 2001 and Rushworth et al., 2003) and attention to saliency (Kanwisher and Wojciulik, 2000 and Kiehl and Liddle, 2001). The recruitment of visual–spatial attention to both increased stimulus saliency as well as more demanding response execution processes is common to both tasks, given that target trials (whether Switch or Simon trials) are more salient and require the organisation of more difficult responses compared to non-target trials. The findings thus demonstrate progressive age-related maturation of task-independent neurocognitive processes in left lateral prefrontal, thalamic and temporo-parietal regions, presumably mediating executive attention and performance monitoring processes underlying the performance of both tasks.

Sex differences and sex by age interactions

No sex differences were observed in performance of either task. While there has been controversy over whether females are superior to males in response incompatibility tasks, our observation of similar performance between the sexes is in line with several previous developmental studies that used the Simon or Stroop task (Daniel et al., 2000, Marsh et al., 2006, Peterson et al., 2002 and Van der Elst et al., 2008). Likewise, neuropsychological studies on similar and even more difficult switching tasks than the one used in this study, including the Wisconsin Card Sorting task and the Intradimensional/Extradimensional (ID/ED) Set Shifting Task, found no sex differences in performance across adult and child development (Caffarra et al., 2004, Crone et al., 2006 and De Luca et al., 2003).

Nevertheless, despite comparable task performance, we observed both sex, and sex by age interaction effects on brain activation. In the Switch task, females showed increased activation in bilateral inferior prefrontal cortex, caudate, inferior parietal and cerebellar regions, typically activated in adults and children during cognitive flexibility in the same (Rubia et al., 2006, Smith et al., 2004 and Smith et al., 2006) and similar tasks (Derrfuss et al., 2005). The finding of increased bilateral inferior prefrontal activation in females extends previous findings of enhanced right prefrontal activation during perceptual flexibility operations in mental rotation tasks (Hugdahl et al., 2006, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003), as well as during motor response inhibition (Garavan et al., 2006). Taken together with these studies, our findings suggest greater reliance in females on inhibitory prefrontal mechanisms necessary for cognitive switching. Unlike studies of mental rotation, however, which show enhanced bilateral inferior parietal activation in males (Hugdahl et al., 2006, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003), we observed enhanced activation in right inferior parietal cortex in females, during our more cognitively demanding switching task. The parietal lobe, together with inferior prefrontal cortex, is a key mediator of cognitive flexibility (Rubia et al., 2006, Smith et al., 2004 and Sohn et al., 2000). It would thus appear that females recruited the entire switching network, comprising fronto-striatal, parietal and cerebellar areas (Rubia et al., 2006) to a greater extent than males.

The fronto-striatal parts of this increased network in females, overlapped with brain activation clusters that were age-correlated across all subjects (see Figs. 2 and 3 respectively). This could potentially suggest that the fronto-striatal activation increase in females reflects a more mature recruitment pattern. This is further supported by the age by sex interaction effects that showed exclusive, female-specific age correlations in bilateral inferior prefrontal brain regions.

One could also argue that overall reduced activation in males, in light of comparable task performance, may suggest that males found the Switch task easier to perform, reflecting enhanced neuronal efficiency (Neubauer et al., 2005). There is no evidence for superior male performance in this study, or in other studies using relatively more difficult switching tasks, like the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test (Caffarra et al., 2004, Crone et al., 2006 and De Luca et al., 2003). However, there is consistent evidence for superior visual–spatial skills in males (De Luca et al., 2003, Mekarski et al., 1996 and Wegesin, 1998). It is therefore possible that male participants engaged visual–spatial processes to switch attention between two spatial dimensions, while females may have relied on top-down inhibitory systems to manage response switching. Importantly, neural efficiency correlates have been shown to interact with sex and task demands, with males showing neural efficiency gains in visual–spatial and females in verbal tasks (Neubauer et al., 2005). It has been hypothesised that, during perceptive flexibility tasks (e.g. mental rotation), men utilise a parietal lobe mediated gestalt strategy in contrast to women who use a more frontal analytic/serial strategy (Thomsen et al., 2000). In support of this proposal and its relationship with relative neural efficiency, electrophysiological studies examining the degree of synchrony of high-frequency oscillations, thought to partly underlie functional network integration, have shown females exhibiting increased synchrony in frontal, and males in parieto-occipital regions during perceptual search in an auditory oddball task (Williams et al., 2005). Given this previous evidence and the fact that the Switch task used in this study had a relatively high load on visual–spatial set shifting (i.e switching between two spatial dimensions), it is plausible that males might have performed the task with enhanced neural efficiency, i.e. reduced recruitment of task-relevant brain regions. The hypothesis that reduced parietal activation in males may reflect enhanced neural efficiency is partially supported by the finding of a negative correlation between parietal activation and age across the whole sample, suggesting that in this region it was males, not females, who exhibited the more mature activation pattern.

The finding of enhanced caudate activation in females is interesting in light of sex-dimorphic regional basal ganglia volume, with the caudate developing earlier and being larger in girls and the globus pallidus being larger in boys (Giedd et al., 1997, Giedd et al., 2006 and Sowell et al., 2002). This dissociation between the associative basal ganglia (caudate) and their output structures (pallidum) may underlie several cognitive/behavioural sex differences; for example, males activate the globus pallidus and motor thalamus more than females during motor response inhibition (Li et al., 2006). Increased activation in the caudate head in females, which was age-correlated across the whole sample, together with increased prefrontal activation in both tasks, may reflect underlying basal ganglia dimorphisms, and their impact on the functional development of fronto-striatal executive networks.

In the Simon task we observed laterality differences: females showed increased activation in left inferior prefrontal, bilateral temporal and posterior cingulate cortices, and males showed increased activation in right inferior prefrontal and inferior parietal cortices, caudate and precuneus. Furthermore, temporal lobe activation in females and right parietal activation in males correlated negatively with the Simon accuracy effect, suggesting that they used different brain regions to achieve similar performance efficiency. Even the earliest behavioural studies of sex differences on performance of the Stroop task had already suggested that, although performance differences were inconsistently identified (MacLeod, 1991), the relative abilities of the sexes, i.e. superior verbal skills in women and superior spatial skills in men, may determine the cognitive strategies they use to suppress interference (Mekarski et al., 1996). As mentioned above, left inferior prefrontal cortex is thought to be the key mediator of interference inhibition (Liu et al., 2004, Peterson et al., 2002 and Rubia et al., 2006), suggesting that females showed a more specialised activation pattern.

The increased activation in the parietal lobes in males may be related to a greater reliance on visual–spatial strategies to manage interference inhibition; i.e. males may suppress interference by relying on earlier, bottom-up visual–spatial perception mechanisms to ignore the interfering information at an early visual–spatial attention stage. By contrast, females may need to recruit left prefrontal function for later-stage top-down inhibition of the tendency to make an incorrect response to the interfering information. This would be in line with evidence for sex differences in event related potentials (ERP) during similar interference inhibition tasks, where females exhibit increased P300 ERP responses during relevant stimuli (Shen, 2005 and Steffensen et al., 2008) and increased inhibitory N400 ERP responses during irrelevant, interfering stimuli (Steffensen et al., 2008). Those results also prompted the suggestion that the increased inhibitory responsiveness to distracting irrelevant stimuli indicates that females may rely more on top-down inhibition of distracters, implying that males may be better at suppressing the influence of distracters at an earlier stage, perhaps during early visual processing (Steffensen et al., 2008).

Sex differences in laterality of brain activation, similar to the ones we report here in prefrontal and parietal regions, have been observed in adults in other task contexts, such as working memory and visual object discrimination (Georgopoulos et al., 2001 and Speck et al., 2000). Likewise, sex-related differences in magnitude and laterality of temporal lobe activation have also previously been observed in adults during other tasks including episodic memory, object/emotion recognition and language processing (Bell et al., 2006, Georgopoulos et al., 2001 and Lee et al., 2002). Our findings of sex differences in magnitude and laterality of activation in frontal, parietal and temporal brain activation are therefore by and large in line with previous adult studies, but extend these to the domain of cognitive inhibition functions and to a wider developmental window including adolescence.

As in the Switch task, the brain regions that were increased in activation in females, i.e. left fronto-temporal regions, were age-correlated across all subjects in the Simon task. The fact that, during both tasks, clusters of increased activation in females compared to males — in bilateral medial and inferior fronto-caudate (Switch task) and left inferior fronto-temporal areas (Simon task) – partly overlapped with, or were adjacent to activation clusters that correlated linearly with age across the whole sample (see Figs. 2 and 3), suggests the possibility that females displayed a more mature pattern of activation in these specific fronto-striatal and fronto-temporal regions compared to males.

Similarly, it can be argued that males appeared to show more mature activation patterns in the parietal cortex. The decreased parietal activation during the Switch task in males compared to females, overlapped with brain activation clusters that decreased with age; while the increased parietal activation clusters in the Simon task, overlapped with brain regions that increased linearly with age. Thus we propose that males showed the more mature parietal activation pattern in a task-specific way, showing reduced recruitment of parietal lobe mechanisms during switching, and enhanced recruitment of parietal activation during interference inhibition.

The findings also show that the pattern of relative maturity of activation in a brain region can be task-dependent, and highlight the importance of investigating different cognitive functions within the same subjects to elucidate task-dependent and task-independent age effects.

In summary, females showed enhanced recruitment of fronto-striatal and fronto-temporal regions, which in turn showed progressive increases in activation with age across the whole sample, while males showed task-specific differences in parietal lobe activation, in the same direction as the age modulation of parietal activation. We hypothesise that this could potentially reflect a more mature activation pattern in fronto-striatal and fronto-temporal regions in females, and a more mature activation pattern in parietal regions in males. This proposal is further supported by the gender by age interactions, showing that prefrontal brain regions were exclusively age-correlated in females during both tasks, while parietal brain regions were exclusively age-correlated in males during the Simon task.

The argument derived from our sex by age interaction findings, of a more mature functional activation pattern in females in fronto-striatal and fronto-temporal regions, is in line with converging evidence showing that structural developmental processes in frontal and temporal regions, peaking during puberty and persisting through to early adulthood, take place earlier in females than in males (Campbell et al., 2005, Giedd et al., 1999, Giedd et al., 2006 and Lenroot et al., 2007). It remains a matter of speculation whether our hypothesis of a more mature pattern of parietal activation in males is related to earlier male structural maturation of the parietal lobes. Males compared to females show reduced cortical thickness and grey matter volume across the lifespan, between late childhood and late adulthood (Allen et al., 2003 and Sowell et al., 2007) and this has been suggested to be potentially related to an earlier structural maturation (Sowell et al., 2007).

Limitations

The strength of this study is the relatively large sample size, permutation fMRI data analyses across the whole brain, and the unprecedented sex by age interaction analysis across the whole brain. Furthermore, the use of two different cognitive control tasks within one study allowed us to elucidate task-specific and task-independent effects.

In this study we were primarily interested in sex differences in the linear age correlations with brain activation. However, non-linear developmental changes are known to occur in functional imaging data (Brown et al., 2005), and should be investigated in future studies of larger sample sizes. Further, age by sex interaction effects on functional brain development could be more thoroughly investigated using large-scale longitudinal studies given that cross-sectional studies are confounded by cohort effects.

Conclusion

To our knowledge, this is the first fMRI dataset combining two tasks of cognitive control that tested for sex differences in brain activation in the light of underlying gender-specific effects on age-related functional brain maturation between childhood and adulthood. We provide first evidence that the superior reliance on functional frontal mechanisms in females, and on functional parietal mechanisms in males, as observed in this study and in previous functional neuroimaging studies during other executive function tasks, may potentially be determined by gender differences in the post-adolescent functional development of these brain regions. These gender differences in functional brain maturation may also underlie aspects of the well-documented differences in cognitive strategies and relative abilities between the sexes. This issue has important implications for the study of developmental neuropsychiatric disorders, which are characterised by significant sex differences in illness onset, progression and prevalence.
