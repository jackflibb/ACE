Changes in male brain responses to emotional faces from adolescence to middle age

Abstract

Facial emotion perception is fundamental to human social behaviour, and changes with age. Nevertheless, age-related differences in the relative activation of components of emotion processing networks are poorly understood. Thus we measured brain activity with event-related fMRI in 40 right handed healthy male controls, age range 8–50 years, during implicit processing of fearful, disgusted, and a control condition of neutral facial expressions. There was a significant negative correlation between increasing age and neural response to fearful and disgusted expressions in dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (BA 10), and middle frontal gyri (BA 6). Hence, in healthy subjects, the functional anatomy of facial emotion processing is not ‘hard-wired’, but undergoes progressive change into adulthood. Possible explanations for the age-related changes in dorsomedial and middle frontal cortical activity may include a reduction in the attentional demands of appraising facial expressions as perceptual skill increases, or changes in processing the self-relevance of facial expressions during social and cognitive development.

Introduction

Both developmental disorders of childhood (e.g. autism) and adult-onset psychiatric disorders (e.g. schizophrenia), are associated with impairments in facial expression recognition, and functional anatomical differences in facial emotion processing compared to healthy controls (Phillips et al., 2003a, Phillips et al., 2003b and Herba and Phillips, 2004). Also, the incidence of some psychiatric disorders changes with increasing age — for example, the onset of paranoid schizophrenia (in which people misconstrue the social behaviour of others, and/or differ in emotional reactivity) normally peaks at age 18 in men, and is rare before age 10 and past age 40 (Sham et al., 1994 and Aylward et al., 2005). Hence, a necessary first step in determining the pathogenesis of disturbances of social cognition is to understand how the functional anatomy of facial emotion processing develops and changes in healthy individuals throughout life.

In healthy adults, facial emotion processing is associated with activation in core visual analysis areas (inferior occipital gyrus, fusiform gyrus, and superior temporal gyrus); subcortical and limbic regions (such as basal ganglia, insula, and amygdala); and prefrontal regions (such as ventromedial and ventrolateral regions, and anterior cingulate gyrus) (Haxby et al., 2002, Phillips et al., 2003a and Phillips et al., 2003b). Also, there is differential activation of limbic regions depending on emotion type (e.g. greater amygdala activation for fear, and insula activation for disgust) (Phillips et al., 2003a and Phillips et al., 2003b). Recent evidence also suggests that children may activate similar neural systems when processing facial emotions (Lobaugh et al., 2006) and have differential emotion-specific responses (e.g. greater amygdala and insula activation to fear and disgust than to sadness) (Lobaugh et al., 2006).

Nevertheless, the developmental trajectories of changes in the relative activation of components of emotion processing networks are poorly understood. Some prior studies have reported age-related reductions in limbic and/or visual cortical responses to emotional expressions (Iidaka et al., 2002, Gunning-Dixon et al., 2003 and Mather et al., 2004). For example, one study employing a gender discrimination task reported that compared to younger subjects (mean age 25.1 ± 5.0 years), older subjects (mean age 65.2 ± 2.6 years) showed reduced activity in left amygdala to negative expressions, and right parahippocampal gyrus and parieto-occipital cortex to positive expressions (Iidaka et al., 2002). Another study employing an emotion discrimination task reported that compared to younger subjects (mean age 25.8 ± 3.1 years), older subjects (mean age 72.3 ± 7.6 years) showed reduced activation in visual cortices and amygdala (Gunning-Dixon et al., 2003). A further study reported reduced arousal ratings and amygdala activation in older (mean age 78.41 ± 4.86) compared to younger adults (mean age 23.41 ± 3.24) to negative but not positive facial expressions (Mather et al., 2004). Nevertheless, contrasts of neural responses across younger age ranges have produced conflicting findings. For example, one study showed reduced amygdala activation in adults (mean age 30.76 ± 3.07 years) relative to adolescents (mean age 13.2 ± 2.60 years) in a contrast of fearful to neutral expressions (Monk et al., 2003). By contrast, another reported that adults (mean age 24 ± 6.6 years) showed greater amygdala activation to fearful relative to neutral expressions compared to children (mean age 11 ± 2.4 years) (Thomas et al., 2001). Hence, there is accumulating evidence from contrasts of younger and older adults that older age is associated with reductions in limbic and/or visual cortical responses to facial expressions of emotion (with evidence that this effect may be restricted to negative expressions; Mather et al., 2004). However, it remains unclear as to whether the pattern of age-related reductions in limbic and/or visual cortical activity to expressions is present from adolescence through to middle age. This is an important period for investigations of the effects of aging on social cognition because it encompasses the transition from adolescence to adulthood, a critical period of social development associated with the peak incidence of adult-onset psychiatric disorders such as schizophrenia (Sham et al., 1994). It also includes key periods of brain maturation (e.g. myelination of the frontal cortex continues to the thirties) (Craik and Bialystok, 2006).

Hence, we investigated age-related changes in the functional anatomy of facial emotion processing spanning the whole period from adolescence to middle age while including a variety of facial emotions. We examined neural responses to fearful, disgusted, and a control condition of neutral facial expressions, using event-related fMRI, in 40 healthy right handed males of normal intelligence, age range 8–50 years. We examined neural responses to fearful expressions because of their proposed key role in socialization, whereby developing individuals learn to avoid behaviours that elicit the aversive arousal associated with fearful expressions (Blair, 2003). Also, prior studies had not investigated age-related changes in neural responses to disgusted expressions per se, although two studies had included disgust in mixed emotion blocks ( Iidaka et al., 2002 and Gunning-Dixon et al., 2003). We therefore included disgusted expressions as important social signals which convey information about avoidance of aversive or potentially noxious stimuli (e.g. rotten foods) in addition to some forms of socially unacceptable behaviour ( Blair, 2003).

We postulated that the affective response elicited by facial expressions of emotion per se (the reinforcement value) would be maximal in childhood and adolescence to motivate attention to important social cues during the most formative period of socialization ( Joffe, 1997, Blair, 2003 and Monk et al., 2003), in contrast to adults who can enlist mature social perceptual skills and contextual knowledge to determine the meaning and emotional significance of facial expressions ( Herba and Phillips, 2004). Emotionally salient expressions elicit increased activity in visual cortices and limbic regions in contrast to neutral expressions ( Surguladze et al., 2003a and Surguladze et al., 2003b). Hence we predicted that age-related reductions in the affective response elicited by expressions of emotion would be associated with attenuation of limbic and visual cortical activity ( Surguladze et al., 2003a, Surguladze et al., 2003b and Vuilleumier et al., 2004)—in other words, that the pattern of reduced limbic and visual cortical activity reported in contrasts of young and old adults would also be shown in the period from adolescence to middle age.

Methods

Participants

We included 40 right handed unmedicated male volunteers (age range 8–50 years, mean age 24 (± 9.6 S.D.), with the majority of subjects in the adolescent to age 40 range). All the participants, and/or their parents gave written informed consent/assent as approved by the local research ethics committee (Institute of Psychiatry, South London and Maudsley NHS Trust). Exclusion criteria included psychiatric and/or physical disorders affecting brain function (e.g. depression and/or psychosis, epilepsy and/or hypertension). Clinical brain MRI was normal in all subjects.

Experimental design

Each volunteer participated in two, 6-minute experiments employing event-related fMRI. Subjects were presented with facial expressions of disgust in one, and fear in the other experiment, at two intensities (low and prototypic), plus neutral expressions, from a standardized series of facial expressions posed by 10 white adult volunteers (5 male, 5 female) (Young et al., 2002, Surguladze et al., 2003a and Surguladze et al., 2003b). Images of each volunteer were presented twice at the three intensity levels in each experiment. In addition to presentations of 20 neutral, 20 prototypic, and 20 low intensity expressions, a fixation cross was presented 12 times in interstimulus intervals, generating 72 trials per experiment. Each facial stimulus was presented for 2 seconds (s) subtending a visual angle of 6°. The duration of the inter-stimulus interval (ISI) varied from 3 to 8 s according to a Poisson distribution to prevent subjects from predicting the timing of the next stimulus presentation with average interval 5 s. In subsequent analyses, the fixation cross was used as the baseline stimulus in each of the experiments. Stimuli of interest (faces) and the baseline stimulus (fixation cross) were thus mixed in the same experiment in a pseudo-random manner, so reducing habituation effects. A gender discrimination task was employed in which subjects were requested to decide upon the sex of each face and press one of two buttons accordingly with the right thumb. Hence, our design was ‘implicit’ in that it enabled objective confirmation of attendance to the stimuli, without requiring that subjects engage in conscious appraisal of the emotional content of each expression (Surguladze et al., 2003a and Surguladze et al., 2003b). This increased the likelihood of measuring activity in neural systems involved in automatic appraisal of facial expressions. In prescan testing all subjects were able to identify the sex of the faces correctly.

Image acquisition

MR images were acquired using a GE Signa 1.5 Tesla system (General Electric, Milwaukee, WI) with an operating console and software (Advanced Nuclear Magnetic Resonance, Woburn, MA) for gradient echo echoplanar imaging (EPI) at the Maudsley Hospital, London, UK. A quadrature birdcage headcoil was used for RF transmission and reception. An inversion recovery EPI dataset was acquired at 43 near-axial 3 mm thick planes parallel to the AC–PC line: TE 73 ms, TI 180 ms, TR 16 s, in-plane resolution 1.72 mm, interslice gap 0.3 mm, matrix size: 128 × 128 pixels. This higher resolution EPI dataset provided whole brain coverage and was later used to register the fMRI datasets acquired from each individual in standard stereotactic space. 180 T2*-weighted images depicting BOLD contrast were acquired at each of 16 near-axial non-contiguous 7 mm thick planes parallel to the intercommissural (AC–PC) line: TE 40 ms, TR 2 s, in-plane resolution 3.44 mm, interslice gap 0.7 mm, matrix size: 64 × 64 pixels.

Neuroimaging data analysis

Data were analyzed with software developed at the Institute of Psychiatry, London, using a non-parametric approach (for full description and references, see www.brainmap.co.uk), generating whole brain activation maps of percentage change in BOLD signal to contrasts of interest (neutral, fearful, and disgusted expressions versus baseline (fixation cross)). These expression-baseline contrasts were then employed in subsequent analyses. In order to determine neural responses to fearful and disgusted expressions in the group as a whole, two-way Analyses of Variance (ANOVA) of percentage change in BOLD signal to prototypic fearful versus neutral expressions, and prototypic disgusted versus neutral expressions, were undertaken.

Correlation analyses

Correlation analyses were performed between age and neural responses to contrasts of prototypic fearful and neutral, and prototypic disgusted and neutral respectively, in order to control for the effects of age on processing non-emotional aspects of face stimuli. The Pearson product moment correlation coefficient was then computed between the age of each subject and the fMRI responses at each voxel for each subject, yielding one correlation coefficient (r) per intracerebral voxel. To determine the significance of these correlation coefficients the appropriate null distribution of r was computed robustly using data permutation. The order of the behavioural data was randomly permuted without replacement (i.e. each data value occurs once but the order is changed) breaking the association between individual behavioural data and their corresponding fMRI responses. The correlation coefficient was then recomputed many times at each voxel and the resulting values of r combined over all voxels to produce a whole brain null distribution of r. The critical value of r for significance at any particular p value was obtained from this distribution after sorting it by value of r and selecting the appropriate point from the sorted distribution, e.g. the critical value of r for a one-tailed test at p 0.05 would be the value of r in the null distribution chosen such that 95% of all the null values of r lay below that point. Testing was then extended to cluster level as described previously. The cluster probability under the null hypothesis was chosen to set the level of expected type I error clusters at an acceptable level (< 1 per whole brain).

Having identified brain areas showing significant linear correlations with age we carried out further examination of the fMRI responses by extracting the average fMRI responses for each individual from selected clusters (Supplementary Information (S.I.), Table 2). This further analysis was carried out using routines written in the statistical programming language R (http://CRAN.R-project.org). In order to allow for outlier effects we used the package rlm for robust fitting of linear models (included in the MASS package — see Venables W N and Ribley B D (2002) Modern applied statistics with R, fourth edition, Springer). The contribution of outliers is reduced in the rlm routine by iteratively computing weights (< 1 for outliers) which are applied to the data.

Results

Behavioural results

In prescan testing all subjects were able to identify the sex of the faces correctly. Analysis of behavioural responses showed that there were no correlations between age and response accuracy or reaction time for fearful, disgusted, and neutral faces respectively (Supplementary Information (S.I.) Table 1).

Two-way analyses of variance of emotional and neutral expressions

Group Brain Activation Maps of the fear-neutral and disgust-neutral contrasts in all 40 subjects revealed activations in extrastriate cortices and other components of face processing networks (S.I. Table 3, S.I. Figs. 1 and 2).

Correlations of neural responses to emotion-neutral contrasts with age

Correlation analyses of age and neural response to the fear-neutral, and disgust-neutral contrasts revealed reductions in relative neural response with age in similar brain regions. Specifically, there was a negative correlation between age and activity in right middle frontal gyrus (BA 6); and superior frontal gyrus (BA 10) (which also formed part of a larger medial prefrontal cortical cluster in the fear-neutral contrast) (see Table 1 and Table 2; Fig. 1 and Fig. 3). However, some age-related reductions in neural activity to increasing age were specific to each emotion-neutral contrast—for example, right thalamus in the fear-neutral contrast (Fig. 1A, Table 1), and right insula for the disgust-neutral contrast (Fig. 3A, Table 2). Also, all brain regions showing relative increases in activation with increasing age were specific to each emotion—for example, right fusiform gyrus (BA 19) for the fear-neutral contrast (Figs. 2A,B; Table 1), and left uncus for the disgust-neutral contrast (Figs. 4A,B; Table 2). All clusters from which bold effect data were extracted remained significant after weighting for outlier effects (S.I. Table 2).

Discussion

Our study demonstrates that in healthy male subjects the functional anatomy of facial emotion processing is not ‘hard-wired’, but undergoes progressive change from adolescence to middle age. Specifically, there were age-related reductions in activation of right middle frontal gyrus (BA 6), and right superior frontal gyrus (BA 10), during implicit processing of fearful and disgusted expressions, in addition to age-related changes in neural responses which are specific to fear and disgust respectively. Further, these age-related changes in neural responses to fearful and disgusted expressions occurred in the absence of changes in task performance. The lack of age-related changes in performance of a gender discrimination task was consistent with a prior study that did not find significant differences in gender discrimination when comparing young (mean age 25.1 years) and much older individuals (65.2 years) (Iidaka et al., 2002). The absence of age-related effects on gender discrimination suggests that alterations in brain activation were associated with changes in the implicit (incidental) processing of the emotional content of the expressions, rather than the gender discrimination task itself.

Superomedial aspects of BA 10, along with medial aspects of BA 9, comprise the dorsomedial prefrontal cortex (DMPC). This brain region is activated during the judgment of emotional compared to non-emotional stimuli (Lane et al., 1997, Gusnard et al., 2001 and Northoff and Bermpohl, 2004), and the cognitive re-appraisal of emotional stimuli (Ochsner et al., 2004). Similarly, activation of middle frontal gyri is associated with maintaining attention to stimuli (Yamasaki et al., 2002). Hence, our findings may reflect a progressive age-related decline in the attentional resources dedicated to processing facial expressions as the ability to determine their significance improves.

An alternative explanation of our findings of an age-related reduction in prefrontal activation follows from studies reporting that DMPC is activated by tasks involved in determining the relevance of stimuli to the self (Gusnard, 2005). For example, DMPC is activated during tasks of: 1) mental state attribution (Frith and Frith, 2003); 2) interaction of self with others (Kampe et al., 2003), and when observing social interactions (Iacoboni et al., 2004); 3) determining the self-relevance of trait adjectives or statements (Kelley et al., 2002, Zysset et al., 2002 and Fossati et al., 2003); 4) autobiographical memory retrieval (Fink et al., 1996); and 5) moral judgments (Greene and Haidt, 2002). Age-related attenuation of activity in these structures may therefore indicate an automatic processing bias towards determining the self-relevance of facial expressions in the most formative period of social development, which declines as mature social perceptual skills and contextual knowledge emerge as a basis for determining the significance of social cues (Herba and Phillips, 2004).

A related possibility is that age-related changes in processing facial expressions reflect changing motivational priorities. For example, socioemotional selectivity theory postulates that the relative priority of different sets of goals changes as a function of perceived time left in life (Carstensen et al., 1999 and Lockenhoff and Carstensen, 2004). From this perspective, younger individuals – with a more open-ended time horizon – prioritize goals to maximize opportunities, such as acquisition of information relevant to personal development and establishing social contacts that could be helpful in the future. In contrast, as time-horizons narrow with increasing age, emotion regulation becomes a higher priority, and older people appear to diminish the processing of negative information that might reduce positive affect (Carstensen et al., 2000 and Mather and Carstensen, 2003). Hence, findings of age-related reductions in DMPC (BA 10) to two negative emotional expressions (fear and disgust) are consistent with predictions from this theory, given that this brain region is known to be active when stimuli are processed as relevant to the self. Future studies should therefore examine age-related changes in neural responses to positive (happy) expressions with a similar paradigm to test the prediction that age-related reductions in DMPC will not be seen.

However, a recent study of age-related changes in neural responses to fearful and happy expressions employing a different (passive viewing) task in a large mixed gender sample (age range 12–79) reported an age-related increase in MPFC activity for a fear-neutral contrast, in addition to an age-related decrease in medial prefrontal (MPFC) (BA24/32, BA 9/10) activity for a happiness-neutral contrast (Williams et al., 2006). The differences in activation by facial fear of MPFC/DMPFC between the prior study and ours may be due to a number of factors. For example the previous study included: 1) facial fear stimuli of 0.5 s duration; 2) a passive viewing task (subjects were requested to attend to the faces in preparation for post-testing behavioural assessments); and 3) older people (upper age 79 years). In contrast our stimuli lasted 2 s, and we employed an implicit gender discrimination task (in which age did not affect performance) in subjects from adolescence to mid-life (mostly spanning teens to age 40). Hence, the discrepant results may reflect emotion-specific, age-related, differences in the effect of stimulus duration and/or task (passive viewing versus gender discrimination) on DMPC activation. For example, the developmental course of emotion processing may vary with task. This possibility requires further investigation in which emotion type (e.g. positive and negative expressions), stimulus duration and task (e.g. passive viewing/gender discrimination/emotion recognition) is varied in the same group of subjects. Also, our sample included males only, whereas prior studies have indicated gender-related differences in facial emotion perception (Killgore et al., 2001). We are currently acquiring data in females to examine the effects of gender and age on facial emotion processing.

We hypothesised that the salience of fearful and disgusted facial expressions would be maximal in childhood and adolescence to motivate attention to important social cues during socialization, and would decline as more mature social perceptual skills and contextual knowledge emerge as a basis for determining the significance of social cues. In keeping with this hypothesis, we found evidence of age-related reductions in right insula activation to facial disgust (Table 2, Fig. 3A). However, we did not find evidence of age-related reductions in amygdala activation to fearful faces. This may be because the stimulus parameters (e.g. a relatively long stimulus duration of 2 s) or MR acquisition parameters (Stocker et al., 2006) were suboptimal for detecting differential amygdala response in a fear-neutral contrast—for example, we did not detect differential amygdala activation in 2 way between condition ANOVA contrasts of fearful and neutral expressions at the whole group level (S.I. Table 3). Also, we did not find evidence of age-related reductions in visual cortical responses to either fearful or disgusted expressions. Instead, there was evidence of age-related increases in visual cortical responses to fearful faces left lingual gyrus (BA 19), right fusiform gyrus (BA 19). Evidence suggests that fusiform–extrastriate cortices are involved in representing social semantic knowledge in addition to face perception (Schultz, 2005). Hence, a possible interpretation of these findings is that age-related increases in activation of fusiform–extrastriate cortices to fearful expressions may reflect increasing engagement of brain systems involved in determining the contextual significance of social–emotional cues.

Age-related changes in BOLD signal ranged from 0.2% to 0.4% variation from younger to older males (e.g. Fig. 1, Fig. 2, Fig. 3 and Fig. 4). The size of these changes may reflect the relatively narrow age range of our sample, and the absence of age-related changes in task performance. Nevertheless, all of the results we report were highly significant in the context of a conservative analysis and thresholding method to reduce the risk of type 1 errors (see Methods, and Table 1 and Table 2).

In summary, our results demonstrate evidence of progressive change in the neural substrates of facial expression perception from adolescence to middle age. Age-related reductions in middle frontal gyrus (BA 6) and DMPFC (BA 10) may reflect a progressive reduction in the attentional demands of appraising facial expressions as perceptual skill increases. This transition may also be associated with attenuation of a processing bias towards determining the self-relevance of facial expressions during the most formative periods of social and cognitive development.
