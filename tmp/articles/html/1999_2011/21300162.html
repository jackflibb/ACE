Developmental effects of reward on sustained attention networks

  • Anna B. Smitha, , ,
  • Rozmin Halaria,
  • Vincent Giampetrob,
  • Michael Brammerb,
  • Katya Rubiaa
  • a Department of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, Institute of Psychiatry, King's College London, UK
  • b Department of Biostatistics and Computing, Institute of Psychiatry, King's College London, UK
Received 23 April 2010, Revised 12 January 2011, Accepted 27 January 2011, Available online 4 February 2011

Abstract

Adolescence is typified by significant maturation in higher-level attention functions coupled with less developed control over motivation, and enhanced sensitivity to novelty and reward.

This study used event-related functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) in seventy male and female participants aged between 10 and 43 years to identify age-related linear changes in cognitive sustained attention systems and the impact of reward on these systems, using a sustained attention task with and without a rewarded condition.

For the non-rewarded sustained attention contrast, increasing age was associated with activation increases in typical regions of sustained attention including right inferior frontal, superior temporo-parietal and cerebellar cortices. Age-related activation decreases were observed within more posterior regions including posterior cingulate, insula and posterior cerebellar cortices, presumably mediating visual–spatial saliency detection.

The effect of reward on sustained attention networks was associated with increased activation with age in regions associated with both executive attention control and reward processing, including dorsolateral, inferior and ventromedial prefrontal cortices (PFC), striatum, and temporo-parietal regions, suggestive of greater integration and executive control of motivation and cognition with maturity. Activation in paralimbic posterior cingulate and inferior temporal brain regions of visual–spatial saliency processing was progressively reduced in activation with increasing development. Thus, with increasing development between adolescence and adulthood, reward appears to enhance maturing cognitive sustained attention and executive reward-processing networks, whilst reducing paralimbic regions of saliency detection. These findings may be the neural underpinnings for the progressive maturation of motivational control over risk taking behaviours between adolescence and adulthood.

Introduction

Executive functions are described as a set of neurocognitive processes which are essential for mature, adult goal-directed behaviour (Welsh and Pennington, 1988). These processes typically include functions such as working memory, inhibitory control, selective attention, cognitive flexibility, decision-making and temporal foresight (Funahashi, 2001). Although less frequently listed under executive functions, sustained attention is an important basis function for mature adult goal-directed behaviour. The maturation of sustained and selective attention processes is thought to emerge during adolescence, a developmental period of behavioural and cognitive transition (Anderson et al., 2001, Leon-Carrion et al., 2004, Luciana et al., 2005 and Luna et al., 2004). The attainment of competence in executive function as well as enhanced regulation of emotion and arousal in adulthood (Steinberg, 2005) reflects the development of resonant states between saliency driven bottom-up processes and top-down regulatory mechanisms (Grossberg, 1999).

These advances in cognitive performance are paralleled by neurobiological changes particularly within the frontal cortex between childhood and adulthood. Volumetric increases have been observed within dorsal (Giedd, 2004), medial and orbital frontal cortex (OFC) (Giedd, 2004 and Sowell et al., 1999), together with changes in the ratio of white to grey matter (Durston et al., 2001 and Sowell et al., 2003) well into middle age. These changes are most likely attributable to increased myelination and synaptic pruning (Blakemore and Choudhury, 2006).

The majority of functional imaging studies investigating the development of executive function have focussed on functions of inhibitory or cognitive control, such as motor and interference inhibition and cognitive flexibility. The most consistent findings show a linear increase in activation between childhood to mid-adulthood in task-relevant frontal, striatal and parietal brain regions during tasks of cognitive control, coupled with activation decreases in earlier developing posterior and subcortical brain regions (Adleman et al., 2002, Booth et al., 2005, Rubia et al., 2009, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2007 and Rubia et al., 2006). In addition, changes from more diffuse to more task-relevant focused regions of neural activation with age are also observed (Durston and Casey, 2006, Marsh et al., 2008 and Moses et al., 2002).

However, to our knowledge, no developmental functional imaging study has investigated developmental changes between childhood and adulthood during tasks of sustained attention. Sustained attention is a key executive function, thought to underpin so-called ‘higher’ attentional processes such as selective and divided attention as well as general cognitive ability (Sarter et al., 2001) and which continues to develop throughout childhood (Levy, 1980) and adolescence (Lin et al., 1999). Vigilance tasks of rapid visual information processing show activation in fronto-parietal and cerebellar regions (Lawrence et al., 2003) whilst auditory and visual tasks of sustained attention show activation in dorsolateral and inferior prefrontal cortices, in addition to striato-thalamic and parieto-temporal regions (Hager et al., 1998 and Voisin et al., 2006).. According to a meta-analysis (Corbetta and Shulman 2002), target detection paradigms requiring the identification of infrequent but predetermined targets from a stream of non-target stimuli, loading heavily upon sustained attention, yield networks of activation involving inferior and middle frontal gyri and the right temporo-parietal junction (TPJ). These findings have been supported by later studies (Bledowski et al., 2004 and Mulert et al., 2004). Whilst there are no developmental fMRI studies on sustained attention, a few developmental imaging studies have investigated the effect of age on selective attention functions within conflict inhibition or oddball tasks, finding linear activation increases with age in prefrontal, striatal and temporo-parietal brain regions (Adleman et al., 2002, Christakou et al., 2009 and Rubia et al., 2006). We therefore thought it was timely to investigate developmental brain function changes between childhood and adulthood during a sustained attention task.

Recent developmental theorists have outlined distinctions between “cool” cognitive executive functions such as attention and inhibition that are known to be mediated by lateral fronto-striatal and fronto-parietal networks and “hot” executive functions that involve incentives and motivation and are mediated by ventromedial OFC, midline, cingulate and limbic brain structures (Zelazo and Müller, 2002). Many tasks require varying ratios of both hot and cool executive functions and subsequent integration between these two domains. Motivation is particularly relevant for attention processes and motivation and attention are hence closely interrelated. For example, enhanced arousal states potentiate selective attention processes (Lang et al., 1990) and this effect is more pronounced with positive rather than negative reinforcement (Rothermund et al., 2001). fMRI studies have demonstrated that motivation or reward can enhance activation within brain regions that mediate arousal and selective attention such as ventrolateral PFC, parietal and posterior cingulate cortices (Lang et al., 1990, Mohanty et al., 2008, Rothermund et al., 2001 and Small et al., 2005).

Furthermore, performance data shows greater influence of reward upon cognition during childhood and adolescence. In adolescents compared with adults, sensitivity to reinforcement is enhanced (Tripp and Alsop, 1999) and immediate rather than future reward has more value (Crone and van der Molen, 2004 and Hooper et al., 2004). These findings are reflective of the behavioural style of adolescence (Arnett, 1992) where drug use (Silveri et al., 2004) and sensation-seeking behaviours (Martin et al., 2002) are highly prevalent, suggesting that cognitive and behavioural control over hypersensitive reward systems are less developed. Neuroimaging studies of immediate and anticipated monetary reward in healthy adults reveal the importance of OFC and ventromedial PFC (Christakou et al., 2009, Ernst et al., 2004, Kirsch et al., 2003, McClure et al., 2004 and Thut et al., 1997) dorsolateral PFC (Thut et al., 1997) and dorsal striatum (Delgado et al., 2003 and Knutson et al., 2000) in processing incentive. The behavioural style associated with youth may be attributable to a delay in the maturation of regulatory systems associated with these regions (Ernst et al., 2006 and Luna et al., 2001). In support of this, children with ADHD, a disorder thought to be associated with delayed maturation of fronto-striatal systems (Rubia, 2007 and Shaw et al., 2007), show decreased activation compared to healthy children in OFC and striatum during reward anticipation (Scheres et al., 2007) and reward delay (Rubia et al., 2009), whilst conversely, behaviourally inhibited adolescents show increased activation in striatum (Guyer et al., 2006).

Given the importance of motivation upon sustained attention functions, and the evidence for increased relevance of motivation on sustained attention in younger subjects compared to older subjects, we thought it was crucial to also investigate the effect of motivation on the developmental brain changes underlying sustained attention. For this purpose we scanned children and adults on a classical sustained attention task, a continuous performance task (CPT), which included rewarded and non-rewarded target trials, to assess the effect of reward on neural networks of sustained attention.

Developmental imaging studies of reward have largely focussed upon incentive in the context of probabilistic tasks of reward-related decision-making, and have typically focussed upon regions of interest such as ventrolateral and dorsolateral PFC, OFC, anterior cingulate cortex (ACC), nucleus accumbens and amygdala. The findings have been relatively inconsistent, with increasing age being associated with increased activation of ACC (Eshel et al., 2007); decreased (Cohen et al., 2010, Ernst et al., 2005 and Galvan et al., 2006) or increased (Bjork et al., 2004) activation of striatum; and increased activation of OFC (Eshel et al., 2007 and Galvan et al., 2006) although not consistently (Cohen et al., 2010 and van Leijenhorst et al., 2006); whilst dorsolateral PFC activation was found not to change throughout development (Eshel et al., 2007 and van Leijenhorst et al., 2006). These inconsistent findings may be attributable to the inclusion of decision-making processes within paradigms where task variations can influence which brain regions are recruited for performance (Manes et al., 2002).

In this study, we therefore aimed to explore three novel aspects of neurofunctional development: 1) the neurofunctional substrates of maturation of sustained attention between childhood and adulthood 2) the neurofunctional correlates associated with exposure to rewarding stimuli embedded within a task of sustained attention and 3) we wanted to do this by designing a task that involved deterministic, rather than probabilistic or judgement-related reward associations to eliminate confounding components of decision-making in the task. The use of reward within a sustained attention task should allow us to observe the influence of incentives during a relatively pure task of sustained attention rather than decision-making which is likely to load upon more complex processes such as judgement and confidence (Vickers, 1979). We chose the continuous performance task (CPT) since the processes involved in this task are known to be sensitive to the effects of reward, and in particular are known to show differential reward sensitivity for children and adults (Tomporowski and Tinsley, 1996).

Based upon the developmental fMRI findings on other executive and selective attention functions (Christakou et al., 2009, Courchesne, 1978, Oades et al., 1997, Rubia et al., 2009, Rubia et al., 2007, Rubia et al., 2006 and Taylor and Eals, 1996), we hypothesised that increasing age between adolescence and mid-adulthood would be associated with a linear increase in activation during sustained attention within lateral inferior prefrontal, temporo-parietal and cerebellar regions typically associated with attentional processing, with decreases expected in earlier developing posterior brain regions. Furthermore, we expected that the effect of reward on sustained attention trials would be associated with age-related increases in activation within executive reward-processing regions such as ventromedial and dorsolateral PFC and dorsal striatum, as well as potentiated linear increases with age within lateral fronto-striatal and cerebellar regions typically associated with sustained attention, suggestive of an age-related enhancement of attentional processes with increased motivation. We hypothesised that younger age, however, would be associated with greater activation within earlier developing posterior limbic and paralimbic reward and saliency processing networks with reduced activation within the regions associated with sustained attention.

Materials and methods

Subjects

Seventy volunteers took part in the study, including 35 right-handed adolescents (12 females and 23 males; mean age 14 and 10 months; st dev 2 years and 1 month; range 10–17 years) and 35 right-handed adults (12 females and 23 males; mean age 27 years; st dev 6 years and 1 month; range 18–43 years). The mean age of the total sample was 21 years (st dev 7 years and 6 months).

All subjects achieved a score above the 10th percentile on the Ravens Standard Progressive Matrices, a non-verbal test of intellectual achievement (Raven et al., 1998), which is equivalent to an IQ estimate of 80. Statistical comparisons of both age groups revealed no significant difference of variance (F = 1.1; p = 0.29) or mean (t = 1.7; p = 0.10). There were no significant between-group differences for gender in Raven's Standard Progressive Matrices scores (Converted mean IQ estimate: males: 107.3 (13); females: 107.7 (12); t = 0.10, p = 0.91).

Exclusion criteria were current or past substance abuse, head injury, mental retardation, or mental or neurological disorder.

All participants were paid £30 for participating (£8 of which was a monetary reward associated with performance) and written informed consent/assent and approval from the local Ethical Committee was obtained.

Rewarded continuous performance task

The 6-minute task was explained to the participants and practiced once before scanning. It was then presented to the subjects in the scanner via a mirror from a liquid crystal diode projector and a keypad was used for recording responses. This task involved a rapid, randomised mixed trial, event-related fMRI design with jittered inter-stimulus intervals of either 800, 900 or 1000 ms. A serial stream of 416 letters was presented, composed of non-target letters (A, B, C, D, E, F, G, H, K, L, M and N) appearing with a frequency of 7.1–7.5% and 24 target letters ‘X’ and 24 target letters ‘O’ each appearing with a slightly reduced frequency of 5.8%, Each letter had a presentation time of 300 ms and each of the 24 target letters ‘X’ and ‘O’ were separated in the stream by at least 5400 ms and at most 9000 ms. Subjects were asked to respond when they saw a target letter using the keypad with their right hand and to withhold responses for any other letter. Half the subjects were told they would be rewarded for responding to the letter X and the other half to the letter O, with the alternative letter remaining non-rewarded. Next to the letter frame two feedback bars appeared on screen at all times with ascending panels numbered from 1 to 8, one of which indicated the accumulation of responses to X (coloured red) and the other, the number of responses to O (coloured blue). These feedback bars would flash for each correct response to their associated letter and on every third successful hit would move upwards to fill a panel with colour. In the case of the rewarded feedback bar each filled panel signalled to the participant that they had made three successful responses to a rewarded letter and had won a £1; in the case of the non-rewarded feedback bar this signalled to the participant only that they had made three successful responses to the non-rewarded letter with no associated winnings. It was emphasised to the subject which feedback panel informed them of the accumulation of their monetary reward (as well as providing feedback) and which bar gave them feedback about the number of successful non-rewarded targets. Since there were 24 rewarded target trials there was a maximum of £8 to win and regardless of their expected winnings, all participants were given this sum at the end of the scanning session (Rubia et al., 2009 and Schmitz et al., 2008).

Analysis

Task performance

The task dependent variables included reaction times for non-rewarded and rewarded target trials, the reward reaction time (RT) effect (differences in reaction times for non-rewarded and rewarded target trials); and percentage of omission and commission errors. For reaction times, the effect of age was tested statistically with a three-way mixed ANOVA with age and gender as between-subjects variables and trial type as a within-subjects variable, and with IQ as a covariate. Partial correlations (controlling for IQ) were computed for age and RTs to non-rewarded and rewarded targets as well the reward RT effect using the Pearson product moment correlation coefficient. Given that omission and commission errors were infrequent, they lacked normal distribution and thus non-parametric tests were used. The effect of trial type on these dependent variables was tested using Wilcoxon non-parametric tests, whilst the effects of age group and gender were tested using Mann Whitney non-parametric tests. Correlational analyses of omission and commission errors and age were performed using Spearman's Rho.

fMRI data analysis

During the task, functional images were acquired on a 1.5 T GE Neuro-optimised Signa LX Horizon System (General Electric, Milwaukee, WI, USA), using a gradient echo planar sequence sensitive to blood oxygenation level dependent (BOLD) contrast. In each of 16 non-contiguous planes parallel to the anterior–posterior commissure, 208 T2*-weighted MR images depicting BOLD contrast were acquired with TE = 40 ms; TR = 1.8 s; flip angle 90°; 64 × 64 matrix; in-plane voxel size 3.75 × 3.75 mm; slice thickness = 7 mm, slice-skip = 0.7 mm.

The fMRI data were analysed with the XBAM software developed at the Institute of Psychiatry (c.f. http://brainmap.it). This method of analysis makes no normality assumptions, which are usually violated in fMRI data, but instead uses median statistics to control outlier effects and permutation rather than normal theory based inference. Furthermore the most common test statistic is computed by standardising for individual difference in residual noise before embarking on second level, multi-subject testing using robust permutation-based methods. This allows a mixed effects approach to analysis—an approach that has recently been recommended following a detailed analysis of the validity and impact of normal theory based inference in fMRI in large number of subjects (Thirion et al., 2007).

Individual analysis

fMRI data were realigned to minimise motion-related artefacts (Bullmore et al., 1999a), and smoothed using a Gaussian filter (full-width half maximum, 8.8 mm). Time–series analysis of individual subject activation was performed using wavelet-based re-sampling method previously described (Bullmore et al., 2001). Briefly, we first convolved each experimental condition with two Poisson model functions (delays of 4 and 8 s). We then calculated the weighted sum of these two convolutions that gave the best fit (least-squares) to the time series at each voxel. A goodness-of-fit statistic (the SSQ ratio) was then computed at each voxel consisting of the ratio of the sum of squares of deviations from the mean intensity value due to the model (fitted time series) divided by the sum of squares due to the residuals (original time series minus model time series). The appropriate null distribution for assessing significance of any given SSQ ratio was established using the wavelet-based data re-sampling method (Bullmore et al., 2001) and applying the model-fitting process to the re-sampled data. This process was repeated 20 times at each voxel and the data combined over all voxels, resulting in 20 null parametric maps of SSQ ratio for each subject, which were combined to give the overall null distribution of SSQ ratio. The same permutation strategy was applied at each voxel to preserve spatial correlation structure in the data. Activated voxels, at a < 1 level of Type I error, were identified through the appropriate critical value of the SSQ ratio from the null distribution. The first contrast involved subtracting activation associated with non-target trials from non-rewarded target trials; then activation associated with this first contrast was subtracted from reward trials.

Group analysis

A generic group activation map was then produced for each condition of the rewarded CPT task. The observed and permuted SSQ ratio maps for each individual as well as BOLD effect size maps were transformed into standard space (Talairach and Tournoux, 1988) using the two stage warping procedure described in detail elsewhere (Brammer et al., 1997). This involves first computing the average image intensity map for each individual over the course of the experiment. The transformations required to map this image to the structural scan for each individual and then from “structural space” to the Talairach template are then computed by maximizing the correlation between the images at each stage. The SSQ ratio and BOLD effect size maps are then transformed into Talairach space using these transformations.

White matter regions were extracted from our analysis using the BET tool from the FSL software package (Smith, 2002). This creates a grey matter mask of the Talairach template used for normalisation. This mask was subsequently used to restrict the analysis to those voxels lying within grey matter.

A generic activation group map was produced for each experimental condition (non-rewarded target trials versus versus non-target trials; rewarded target trials versus non-target trials; and rewarded v non-rewarded target trials) by calculating the median observed SSQ ratio over all subjects at each voxel in standard space and testing them against the null distribution of median SSQ ratios computed from the identically transformed wavelet re-sampled data (Brammer et al., 1997). The voxel-level threshold was first set to 0.04 to give maximum sensitivity and to avoid Type II errors. Next, a cluster-level threshold was computed for the resulting 3D voxel clusters such that the final expected number of Type I error clusters was < 1 per whole brain. The necessary combination of voxel and cluster-level thresholds was not assumed from theory but rather was determined by direct permutation for each data set, giving excellent Type II error control (Bullmore et al., 1999a). Instead of relying on asymptotic distributions such as t or F that assume data normality, we use data-driven, permutation-based methods with minimal distributional assumptions that have been shown to be more suitable for fMRI data analysis in these kind of cluster sizes (Zhang et al., 2009). Cluster mass rather than a cluster extent threshold was used to minimise discrimination against possible small, strongly responding foci of activation (Bullmore et al., 1999a). Briefly, a voxel-wide significance threshold was set (p < 0.04), and surviving voxels were assembled into 3D clusters using a contiguity criterion. The mass of each cluster was calculated by adding the statistical values of all cluster members and thresholded at p < 0.02. Less than one false positive activation cluster was expected for p < 0.04 at voxel level and p < 0.02 at cluster level.

Whole-brain correlations between brain activation and age across all subjects

To test for a linear correlation between whole-brain activation for the contrast of non-reward trials versus non-target trials; rewarded target trials versus non-target trials and reward versus non-reward trials and age, the Pearson product moment correlation coefficient was first computed at each voxel in standard space between age data and signal change over all subjects. The correlation coefficients were recalculated after randomly permuting the ages but not the fMRI data. Repeating the second step many times (1000 times per voxel) gives the distribution of correlation coefficients under the null hypothesis that there is no association between specific ages and specific BOLD effects. This null distribution can then be used to assess the probability of any particular correlation coefficient under the null hypothesis. The critical value of the correlation coefficient at any desired Type I error level in the original (non-permuted) data could be determined by reference to this distribution. Statistical analysis was extended to cluster level as described previously (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Given the wide range of IQ scores, this variable was included in the analysis as a covariate.

For all experimental contrasts, information can be obtained about the size, and also the direction of the activation from the general linear model fit to the time series of activation. The sign of the BOLD response can either be positive or negative with respect to the regressor (the implicit baseline in this case). We were careful to consider the possibility of negative BOLD signal change by examining sign of the signal change relative to the regressor. For all analyses only BOLD responses were considered where the average SSQ ratio in response to the activation condition was positive.

In order to examine whether the clusters of activation significantly correlated with age were associated with performance, standardised BOLD response values (SSQ ratios) were extracted from each cluster for both the non-reward/non-target and reward/non-reward contrasts. Pearson's product moment correlation coefficients were calculated for the SSQ ratios for the linear activation changes for the contrast between non-reward and non-target trials and RTs to non-rewarded targets. Spearman's rank correlation coefficients were calculated for these SSQs and omission errors to non-rewarded targets as well as overall commission errors.

Pearson's product moment correlation coefficients were also calculated for the contrast of rewarded targets versus non-rewarded targets and performance as measured by RTs to rewarded targets, and the difference between rewarded and non-rewarded target RTs. Spearman's rank correlation coefficients were calculated for these SSQs and omission errors to rewarded targets, the difference between omission errors for rewarded and non-rewarded targets as well as overall commission errors.

ANOVA for sex differences in brain activation

Analysis of variance was conducted for between-group differences using a randomisation-based test for voxel- and cluster-wise activation differences (Bullmore et al., 1999b) and including IQ as a covariate. Less than one false positive activation cluster was expected at p < 0.05 at voxel level and p < 0.01 at cluster level. First, the difference between the mean SSQ ratio values in each group was calculated at each voxel. The mean ratio was then recalculated 1000 times at each voxel following random permutation of group membership and the difference in SSQ ratios was calculated after each permutation. The same set of random numbers was employed for the permutation at each voxel to preserve spatial correlations in each permuted map. The probability of the original SSQ ratio difference under the null hypothesis of no effect of group membership is the number of times we observed an SSQ ratio difference as large as or larger than the original difference during the permutation process, divided by the total number of permutations. If this value exceeded our threshold for voxel-level activation, activated voxels were used to identify activation clusters, which were subjected to cluster analysis.

Results

Task performance

The examination of age differences on reaction times found a significant main effect of target type (F = 4.5; p = 0.037), a significant effect of IQ (F = 6.7; p = 0.01) but no effect for age group (F = 1.0; p = 0.31); no significant effect of gender; but an interaction between age group and target type which approached significance (F = 3.1; p = 0.08). Post hoc tests showed that adolescents were slower to respond to non-rewarded targets than adults (t = 2.4; p = 0.018) and also that there was a significant difference between non-rewarded and rewarded trials for adolescents (t = 2.3; p = 0.03) (see Table 1 and Fig. 1). No other differences were significant. There were no significant correlations between age and reaction times to non-rewarded and rewarded targets individually. However, there was a negative correlation between age and the difference between reaction times for rewarded and non-rewarded target trials which approached significance (r = − 0.21; df; 68; p = 0.09), suggesting that the effect of reward on reaction times was more pronounced in younger subjects.

Brain activation differences

Motion

No significant between-group differences were observed in the extent of 3-dimensional motion during task performance as measured by voxels. The greatest motion observed was 1.29 voxels on the Z axis, which was an outlier. When this subject was excluded from the analyses, maximum motion was 0.67 voxels for x axis, 0.24 for y and 0.39 for Z axis and only very minor changes in cluster size. Consequently, this subject remained in the analysis.

Contrast of non-rewarded target versus non-target trials

For the contrast of non-rewarded target trials versus non-target trials across all subjects, significant activation differences were observed in bilateral temporal cortex, supramarginal gyri, cerebellum, cuneus and putamen (see Table 2, Fig. 2).

Contrast of rewarded target versus non-rewarded target trials

Across all subjects, significant clusters of activation difference were observed for this contrast within anterior and posterior cingulate cortices, and left middle temporal and middle and superior occipital cortices (see Table 2, Fig. 2).

The whole-brain correlation analysis between age and activation during this contrast showed significant linear increases in activation with age within bilateral dorsolateral and inferior PFC, left ventromedial OFC, left SMA, left anterior insula and striatum, right brain-stem and thalamus, left middle temporal and occipital cortices and bilateral cerebellum. Significant decreases were observed in right putamen, left inferior temporal gyrus and left posterior cingulate (see Table 3, Fig. 3).

Both of these whole-brain correlational analyses between age and activation were potentially confounded by differences in reaction time (and therefore processing time). In order to ensure that this variable was not a confounder, both analyses were carried out again, including reaction time as a covariate. There were minor changes in cluster size, but no differences in actual cluster locations.

It is possible that linear changes with age across the age range of 10 to 43 years were driven by maturational changes in adolescence. Therefore, in order to test whether increases in brain activation with age were disproportionately driven by the younger participants, we used the Fisher r–Z transformation to compare the correlation coefficients of the adolescent and adult subgroups of our sample for each cluster identified in the two whole-brain correlation analyses. There were no significant differences in correlation coefficients between the two subgroups, suggesting that the linear changes are not associated disproportionally with adolescence.

In order to test whether the observed linear changes in activation could be better explained by non-linear models, we compared quadratic and cubic with linear functions to test whether they better explained the relationship between SSQs and age by examining the change in R2 when each term was added to the linear regression model. No clusters in either contrast could be better explained by polynomial models.

The examination of relationships between SSQ ratios for activation for both contrasts and performance variables for each age group, revealed a correlation between activation within lateral putamen for the contrast of rewarded target trials and non-rewarded target trials which approached significance (r = 0.316; p = 0.065) but only for the sample of children and adolescents. This was not significant after controlling for multiple testing.

Discussion

This study investigated linear age-related changes between adolescence and mid-adulthood in brain activation differences during a sustained attention task and the age-associated effects of reward upon this attention network. Performance data showed an interaction which approached significance and post hoc tests showed that adolescents were significantly slower than adults to non-rewarded sustained attention target trials, but more sensitive to incentives, so that this difference was normalised in the reward condition. As hypothesised, the whole-brain correlation analysis between age and activation for the sustained attention contrast of non-rewarded target with non-target trials showed linear increases in activation with age in typical brain regions associated with sustained attention of right-hemispheric lateral inferior frontal, superior temporal and inferior parietal cortices with decreases in linear activation with age in earlier developing limbic and paralimbic medial temporal, posterior insular and posterior cingulate regions known to be important for saliency detection. The findings extend evidence for progressive age-correlated development between child and adulthood in task-relevant fronto-parieto-temporal systems of cognitive control to the attention domain. Reward further enhanced the age-dependent activation increases observed in the non-rewarded sustained attention regions in inferior frontal, temporal and cerebellar brain regions and elicited additional activation increases within top-down executive attention and motivation control areas such as dorsolateral and ventromedial orbital PFC and dorsal striatum. As predicted, with increasing age, decreases in activation were observed in inferior temporal cortex and posterior cingulate, associated with reward and saliency processing. The findings show that incentives have age-dependent effects on the development of attention networks, increasing activation in developing attention control and executive reward-processing regions, whilst decreasing paralimbic networks of visual–spatial attention to motivational saliency.

Sustained attention

Performance in the sustained attention task increased with age into early adulthood as shown by significantly faster reaction times to target trials in adults compared to adolescents, which is in line with previous findings (Fuchigami et al., 1993 and Johnstone et al., 1996). Mirroring the findings of behavioural attention maturation, increased activation with age during the sustained attention contrast was observed in areas known to be important for this function such as inferior PFC and SMA, the superior temporal/inferior parietal junction and anterior cerebellum. Right inferior frontal, SMA and temporo-parietal activation is typically associated with target detection processing, regardless of modality and task requirement (Ardekani et al., 2002, Clark et al., 2000, Corbetta and Shulman, 2002, Gur et al., 2007, Kiehl et al., 2001, Kirino et al., 2000 and Madden et al., 2004) and with tasks loading on sustained attention (Lawrence et al., 2003, Lewin et al., 1996 and Voisin et al., 2006). The cerebellum is an essential part of fronto-cerebellar networks and in particular the later developing anterior cerebellum has been shown to be crucially implicated in attention functions (Allen et al., 2005, Bonnet et al., 2009, Gross et al., 2002 and Martin-Soelch et al., 2001).

These findings of activation increases with development in task-relevant inferior fronto-temporo-parieto-cerebellar regions that mediate sustained attention extend evidence for the continued functional development of these regions throughout childhood to mid-adulthood in cognitive control processes (Bunge et al., 2002, Christakou et al., 2009, Durston et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2007, Rubia et al., 2006 and Tamm et al., 2002) to the domain of sustained attention. The findings are also in line with ERP findings of developmental enhancement of frontal and parietal P3b components during attentional processes (Courchesne, 1978, Mueller et al., 2008 and Taylor and Eals, 1996) and with our recent findings of enhanced fronto-temporo-parietal activation with age during an oddball task measuring selective attention (Rubia et al., 2010).

As hypothesized, significant decreases in activation for the sustained attention contrast were observed within earlier developing posterior brain regions including medial temporal cortex, posterior cingulate, hippocampus, posterior insula and posterior cerebellum. Increased activation within these posterior cortical and subcortical brain regions has been found in younger compared with older subjects during tasks of cognitive control (Booth et al., 2003, Christakou et al., 2009, Durston et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2007 and Rubia et al., 2006). Posterior cingulate, hippocampus and insula form part of the limbic system and visuo-motor pathways and are an interface between motivation and spatial attention (Mohanty et al., 2008). Whilst, the posterior cingulate has consistently been shown to mediate the dynamic allocation of visual–spatial attention to salience (Ardekani et al., 2002, Clark et al., 2000, Gur et al., 2007, Kiehl et al., 2001, Mesulam et al., 2001, Mohanty et al., 2008 and Small et al., 2003), the hippocampus has been shown to contribute to selective visual attention to targets (Wu and Guo 1999). In conclusion, together with medial and inferior temporal regions (Ardekani et al., 2002, Bledowski et al., 2004 and Mesulam et al., 2001), these brain areas are known to be involved in bottom-up visual–spatial attention processes.

The activation of posterior temporo-limbic, paralimbic and cerebellar regions in younger subjects during sustained attention may hence reflect a compensatory, immature and less effective attentional system triggering more strongly bottom-up visual–spatial attentional saliency processing and less likely to activate mature adult top-down fronto-parieto-temporal executive attention control system (Rubia et al., 2009 and Rubia et al., 2006). Thus performance is less planned in younger people and more determined by the interest value of the targets, in contrast with more mature performances reflective of a more strategic, top-down approach associated with adult performance where targets have less interest value or saliency and but are processed by attentional networks.

The impact of reward upon sustained attention

The developmental analysis of performance showed that younger subjects are more sensitive to the effects of reward than older subjects as evidenced by a decrease in latency of reaction time for rewarded compared with non-rewarded targets, which was not observed in adults (see Table 1, Fig. 1). This finding could imply increased stability and control of performance with age, which is already optimal and less influenced by reward in adults, whilst being suboptimal and more susceptible to motivation effects in adolescence and adolescence. This is supported by the imaging findings of increased activation differences during reward with age of the same regions that were activated during non-rewarded sustained attention, such as right inferior PFC, SMA and anterior cerebellum. This suggests that reward amplifies activation within regions associated with sustained attention. This enhancing effect of reward on cognitive networks has been observed in adults during selective attention (Mohanty et al., 2008 and Small et al., 2005) working memory (Krawczyk et al., 2007 and Pochon et al., 2002) and motor response preparation (Ramnani and Miall 2003) and is in line with models which propose that reward impacts upon cognition via increased dopamine in PFC and basal ganglia, leading to increased cognitive control and signal-to-noise ratio, thus enhancing performance (Ashby et al., 1999, Cohen et al., 2002 and Volkow et al., 2007).

Furthermore, reward also enhanced activation in additional brain areas of right dorsolateral frontal cortices, ventromedial OFC and putamen. Whilst some of these regions, in particular dorsolateral PFC and putamen have also been associated with sustained attention (Lawrence et al., 2003, Lewin et al., 1996 and Voisin et al., 2006), others, such as the left ventromedial OFC, but also dorsolateral PFC have been associated with executive reward processes (Christakou et al., 2009, Hare et al., 2008 and Kringelbach, 2005).

Both ventrolateral (Bjork et al., 2004, Christakou et al., 2009, O'Doherty et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2009, Thut et al., 1997 and Zalla et al., 2000) and dorsolateral PFC (Bjork et al., 2004, Critchley et al., 2001, Leon and Shadlen, 1999 and Thut et al., 1997) have been associated with executive reward processing and are thought to have highly intregrated roles during cognitive tasks which contain an affective or rewarding component (Baker et al., 1997 and Gray et al., 2002). In fact, dorsolateral PFC has been proposed as the site of emotion–cognition integration (Gray et al., 2002) reflected in the way dorso- and ventrolateral PFC are interconnected (Haber et al., 2006). The findings of a positive correlation between orbitofrontal brain regions and age for rewarded target trials are in line with evidence for reduced activation in children in these areas during other incentive processing tasks (Eshel et al., 2007 and Galvan et al., 2006).

The specific region of increase in activation within dorsal striatum observed here is located medially in contrast to the more laterally located region of putamen shown to decrease with age in response to reward. It is postulated that lateral dorsal striatum has a motor function whilst medial dorsal striatum has a stronger role in attentional processes, reward-associated learning and strategy selection (Adams et al., 2001 and Pisa and Cyr, 1990). It is possible that the reward-related enhancement in reaction time speed in younger subjects may have been mediated by the lateral putamen activation increase, whilst enhanced activation in medial striatum is likely to be part of the ventromedial OFC–striatal systems of executive motivation processes (Christakou et al., 2009, Hare et al., 2008 and Kringelbach, 2005). This is supported by the modest correlation observed in children and adolescents between lateral putamen activation and the difference in reaction time during rewarded and non-rewarded target trials.

In conclusion, it appears that reward enhances the regulation of fronto-temporo-cerebellar brain regions that mediate sustained attention in a progressive way between adolescence and adulthood, presumably further stabilizing attention performance with increasing age, as well as amplifying dorsolateral and ventromedial fronto-striatal executive reward-processing areas that integrate reward with cognitive processes. A negative association with age was observed during the rewarded contrast for activation within posterior cingulate, inferior temporal gyrus, and lateral putamen. The finding of increased posterior cingulate activation in younger participants with reward echoes the findings for the enhanced activation in this region during the sustained attention contrast, suggesting that reward amplifies activation within existing posterior paralimbic bottom-up visual–spatial saliency and target detection networks in younger participants. There is evidence that motivational states increase the salience of stimuli (Kelley and Berridge 2002) via the activation of limbic dopaminergic systems (Montague et al., 2004 and Volkow et al., 2007). Thus there appears to be an age-dependent dissociative effect of reward upon sustained attention substrates, enhancing bottom-up paralimbic regions associated with saliency detection in adolescents, whilst amplifying top-down executive attention and reward-processing networks in adults.

This process of reward-related enhancement of paralimbic areas of visual–spatial attention to saliency in adolescents compared with adults may reflect a greater behavioural impact of reward on motivational salience in younger people. In fact, this is supported by the performance data which showed that reward influenced reaction times in adolescents but not adults. Thus, in adult performance, a mature sustained attention network appears to be fully operational, even though susceptible to potentiation with reward, resulting in an effective and stable performance across both rewarded and non-rewarded conditions. In contrast, during adolescence and adolescence, attentional performance appears to be driven and enhanced by bottom-up visual–spatial saliency detection processes, which, compared with adults, are more likely to be subject to change according to the degree of motivational saliency, with resulting performance changes.

Lastly, the analysis of gender revealed increased activation differences in temporo-occipital, posterior cingulate and cerebellar cortices in females compared with males during the sustained attention contrast, supporting previous findings of enhanced activation in females in these regions during cognitive control and selective attention tasks (Christakou et al., 2009, Garavan et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2009 and Weiss et al., 2006).

Limitations

The strength of this study is the unprecedented investigation of developmental processes of reward within a cognitive attention task, the relatively large sample size and the use of permutation fMRI data analyses across the whole brain. A limitation of this study is that information on puberty was not available which would have been useful for elucidating more complex patterns of functional development.

Although our data allowed us to show that adding incentive to an attention task enhances activation in the neural substrates of attention functions in an age-dependent way, future research could address the question of an age-dependent upregulation of reward of attention-related activation and functional regional interconnectivity between key attention regions more thoroughly with the use of dynamic causal modelling (DCM).

It is surprising that there were no differences in omission errors between children and adolescents. However, performance differences are not always observed alongside differences in brain function in developmental studies. Often there are differences in strategy between adults and children and tasks may be more difficult or effortful for younger participants which may be unobservable within external task scores but can be reflected in differences in brain activation. Furthermore, a lack of performance differences with age is in fact advantageous as it shows that developmental changes are not due to the confound of poorer performance in children, but reflect true activation differences between age groups.

In this study design we were interested in the enhancement of attention activation by both anticipation and obtainment of reward in a task design which does not distinguish between anticipatory and outcome related reward processes. It would be interesting in future studies to disentangle the effects of anticipation of reward (i.e. attentional saliency) and actual reward outcome on attention networks, as well as the differential impact of reward value upon executive function throughout development.

Finally, the analysis of gender differences, although interesting, can only be considered exploratory as the numbers of males and females were not matched which may have biased the analysis.

Conclusion

In conclusion, the findings of this study suggest that reward has an age-dependent impact on sustained attention networks. Regions that change significantly in their activation with development during task performance, either positively or negatively, are further potentiated in their activation changes by the effect of reward. Hence, throughout development, reward amplifies the developmental activation increases of mature top-down fronto-striato-parietal attention-processing regions as well as enhances dorsolateral and ventromedial executive reward-processing systems, presumably leading to more effective integration of motivation and cognition. With decreasing age, however, reward seems to amplify earlier developing, more primitive paralimbic bottom-up visual–spatial saliency detection regions.

These findings may elucidate the neural underpinnings for the increased risk taking behaviours in adolescence. They show that in adolescence top-down executive attention control and reward-processing systems are less mature and may consequently be less able to inhibit hyper-responsive paralimbic systems that respond to motivationally salient events. This underdeveloped regulation between an immature top-down executive motivation control system and a hyper-responsive bottom-up system of registering motivational salience would lead to reduced inhibitory control over incoming motivational signals in the environment and the resulting behavioural impulses triggered by them.
