A developmental examination of gender differences in brain engagement during evaluation of threat

Received 19 May 2003, Revised 4 February 2004, and 4 February 2004, Accepted 12 February 2004, Available online 2 April 2004

Abstract

Background

Females appear to be more sensitive and responsive to social cues, including threat signals, than are males. Recent theoretical models suggest that developmental changes in brain functioning play important roles in the emergence of such gender differences.

Methods

We used functional magnetic resonance imaging to examine developmental and gender differences in activation of neural structures thought to mediate attention to emotional faces depicting varying degrees of threat. Analyses focused on the orbitofrontal cortex, amygdala, and anterior cingulate cortex during the evaluation of threat conveyed by faces. Healthy adolescents (n = 17; 53% male) and adults (n = 17; 53% male) were scanned while they rated how threatening pictures of neutral and emotional (angry, fearful, or happy) faces appeared.

Results

Results indicate significant interactions among age, gender, and face type for activation during explicit threat monitoring. In particular, adult women activated orbitofrontal cortex and amygdala selectively to unambiguous threat (angry) cues, while adult men showed a less discriminating pattern of activation. No gender differences were evident for adolescents, who as a group resembled adult males.

Conclusions

These findings suggest that there are gender differences in patterns of neural responses to emotional faces that are not fully apparent until adulthood.

Numerous studies report gender differences in perceiving, interpreting, and reacting to social cues that signify the potential presence of interpersonal threats. Across development, females appear to be especially sensitive and responsive, both behaviorally and physiologically, to such cues (Bradley et al., 1999, Galen and Underwood, 1997, Goos and Silverman, 2002, Hall, 1978, McClure, 2000, McManis et al., 2001, Morris-Prather et al., 1996 and Stroud et al., 2002). Not only do they tend to perform more accurately than males when labeling others' feelings, such as anger or hostility, based on external cues such as facial expressions (Hall, 1978 and McClure, 2000), but also they show greater increases in skin conductance and a tendency toward stronger startle potentiation to threatening scenes (e.g., a knife-wielding attacker) (Bradley et al., 1999 and McManis et al., 2001). Although such gender differences in social perception and behavior are present throughout the life span, they appear to be largest after the transition from adolescence to adulthood (Hall, 1978, McClure, 2000 and Nelson et al., 2002).

Traditionally, research on gender differences in the development of social perception and behavior has examined how environmental experiences, such as socialization by adults and peers (Brody and Hall, 1993 and Garner et al., 1997), relate directly to distinctive male and female perceptual and behavioral patterns across the life span. Recently, however, more integrative theoretical models that implicate the dynamic interplay between experiential and biological factors in the development of gender differences have gained currency (Lytton and Romney, 1991, Maccoby, 1998 and McClure, 2000). With the rise of these models has come increasing awareness that developmental changes in brain functioning play important roles in the emergence of gender differences in social perception and behavior.

Despite recent efforts to integrate neural as well as experiential factors into models of gender differences in social perceptual and behavioral development, neural factors remain poorly understood. In particular, little research has examined developmental differences in brain function that may relate to the emergence of gender-linked patterns of social threat processing. In part, this lack of knowledge reflects the difficulty of applying biologically based techniques to the study of complex social thoughts and actions, particularly across development. Noninvasive neuroimaging techniques such as functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), however, provide a promising approach for examining the neurobiology of developmental gender differences in perception of and response to cues that signify potential social threat.

Of the many visual cues that can signal social threat, facial expressions have been the most widely studied with neuroimaging techniques. Using fMRI and other approaches, researchers have identified a set of structures encompassing the orbitofrontal cortex (OFC), amygdala, and anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) that participates consistently in attentive processing of emotional faces (Adolphs 2002). Preliminary evidence suggests that male subjects and female subjects engage these and other structures in different ways while passively viewing emotional faces, some of which signal anger or social threat (Kesler/West et al., 2001, Killgore and Yurgelun-Todd, 2001 and Lee et al., 2002). No published studies, however, have examined such gender-specific patterns of activation across development from adolescence to adulthood. These prior results inform the current study, which is designed to examine gender differences in activation within the OFC, ACC, and amygdala across adolescence.

No studies have considered the role of attention in relationships among gender, development, and brain function during threat cue processing. Behavioral research findings indicate that adults' cognitive-affective responses to socially threatening stimuli vary depending on whether their attention is focused on arousing/emotional aspects of the stimuli (Ayduk et al 2002). Similarly, neuroimaging research demonstrates that patterns of neural activation differ, depending on whether or not adult subjects attend to emotional features of visual stimuli (Lane et al., 1997, Lange et al., 2003, Monk et al., 2003 and Pessoa et al., 2002). Some evidence suggests that gender moderates associations between attention and processing of threat cues, at least at a behavioral level. For example, attentional priming can differentially influence adult male and female perceptions of ambiguous threat cues, such that certain emotionally salient priming conditions increase threat ratings in women but not in men (Mussweiler and Förster 2000). However, no studies have considered whether gender moderates associations between attention to emotionally salient stimulus features and neural engagement and how this moderating role may vary across development.

We used fMRI to examine gender differences in neural activation patterns in adolescents and adults during a face-viewing task that explicitly directed subjects' attention to threat cues. In accordance with Spear (2000), we define adolescence broadly as a period of plasticity that is characterized by multiple, gradual physiologic and sociobehavioral transitions, rather than by any discrete event, such as the onset of puberty. The temporal boundaries of this period are widely debated; some research includes the entire second decade of life. We therefore elected to include subjects that spanned a relatively wide age range.

We focused on adolescents versus adults for several reasons. First, social relations begin to become more intimate and salient during adolescence, particularly for females (Berndt, 1996, Buhrmester, 1990 and Csikszentmihalyi and Larson, 1984). Second, during adolescence, strong gender differences emerge in vulnerability to emotional disorders such as depression and social phobia, which are partly characterized by elevated sensitivity to social threat (Merikangas et al., 2002 and Nolen-Hoeksema, 1990). Third, adolescence represents a period of important changes in the biological substrates of gender. In particular, adolescent secretion of gonadal steroids is associated with activation of many previously quiescent gender differences in organization of brain and behavior (Kelly et al., 1999 and Rubin et al., 1981). Thus, adolescence represents a period in which gender differences at both behavioral and neural levels are emergent and unlikely to be fully fixed and formed. During adulthood, in contrast, these differences should be maximally expressed. By comparing groups in these two stages, we hope to obtain insight into how gender differences evolve across development.

This study provides a developmentally oriented examination of gender differences in brain activation during explicit evaluation of social cues that signal unambiguous threat (angry faces), ambiguous or indirect presence of threat (fearful faces, neutral faces) (Whalen et al 1998), or the unambiguous absence of threat (happy faces). We tested two hypotheses, both of which focus on activity in specific brain regions implicated in emotional responding to facial stimuli. First, given the evidence that women are more responsive than men to threat stimuli, we hypothesized that females would show more activation than would males in the OFC, amygdala, and ACC, structures that have been identified as critical to the attentive processing of faces that convey threat and other emotional cues. More specifically, we predicted that gender differences would be especially apparent in response to faces connoting unambiguous threat relative to ambiguous or nonthreatening faces. Our prediction that threat cues would lead to increased activation is based on findings from prior studies of affective picture viewing (e.g., Lane et al., 1997 and Lange et al., 2003). We note, however, that studies of other processes have yielded evidence of reduced activation (e.g., Ungerleider 1995). Limited data exist on which to base hypotheses regarding gender differences in patterns of activation to threat; however, in light of data suggesting that negatively valenced scenes, some of which depict unambiguously threatening situations, evoke more activation in relevant regions than neutral scenes in women but not men (e.g., Wrase et al 2003), we anticipated gender differences to be strongest in response to unambiguous threat cues. Second, in light of evidence that gender differences in some behavioral responses to emotional cues may not fully emerge until adulthood (Hall, 1978 and McClure, 2000), we hypothesized that gender differences in patterns of neural activation to threat-related cues would be more pronounced in adults than in adolescents.

Methods and materials

Participants

Seventeen healthy adults (8 women) aged 25 to 36 years and 17 healthy adolescents (8 girls) aged 9 to 17 years participated in the study. Adolescent boys and girls did not differ in age, t(15) = .45, p = .66 (see Table 1 for mean ages by group). Data from three additional adults and six additional adolescents were excluded from the analysis because of problems with subject task performance, excessive motion, or technical problems during the scan. All participants were healthy as determined by physical examination and standardized structured psychiatric interview. Additionally, all participants scored within the average/above average range on the Wechsler Abbreviated Scale of Intelligence (WASI) ( Wechsler 1999), a screening measure of cognitive functioning (adolescent mean = 117.4 ± 12.1; adult mean = 113.2 ± 10.4). Each subject and a parent of each adolescent gave written informed assent/consent to participate in the study, which was approved by the National Institute of Mental Health Institutional Review Board.

Tasks

Participants viewed a series of 32 adult faces (8 happy, 8 angry, 8 fearful, 8 neutral) and rated how threatening each face appeared (How hostile?) on a five-point scale. This task was interleaved with three other tasks, during which participants were instructed either to passively view the faces, to rate their internal emotional responses to the faces (How afraid are you?), or to rate the size of a nonthreatening facial feature (How wide is the nose?). Only the threat-rating task is discussed in the current article, because we wanted to constrain contrasts to conditions in which subjects explicitly evaluated threat levels. Data from the other three tasks are presented in Monk et al (2003). No gender differences emerged on any of these tasks.

The four tasks were integrated into one 160-trial run, which was divided into four 40-trial epochs (32 face trials, and to facilitate data analysis, 8 trials with fixation points). Epochs consisted of four blocks (one for each task); epoch and block order were randomized across participants. During each block, 8 of the 32 faces and 2 fixation trials were presented in random order (Figure 1). Thus, within an epoch, each block comprised 10 trials (8 faces, 2 fixations) from one of the four tasks. For each block, instructions were presented for 3000 milliseconds and each face or fixation was displayed for 4000 milliseconds. Subjects rated each face while it was displayed on the screen. We set the stimulus duration at 4000 milliseconds based on findings in a behavioral study of 152 subjects aged 9 to 20 years (D.S. Pine et al, unpublished data, 2003). When the threat-rating task was administered behaviorally, mean reaction times for ratings of different emotions ranged from 645 to 2924 milliseconds for youth of all ages. On average, up to 10% of subjects required more than 2500 milliseconds to rate some face types; thus, use of a shorter stimulus duration would have resulted in considerable loss of data. Prior research by our group has indicated that reaction times tend to be longer in the magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) scanner (E. Leibenluft et al, unpublished data, 2003). Therefore, we displayed each stimulus for 4000 milliseconds to minimize the likelihood that subjects would fail to respond before a trial ended. This stimulus duration also ensured that subjects would have time to make considered ratings without having to rely on working memory. Each stimulus or fixation was followed by an intertrial interval that varied in duration from 750 to 1250 milliseconds.

Grayscale face stimuli were selected from standardized sets constructed by Ekman and Friesen, 1976 and Gur, and Tottenham and Nelson (2000). Each subject viewed faces posed by 32 adults drawn randomly from a pool of 56 actors with the constraints that two pictures of each emotion type appeared in each block and that no subject viewed any actor posing more than one expression. The facial expression that a given actor posed varied randomly across subjects; for example, subject 1 might have seen a specific actor posing a happy face and subject 2 might have seen the same actor posing an angry face. Thus, different actors displayed different emotions to different participants, which prevented potential rating biases related to the identity of the actor rather than the expression displayed on the face.

Visual stimuli were displayed on Avotec Silent Vision Glasses (Avotec, Stuart, Florida), and responses were recorded using a 5-key button box developed by MRI Devices (Waukesha, Wisconsin). Before MRI scanning, participants rehearsed the task in an MRI simulator using a practice set of neutral faces drawn from the Benton Facial Recognition Test (Benton et al 1994). This practice session served to acclimate participants to the MRI environment and to ensure that they understood the task.

MRI data acquisition

Whole-brain blood oxygen level dependent (BOLD) fMRI data were acquired on a General Electric (Waukesha, Wisconsin) Signa 3T scanner. Following sagittal localization and manual shimming, functional T2*-weighted images were acquired using an echo-planar single-shot gradient echo pulse sequence with a matrix size of 64 × 64 mm, repetition time (TR) of 2000 milliseconds, echo time (TE) of 40 milliseconds, field of view (FOV) of 240 mm, and voxels of 3.75 × 3.75 × 5 mm. Images were acquired in 23 contiguous 5-mm axial slices per brain volume positioned parallel to the anterior commisure and posterior commissure (AC-PC) line. All functional data were gathered in a single 14-minute run for each subject. After echo-planar imaging (EPI) acquisition, a high-resolution T1-weighted anatomical image was acquired to aid with spatial normalization. A standardized magnetization-prepared gradient echo sequence (180 1-mm sagittal slices, FOV = 256, number of excitations [NEX] = 1, TR = 11.4 milliseconds, TE = 4.4 milliseconds, matrix = 256 × 256, time to inversion [TI] = 300 milliseconds, bandwidth = 130 Hz/pixel, 33 kHz/256 pixels) was used.

fMRI analysis methods

For each subject, reconstructed fMRI images were examined for excessive motion using MedX software (Medical Numerics, Sterling, Virginia). Subjects who moved more than 1.5 mm in any plane were discarded. All subsequent analyses were conducted with SPM software (SPM99b, Wellcome Department of Imaging Neuroscience, University College of London, London, United Kingdom) and Matlab 5.3 (Mathworks, Natick, Massachusetts) routines. Functional data were corrected for slice timing, motion corrected, coregistered to the anatomical data, spatially normalized to a Montreal Neurologic Institute (MNI) T1-weighted template image supplied with SPM99, and smoothed with an isotropic 8-mm full width at half maximum (FWHM) Gaussian kernel. After preprocessing, fMRI images were visually inspected to evaluate the quality of the normalization procedure.

At the individual subject level, event-related response amplitudes were estimated using the General Linear Model (GLM) for each event type (angry, fearful, happy, neutral faces). The waveform used to model each type of event-related response in the GLM was a rectangular pulse (4-second duration) convolved with the hemodynamic response function specified by SPM99b. Contrast images were generated for each subject using pairwise comparisons of the event-related BOLD responses across event types. Each contrast image was divided by the subject-specific voxel time series means, yielding values proportional to percentage fMRI signal change (Zarahn et al 1997). Each contrast image was then subjected to another level of smoothing with an isotropic Gaussian kernel (FWHM = 11.4) to decrease nonstationarity in spatial autocorrelation structure introduced by the previous step (Friston et al 2000). This additional smoothing has the disadvantage of decreasing spatial resolution, particularly in small areas such as the amygdala and OFC; however, we elected to use this approach in light of its statistical advantages.

For all group-level analyses, a random effects model was employed to permit population-level inferences (Holmes and Friston 1998). Statistical analysis focused on selected regions of interest (ROIs). As noted above, we entered the study with two clear a priori hypotheses. Therefore, we defined ROIs a priori and used the Gaussian random field threshold (α = .05). To correct for multiple comparisons, we applied the small volume correction within each region. Regions of interest were defined to include the right and left amygdala, the right and left OFC, and the ACC (Figure 2). Each was defined using standard anatomical criteria (Szeszko et al 1999) on a single MNI template and applied to all normalized brains at the group level. Binary masks for each ROI consisted of voxels in which all subjects showed a measurable BOLD response; thus, subjects' data were not differentially affected by signal dropout in difficult-to-image regions. Previous research has demonstrated the feasibility of using a common stereotactic space for pediatric and adult brains (Burgund et al 2002).

Group-level contrasts were constructed to permit examination of interactions among face type, age group, and gender. These analyses allowed us to test a priori hypotheses at an omnibus level while controlling experimentwise error. We tested our two a priori hypotheses that gender differences in activation would exist and would vary across development by generating contrasts to examine whether developmental stage (adult, adolescent) moderated gender differences in relative activation to angry versus other emotional facial expressions. Specifically, we computed t-values to evaluate differences in activation in each ROI between angry faces and each other type of face across adult females and males and adolescent females and males. Where significant, development-by-gender interactions existed, we then decomposed them post hoc. This involved examination of gender differences in activation within each age group.

Where gender differences were evident, we also conducted additional secondary post hoc analyses comparing activation for each face type with activation during passively viewed fixation events. Some disagreement persists concerning the utility of fixation as a measure of baseline brain activity (Gusnard and Raichle 2001). Regardless, it provides an alternate benchmark against which activation during face viewing can be compared, possibly facilitating more precise characterization of group differences in a way that might offer novel insights. These post hoc analyses, which were conducted separately by age group and gender, allowed us to look more closely at men's, women's, boys', and girls' idiosyncratic patterns of neural activity to individual emotional expressions in isolation rather than in contrast with other emotional expressions. Finally, for contrasts of angry versus fearful, angry versus neutral, and angry versus happy faces, we examined main effects of age group and gender.

Behavioral data analysis methods

Data were gathered regarding subjects' behavioral performance. These data served two main purposes. First, they allowed us to confirm that subjects were attending to the instructions and face types presented and performing the task in a consistent manner. Second, they allowed us to interpret the degree to which potential differences in brain activation might be mediated by differences in perceptual response to the stimuli. To the extent that behavioral differences emerge, this suggests the need to examine potential differences in activation while controlling for behavioral differences (Casey 2002). Differences in activation in the context of comparable behavioral performance, in contrast, clearly identify differences in the degree to which one or another brain region was engaged under conditions eliciting comparable performance (Callicott and Weinberger 2000).

To evaluate potential differences across groups and emotional expression types in behavioral performance, we conducted repeated measures analysis of variance (ANOVA) with Greenhouse-Geisser corrections. Hostility rating and reaction time served as dependent variables. Reaction time data for two adult subjects were not recorded due to equipment problems; consequently, these subjects were excluded from analyses of reaction time.

We first examined three-way interactions among age group, gender, and pairs of emotion types (angry-neutral, angry-fearful, angry-happy). These analyses were designed to test our primary a priori hypotheses that gender differences in activation would exist and would be greater in adults than adolescents. Region of interest analyses corrected for multiple comparisons within each region revealed significant interactions between age group and gender for the angry versus neutral and angry versus fearful but not the angry versus happy contrasts (Table 2). When interaction terms were excluded from the model, post hoc analyses of main effects of gender and age group yielded no significant effects for contrasts of angry versus fearful, angry versus neutral, or angry versus happy faces (p > .05).

Angry versus fearful

The contrast of angry versus fearful conditions yielded significant age by gender interactions bilaterally in the OFC and in the left amygdala (Table 2). Post hoc ROI analyses conducted separately for each age group showed that relative engagement in the right OFC and left amygdala to angry versus fearful faces was significantly greater in adult women than adult men, with no significant gender differences apparent in adolescents. Adult women also showed more relative activation to angry than fearful faces than did adult men in the right amygdala, a region in which no significant age by gender interactions had been detected (Table 3). For both angry versus neutral and angry versus fearful contrasts, no significant interactions were evident in the ACC.

Secondary analyses

Post hoc ROI analyses comparing activation associated with each type of emotional face to activation during fixation provided further detail regarding the gender differences in patterns of performance (Table 4 and Table 5). Relative to baseline fixation, adult men showed significant bilateral OFC activation to angry and fearful faces and significant right OFC activation to neutral faces. Activations in the left amygdala in response to neutral faces and in the ACC in response to angry and fearful faces were also significant. No significant effects were evident for contrasts of happy faces versus fixation in any ROI (p > .05). Adult women, in contrast, showed a more specific pattern of engagement. For adult women, bilateral OFC, left amygdala, and the ACC showed significant activation only to angry faces relative to fixation. Contrasts of fearful, neutral, and happy faces versus fixation yielded no significant effects in any ROI (p > .05). Thus, in adult men, a variety of expressions elicited more activation in the OFC, amygdala, and ACC than did fixation, whereas only angry faces were associated with greater relative ROI activation in adult women.

Behavioral data

For all subjects combined, subjective hostility ratings differed significantly across facial expression types [F(3,81) = 131.24, p < .001]. On a 5-point scale (1 = no hostility and 5 = extreme hostility), mean hostility ratings (standard deviations are in parentheses) were 3.50 (.71) for angry, 2.15 (.85) for fearful, 1.81 (.64) for neutral, and 1.06 (.16) for happy faces. There were no significant interactions with age group [F(3,81) = 1.83, p = .15] or with gender [F(3,81) = 1.14, p = .34], nor was the age × gender × face type interaction significant [F(3,81) = 1.26, p = .29].

Reaction times for the sample as a whole differed significantly across facial expression types [F(2.7,75.6) = 46.98, p < .001]. Mean reaction times in milliseconds were 2042.77 (377.65) for angry, 2033.16 (480.86) for fearful, 1895.48 (459.40) for neutral, and 1380.47 (315.35) for happy faces. There were no significant interactions with age group [F(2.7,75.6) = 1.50, p = .22] or with gender [F(2.7,75.6) = 2.03, p = .12], nor was the age × gender × face type interaction significant [F(2.7,75.6) = 1.76, p = .17].

Discussion

Consistent with our hypotheses, findings indicated developmental differences between male and female patterns of neural activation while evaluating the degree of threat conveyed by varied emotional faces. Whereas women showed greater relative OFC and amygdala activation than adult men when processing unambiguous versus ambiguous threat stimuli, no such significant differences in engagement of the OFC, amygdala, or ACC were apparent between adolescent boys and girls. Thus, in line with our predictions and prior behavioral findings indicating that gender differences in response to threat stimuli may vary across development, gender differences in patterns of neural activation to threat faces relative to ambiguous faces were significant in adults but not in adolescents.

Contrary to our expectation that unambiguous threat cues would elicit heightened activation in female subjects relative to male subjects, however, the present findings suggest instead that gender disparities are apparent because adult women respond differently than adult men or adolescents of either gender to ambiguous threat cues. As the direct contrasts between activation to threatening or ambiguous emotional faces and a baseline fixation condition illustrate, the adult gender differences in activation during explicit evaluation of threat emerged because adult women engaged frontolimbic structures more selectively than did men, not because women showed more activation than men to threatening faces per se. For women, whereas unambiguously threatening (angry) faces elicited significantly greater activation in these regions than did fixation, ambiguous (fearful, neutral) faces did not. Thus, for women, direct contrasts between unambiguously threatening faces and ambiguous faces yielded significant results, with greater activation to unambiguous threat. For men, in contrast, threatening and ambiguous faces both elicited significant or near significant levels of activation relative to fixation, largely in the OFC and ACC. Direct contrasts between unambiguously threatening and ambiguous faces therefore yielded nonsignificant results for men. Interestingly, neither group showed significant activation to unambiguously nonthreatening (happy) faces relative to fixation; consequently, given that both groups showed significant activation to angry faces versus fixation, results of the angry versus happy contrast did not differ between groups.

Areas in which women showed greater activation than men in the anger versus fear and anger versus neutral contrasts, including the amygdala, were largely right-lateralized. Some research has indicated that whereas the right amygdala figures prominently in the early, rapid detection of emotional cues, the left amygdala is specialized for more sustained evaluation of such cues (e.g., Phillips et al., 2001 and Wright et al., 2001). The present findings suggest that at least in the context of faces that convey ambiguous signals regarding threat, women may engage the right amygdala differently than do men, thus highlighting the need to examine such laterality effects separately across genders.

Our pattern of findings is particularly interesting in light of the absence of gender differences in subjective ratings of faces' hostility and latencies to response. Male and female adults performed comparably on the task, drawing similar conclusions about the degrees of hostility that different facial expressions communicated and doing so at comparable speeds; however, they appeared to engage neural paths differently in this process. The lack of behavioral gender differences is interesting in light of consistent findings that females perform more accurately than males on facial emotion identification tasks (Hall, 1978 and McClure, 2000). The present task, however, differed from standard facial emotion identification tasks in that it involved rating the intensity of displayed emotion rather than categorizing it into discrete categories. Gender differences might have been more pronounced in a forced choice categorization paradigm. Additionally, the faces in the present study represented extreme prototypes of different emotions and may therefore have rendered the study subject to ceiling effects.

In contrast to adults, adolescent boys and girls showed no significant differences in neural engagement to any emotion type. Thus, gender does not appear to moderate patterns of neural activity to facial emotion in the same way during adolescence as it does in adulthood. It may be that gender differences in neural activation to varying degrees of threat emerge gradually across the course of adolescence. Findings from one study, for example, suggest that associations between age and both amygdala and dorsolateral prefrontal cortex activation to fearful facial expressions differ for male and female adolescents (Killgore et al 2001). This possibility, that adolescence represents a transitional stage during which gender differences progressively develop, merits further study in larger samples of adolescents, where age effects can be examined more closely. Additionally, it will be useful to examine the emergence of gender differences in neural responses to ambiguous threat cues in the broader context of face processing. In particular, investigation of developmental gender differences in the underlying mechanisms for other aspects of face processing (e.g., face recognition, face memory) is warranted.

Contrary to expectations, no significant gender differences in relative activation were evident for any group in contrasts of threatening and unambiguously nonthreatening (happy) faces. Indeed, all groups showed an absence of significant activation to nonthreatening faces, which could stem from our constraint of subjects' focus to identification of threat. Because happy faces convey little to no threat, subjects may have afforded them less attention or processed them less thoroughly (e.g., subjects may have used smiles as iconic markers of “no threat”). In future studies, use of parametrically varied emotional face stimuli and monitoring of eye movements during stimulus processing may help in characterizing the processes that occur when individuals evaluate threat in happy faces.

Our findings extend results of previous fMRI research that examined related gender differences among adults or adolescents alone. Prior adult studies yielded evidence of gender differences in laterality of response to different emotional expressions (Kesler/West et al., 2001, Killgore and Yurgelun-Todd, 2001 and Lee et al., 2002), and a study of adolescents suggested that the ratio of prefrontal cortex to amygdala activation during passive viewing of fearful faces increased with age in girls but not boys (Killgore et al 2001). Procedural differences from the present study, including our use of an event-related design and manipulation of attention state, render direct comparisons across these and other studies of facial expression processing difficult. Nonetheless, taken together, our findings and those from earlier studies suggest that gender differences in patterns of neural responses to emotional faces may increase during adolescence until they are fully expressed in adulthood.

Our results underscore the importance of using an active behavioral task and multiple types of emotional stimuli in studies of gender differences in neural activation to social cues. In particular, our findings suggest that gender differences in engagement to such cues may be particularly apparent under specific, constrained attentional conditions, such that women may engage neural pathways differently from men when they explicitly evaluate ambiguous signals of social threat. Taken together with results of earlier fMRI studies of gender differences in emotional face processing, this pattern of findings suggests that different results may emerge depending on whether and how participants' attention is constrained or directed.

The gender differences observed in the present study were evident in contrasts of faces that conveyed different levels of threat. By limiting studies to comparisons of individual emotional facial expressions against fixation, researchers risk overlooking subtle differences in gender-specific patterns of activation. Other stimulus characteristics are also worthy of further attention. For example, in light of behavioral findings indicating that women may be particularly sensitive to threat cues displayed by male faces (Goos and Silverman 2002), it will be important for future studies to examine ways in which stimulus and subject characteristics interact to influence patterns of neural activation. The use of face stimuli that display emotions that parametrically increase in intensity, rather than faces that show iconic “peak” emotional expressions, would also extend the current literature. Given that women appear to be more adept than men at categorizing subtly distinct emotional faces (Hall, 1978 and McClure, 2000), use of such stimuli might facilitate identification of associations between neural and behavioral gender differences.

Closer examination of the effects of stimulus presentation duration is also warranted. Stimulus duration varies widely across studies of face processing, with some, such as ours, presenting stimuli for as long as several seconds (e.g., Kesler/West et al., 2001 and Killgore and Yurgelun-Todd, 2001) and others displaying stimuli much more briefly (e.g., Thomas et al 2001). It is therefore possible that the activation patterns we observed reflected different aspects of face processing than those that some other researchers have studied. Whereas findings from studies with short presentation times may reflect correlates of initial face perception, findings from studies with longer presentation times may be associated with later, more elaborative processes. Systematic evaluation of the role that stimulus duration plays in activation during facial expression processing could facilitate reconciliation of different findings across studies.

The present results provide evidence that gender differences exist in patterns of activation in neural structures implicated in attention to emotional stimuli, particularly those that convey ambiguous signals of threat, and that these differences emerge across the course of the transition from adolescence to adulthood. These findings, especially if replicated in larger samples where age can be examined continuously, have implications for studies aimed at elucidating gender differences in the development of mood and anxiety disorders. For example, we found that for women, regions linked with the mediation of emotion activate selectively during evaluation of overt threat cues. For adolescent girls, in contrast, these regions showed activation during evaluation of both overt and ambiguous threat signals. This finding could indicate that changes in female neural processing of potential threat cues are not consolidated until adulthood. Thus, adolescence, when such changes ostensibly begin to emerge, may be an optimal period for intervention aimed at helping girls learn to objectively evaluate and cope with perceived threats. Replication of our findings in populations at risk for emotion-based psychopathology is therefore important, as such research may help explain how gender-associated changes in neural function during adolescence relate to both healthy development and risk for psychopathology.
