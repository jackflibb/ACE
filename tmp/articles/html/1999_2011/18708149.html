Developmental changes in neural activation and psychophysiological interaction patterns of brain regions associated with interference control and time perception

  • Susanne Neufanga, b, c, d, , ,
  • Gereon R. Finka, b, e,

Received 19 February 2008, Revised 11 July 2008, Accepted 17 July 2008, Available online 29 July 2008

Abstract

Interference control and time perception are mediated by common neural networks, including the frontal and parietal lobes, the cerebellum and the basal ganglia. Previous studies have shown that while time perception develops early in life, interference control seems to follow a protracted course of maturation into late adolescence. Thus, the current study examined developmental changes in neural activation and functional interaction between brain regions during a combined time discrimination and interference control task using fMRI. Thirty-four participants, aged 8–15 years, were scanned while performing a spatial stimulus response compatibility (SRC) task and a time discrimination (TD) task using identical stimuli. We found shared neural activation in a fronto-parieto-cerebellar network as well as task-specific patterns of psychophysiological interaction with positive coupling between the right inferior frontal gyrus (IFG), the superior parietal lobes bilaterally, the contralateral IFG and the thalamus during interference control and positive interactions between the right IFG and bilateral cerebellar activity and the thalamus during time discrimination. Developmental changes in task performance and brain activation patterns were only observed during the SRC task, with increased neural activity in the left inferior parietal gyrus and positive coupling between fronto-parietal brain regions that was only observed in the adolescents group. These results suggest that although both cognitive tasks rely on a shared neural network, distinct developmental curves of brain activation and connectivity could be observed associated with differential maturation patterns underlying cognitive development.

Introduction

Interference control and time perception are two key cognitive functions that are essential for normal social functioning and have an impact on a wide range of motor and cognitive functions, such as movement, speech, and planning or speed of cognitive processing (Rubia and Smith, 2004). In a number of everyday activities, both processes seem to interact, for example, with regard to prospective memory, time monitoring or motor planning (Kerns et al., 2001 and Mäntylä et al., 2007). Abnormalities in both interference control and time perception have been described in several neuropsychiatric disorders, such as attention deficit/hyperactivity disorder (Rubia et al., 1999a, Rubia et al., 1999b, Smith et al., 2002 and Sonuga-Barke et al., 1998), dyslexia and dysphasia (Nicolson et al., 1995), schizophrenia (Davalos et al., 2003 and Volz et al., 2001), depression (Kuhs et al., 1991) and in patients with brain lesions (Harrington et al., 1998 and Rubia et al., 1997). Previous research has shown that both functions are dependent on working memory performance, since holding either the goal of the task or the temporal interval online is an important underlying cognitive component involved in both cognitive control and timing processes.

Interference or cognitive control is a key aspect of “executive functions” and is defined as the flexible regulation of thoughts and actions in the presence of competing ones (Miller and Cohen, 2001 and Durston and Casey, 2006). It is measured typically with Stroop tasks (Tipper et al., 1989 and Adleman et al., 2002), spatial stimulus–response compatibility tasks (Casey et al., 2002 and Davidson et al., 2006) or go–no-go tasks (Luria, 1961, Casey et al., 1997 and Tamm et al., 2002). Interference control has been shown to become more efficient with age (Harnishfeger and Bjorklund, 1993), with children having more difficulty in making the correct response when there is interference from competing response alternatives. Generally, adult-like performance on these tasks cannot be observed before age 12 (Huizinga et al., 2006 and Bédard et al., 2002).

By contrast, little is known about the development of timing abilities. Time perception refers to the perceptive aspects of cognitive time management such as perceiving temporal intervals and the ability to estimate temporal delays (Fuster, 1990). Within the framework of elementary time experiences independent timing functions are assumed (Rammsayer and Lima, 1991), and there is preliminary evidence for distinct mechanisms underlying duration discrimination with very brief intervals in the range of milliseconds (time perception) and longer intervals in the range of seconds (time estimation) (Fraisse, 1984 and Buhusi and Meck, 2005). Previous studies suggest that timing abilities compared to interference control mature earlier in development with performance improvements observed in the age range of 5 to 8 years, but with adult-like behaviour appearing from age 8 and on (McCormack et al., 2005 and Clement and Droit-Volet, 2006). Smith et al. (2002) suggested that healthy children can detect differences in temporal intervals as short as 180 ms.

Previous neuroimaging research has indicated that there are two timing circuits that can be dissociated: an automatic timing system that works in the millisecond range, which is used in discrete-event (discontinuous) timing and involves the cerebellum; and a continuous-event, cognitively controlled timing system that requires attention and involves the basal ganglia and related cortical structures. In many tasks, these two timing systems seem to work in parallel (Buhusi and Meck, 2005). Thus, there seems to be a substantial overlap between brain structures involved in interference control and those involved in timing mechanisms (Rubia and Smith, 2004), including the fronto-parietal cortex, in particular the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (DLPFC) (for review Durston and Casey, 2006), posterior parietal cortex (Liston et al., 2006), the supplementary motor area (SMA) (Rubia and Smith, 2004) and the anterior cingulate cortex (ACC) (e.g. Fan et al., 2008), the basal ganglia (Rubia et al., 2007) and the cerebellum (Casey et al., 2007;Rubia et al., 2007 and Rubia and Smith, 2004). Each of these different brain regions seems to have their specific role in contributing to cognitive control and time management processes. For example, the dorsolateral and inferior prefrontal cortices –possibly in connection with their role in working memory and attention– appear to play a special role in focusing on time and holding temporal intervals online, which is essential for most time discrimination functions (Livesey et al., 2007, Lewis and Miall, 2006 and Nenadic et al., 2003). The SMA appears to be a crucial brain area to process temporal intervals in order to adjust movement in the temporal domain. The ACC has been suggested to have a more generic role in error monitoring and cognitive control processes necessary for both executive tasks (attention to action) and time estimation (evaluative comparator). The basal ganglia and the cerebellum have been implicated in pure time perception (Rubia et al., 1999a) and sensorimotor synchronization in motor timing (e.g. Molinari et al., 2007 and Jantzen et al., 2007), as well as learning about the frequency and timing of events, i. e. learning what to expect and when — with anticipation as an important aspect of cognitive control (Herwig et al., 2007, Toplak and Tannock, 2005 and Birbaumer et al., 2005). The posterior parietal region has been implicated in signalling the prefrontal cortex when competing stimuli require top-down biasing of attention in favour of one stimulus attribute or location over another (Tamm et al., 2006 and Durston et al., 2003), as well as sustained attention to time (Ortuno et al., 2002). Each of these regions project both to and from the prefrontal cortex via either the fronto-striato-thalamic (Alexander et al., 1986) or the fronto-thalamo-cerebellar pathways (Middleton and Strick, 1994) to impose top-down control of behaviour.

Although it is well established that parts of this network, such as the prefrontal cortices, undergo dramatic structural changes throughout childhood and adolescence (Barnea-Goraly et al., 2005 and Klingberg et al., 1999) and that these changes are thought to be critical for changes in brain activity associated with cognitive development (Olesen et al., 2007 and Rubia et al., 2007), little is known about how the interaction of the brain areas involved mature functionally. Recently, Edin et al. (2007) demonstrated that increased strength of synaptic connections between fronto-parietal regions coding for similar stimuli was the only developmental process that accounted for the observed changes in brain activity associated with the development of working memory during childhood. Likewise, protracted developmental changes in connectivity in functional networks associated with response inhibition, were reported by Stevens et al. (2007) with adolescents showing weaker integration between networks and still incomplete functional specialization compared to adults.

The aim of the present study was to investigate the neural basis underlying the development of two interrelated cognitive functions, interference control and time discrimination during childhood and adolescence, by using a combined experimental paradigm with identical stimuli for both tasks. This approach allows to identify developmental dissociations which are independent of differences in sensory stimulation, working memory demands or motor responses. We expected to find differential developmental patterns of cognitive control processes and timing abilities with the latter maturing earlier in childhood. In addition, we conducted psychophysiological interaction analyses to assess the developmental changes in the interaction between the brain areas involved. We hypothesized finding on the one hand large overlapping networks of neural activity involved in both tasks. On the other hand we expect task-specific connectivity patterns and developmental changes in top-down modulation processes in particular within/from prefrontal areas, such as age-dependent changes in the connectivity between the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex and the cerebellum in the time discrimination (TD) task and between prefrontal regions and the superior parietal cortex and striatum in the stimulus response compatibility (SRC) task.

Methods

Subjects

Thirty-four subjects in the age range of 8 to 15 years (21 boys and 13 girls, Mage = 12.2 years ± 2.2 years) were recruited as part of an ongoing neurodevelopmental study at the Department of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry. All subjects were carefully screened for childhood psychiatric disorders and neurodevelopmental diseases by a standardized semi-structured interview for the diagnosis of mental disorders in children ( Unnewehr, 1995) and were free of any current or present mental disorder (i.e. ADHD, pervasive developmental disorders, etc.). IQ was estimated on the basis of a short version of the Wechsler Intelligence Scale for Children III ( Wechsler, 1991 and Tewes et al., 1999). Children with an IQ below 80 were excluded (IQMean = 103.2 ± 13.9; IQrange = 80–133). The physical sexual maturation was assessed via Tanner Stages (TS, Tanner and Davies, 1985) in a medical examination conducted by an experienced physician (TSMean = 2.8 ± 1.4, TSrange = 1–5). All subjects were screened for laterality with the Edinburgh inventory ( Oldfield, 1971) and were judged as right-handed according to the laterality index (L), suggested by Oldfield ( Oldfield, 1971) (LMean = 80 ± 16.0, Lrange = 47–100).

The study was approved by the Medical Ethics Committee of the University Hospital of Aachen, and all volunteers and all parents, respectively, gave informed consent.

Paradigm

Our subjects performed two tasks on an identical set of stimuli; one task was a time discrimination task (TD Task, adapted from Smith et al., 2003), the other one a stimulus–response compatibility task (SRC task, adapted from the dots test, Davidson et al., 2006). As both original tasks were comparable with respect to the sequence and type of stimuli they could be easily combined without affecting the underlying concept that should be measured. In the adopted paradigm each stimulus consisted of two symbols, which were presented consecutively on the left or right side of the screen (the order of the side was balanced over the whole experiment). The symbols could either be one square and one heart, two squares or two hearts. All stimuli were balanced for responses (same number of right and left answers), symbols and presentation order. One symbol was presented longer than the other, varying between 250 ms and 350 ms (average difference in presentation time = 300 ms) (see Fig. 1 for the time course of the experimental paradigm). The presentation duration of the shorter presented symbol was 250 ms (reference item), the presentation of the longer presented symbol varied between 500 ms and 600 ms.

In the time discrimination task, subjects were instructed to indicate via button press on which side of the screen the symbol was presented longer. In the SRC Task, subjects were asked to press the button on the SAME side where the second symbol had appeared if the symbols were identical. For example, if two squares were presented, the first on the right, the second on the left side of the screen, the subject had to press the left button (spatially compatible response). In the case of two different symbols, the subject had to press the button opposite to the position of the second symbol (spatially incompatible response). The overall ratio of compatible and incompatible trials was 70:30, varying from 60:40 to 80:20 between the blocks.

Ratio of compatible/incompatible trials in the SRC Task as well as the time difference in presentation time of the symbols in the TD Task was varied in order to create two levels of task difficulty. Variation of task difficulty for both tasks was based on recent findings which provided evidence for larger discrimination intervals to be easier to detect in a TD task (Smith et al., 2003) and responses to incompatible trials to be more accurate in conditions with high compared to low frequencies of incompatible trials in SRC tasks (Casey et al., 1997).This experimental manipulation allowed accounting for performance differences between younger and older subjects (Durston and Casey, 2006). In the easier condition of the TD task, the mean difference in presentation duration was 350 ms (low task difficulty, TD_low) and 250 ms in the more difficult condition (TD_high) (Smith et al., 2003). The easier condition of the SRC Task consisted of a higher number of incompatible trials (60:40%, SRC_low), while in the more difficult condition, the ratio was 80:20% (SRC_high) (Casey et al., 2002).

We used a block design, including blocks of either the TD task or the SRC task. We decided for a block design in order to conduct an efficient analysis of psychophysiological interactions separately for both cognitive functions. The experiment included 4 runs; one run consisted of 4 blocks of the same task, so that the order was either 4 blocks SRC Task, 4 blocks TD Task, 4 blocks SRC Tasks, 4 TD Tasks or vice versa. To minimize working memory load the specific task symbol was presented for 1500 ms at the beginning of each block (every 10th stimulus). If the task changed a short instruction was presented for 3 s (see Fig. 1). There were breaks between the runs of 15 s each.

One block consisted of 10 stimuli. Stimulation was continuous with a fixed trial duration of 2500 ms and no separate inter-trial interval. To avoid response tendencies a random onset of the first stimulus was included, varying from 0 to 350 ms, stimulus presentation of the first stimulus, and an interstimulus interval (ISI) of 100 ms, followed by the presentation of the second stimulus. Response time lasted until the fixed trial length of 2500 ms was completed (see Fig. 1) for the duration of at last 1300 ms. Thus, trials could be separated from each other and the correct pair of stimuli could be identified clearly. Each block was synchronized with the scanner. To avoid effects of fatigue, a short break was implemented after each block (see Fig. 1). In these phases a specific break symbol was presented while the scanner was still running. The duration of one block was 33 s, and one run lasted 2.5 min. Pure stimulation time was 6.67min, and the duration of the whole experiment was 13.4 min. Stimuli were presented via a visual stimulation device (goggles, Silent Vision™, Avotec, FL, USA). Each stimulus had a height and width of 2.25° visual angle and was presented at a horizontal eccentricity of 9° to the medial border of the stimulus in the periphery of either the right or left visual field. Responses were given by the index and middle finger of the right hand using a fibre optic response device.

All subjects were trained before the experiment with a training-version including visual feedback. As both tasks were presented one after the other, the tasks were trained in the same order as it was presented during the experiment. Subjects started the experiment when they succeeded at the task with less than 2 errors per block.

To minimize working memory load for task instructions and prevent confusion between the two tasks subjects performed the same task for 4 consecutive blocks. If the task changed an additional short instruction was presented. Thus, subjects were given some extra time to shift between the tasks. In addition, a short break was implemented after each run.

Data acquisition

A SONATA MRI system (Siemens, Erlangen, Germany) operating at 1.5 T was used to obtain T2⁎-weighted echoplanar (EPI) images with BOLD contrast (matrix size: 64 × 64; voxel size: 3.12 × 3.12 mm2). In all, 266 volumes of 30 4-mm-thick axial slices were acquired sequentially with an 0.8-mm gap (repetition time = 3.2 s, echo time = 66 ms). The first five volumes were discarded to allow for T1 equilibration effects. Images were spatially realigned to the first volume to correct for interscan movement and normalized to a standard EPI template (resampled to 3 × 3 × 3-mm3 voxels). The data were then smoothed with a Gaussian kernel of 8-mm full-width at half-maximum to accommodate intersubject anatomical variability. A high-pass filter (using a cutoff of 512 s) and a correction for temporal autocorrelation in the data (AR 1 + white noise) were applied to accommodate serial correlations.

Structural images were acquired for each subject using an isotropic T1-weighted MP-Rage (magnetization-prepared rapid acquisition gradient echo). Images yielded 160 contiguous isotropic 1 mm sagittal slices (image matrix = 180 × 256 mm2, FoV = 256 mm, TR = 2200 ms, TE = 3.93 ms, flip angle P = 15°, inter-slice-gap = 0.5 mm).

Statistical analysis of imaging data

Data were analyzed with Statistical Parametric Mapping software SPM2 [Wellcome Department of Imaging Neuroscience, London (http://www.fil.ion.ucl.ac.uk/spm2.html)] using random-effects models. At the first level, the four experimental conditions (i.e., TD_high, TD_low, SRC_high, SRC_low), and the baseline condition (BL, consisting of visual stimulation during the break with the break sign) were incorporated into one design matrix. Regressors contained onsets of the blocks of the specific task and task difficulty condition with a duration of 30 s for the experimental conditions and 15 s for the baseline condition. For each session, the six conditions were modeled as a boxcar function convolved with the canonical hemodynamic response function (HRF). The six head-movement parameters (three rigid body translations and three rotations) were included as confounds. Estimated motion parameters were examined on a subject-by-subject basis to ensure that the amount of absolute motion did not exceed 3 mm. All subjects exhibited less than 1 mm of absolute motion over the course of the experiment.

The first-level analysis focused mainly on activated networks for both functions. For this purpose, the following condition-specific effects for each subject were estimated according to the general linear model, and the parameter estimates were passed into second-level one-sample t-tests with nonsphericity correction: TD Task (TD_high + TD_low) > BL and SRC Task (SRC_high + SRC_low) > BL. This random-effects analysis assessed effects on the basis of intersubject variance and thus allowed inferences about the population from which the subjects were drawn. Subjects were excluded from the task-specific group analysis if accuracy in the specific task was below chance (less than 50% correct responses).

In the next step, we analyzed task-overlapping and task-specific brain activation patterns. To determine brain areas commonly involved in the compatibility and the time discrimination task, we performed a random-effects conjunction analysis, based on inclusive masking, as suggested by Nichols et al. (2005). This conservative analysis corresponds to a logical AND operation, showing those voxels, which are significant in both the TD Task (TD_high + TD_low) > BL and SRC Task (SRC_high + SRC_low) > BL. For this analysis, we used a threshold of p < 0.05, corrected for multiple comparisons at the voxel level. Areas of task-specific activations were revealed within a random-effects analysis directly contrasting activation pattern of both tasks (TD Task > SRC Task; SRC Task > TD Task).

To investigate developmental changes in brain activation patterns, we performed simple regression analysis with age using the baseline contrast for the specific task. If task performance was correlated with age, multiple regression analyses were performed using age as a covariate of interest and error rates or RTs as covariates of no interest in order to control for the possible artefacts caused by behavioural performance differences. Multiple regressors were orthogonalized to accomplish statistical constraints (Büchel and Friston, 1997).

In addition to linear regression analyses, extreme group comparisons (Schlaggar et al., 2002) were conducted, contrasting younger prepubertal children (n = 11, 5 boys, 6 girls; aged 8 to 11 years with mean TS = 1.4) to adolescents (n = 11, 7 boys, 4 girls; aged 14 to 15 years with mean TS = 4.1). As accuracy was highly correlated with age in the SRC Task, we applied an AnCova Model using error rate as a covariate of no interest to control for possible confounding effects.

The effect maxima were plotted by extracting intensity values of the individual local maxima from beta images. Except for the conjunction analysis, results were reported for whole brain analyses (WB) on a level of p < 0.05, corrected for multiple comparisons on a cluster level with an underlying threshold on voxel level of p < .001. Coordinates were reported in MNI-space.

PPI analysis

To assess the hypothesis that the DLPFC mediates executive functions and time perception, we estimated the functional integration of both systems in a psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis. Based on the results of the conjunction analysis, the BOLD signal time course in the right prefrontal cortex was entered into a psychophysiological interaction (PPI) analysis (Friston et al., 1997). A PPI analysis aims to explain neural responses in one brain area in terms of the interaction between influences of another brain region and a cognitive/sensory process. Thus, a psychophysiological interaction can be seen as a condition-specific change of coupling between brain areas. The PPI analysis consists of a design matrix with three regressors: (i) the “psychological variable”, representing the cognitive/sensory process of interest (here TD Task vs. SRC Task), (ii) the “physiological variable”, representing the neural response in a given brain region (here the right inferior frontal gyrus), and (iii) the interaction term of (i) and (ii).

The psychological variable used was a vector coding for the specific task (1 for TD Task, − 1 for SRC Task) convolved with the HRF. The individual time series for the right PFC was obtained by extracting the first principal component from all raw voxel time series in a sphere (8 mm radius) centered on the coordinates of the subject-specific PFC activations. These time series were mean-corrected and high-pass filtered to remove low-frequency signal drifts. The physiological factor was then multiplied with the psychological factor: this constitutes the interaction term. PPI analyses were then carried out for each subject involving the creation of a design matrix with the interaction term, the psychological factor, and the physiological factor as regressors.

Subject-specific contrast images using the contrast [1 0 0], where the first column represented the interaction term, were then entered into a random-effects group analysis (thresholded at p < .05, corrected for multiple comparisons on cluster level with an underlying threshold of p < .001 uncorrected), and task-specific ROI-analyses were performed for the TD Task in the cerebellum and for the SRC Task in the parietal cortex and the striatum. Beta values of the PFC were correlated across both conditions (TD Task > SRC Task and vice versa) as well as for each condition (TD Task > BL, SRC Task > BL). Again, we used linear as well as non-linear approaches to investigate developmental changes in PPI-patterns via simple regression analyses and extreme group comparisons between younger and older subjects.

Results

Behavioral data

Across the whole group, mean accuracy on the TD task was 84.7% ± 11.0% and 87.0% ± 11.0% on the SRC Task. Mean RT on correct trials was 942 ms ± 102.4 ms for the TD task and 921 ms ± 147.9 ms during the SRC task. The mean incompatibility effect (ΔRTincompatible − compatible trials) was 24 ms. An overall 2 × 2 ANOVA with task and task difficulty as repeated measure factors revealed a significant interaction effect for accuracy (F(1,33) = 5.8, p = .02) but not for RT measures, indicating a stronger impact of task difficulty on response incompatibility performance than on time perception. However, accuracy on the TD task was also significantly poorer when the critical time difference was about 250 ms compared to intervals above 300 ms (high difficulty) (p < .02).

Accuracy on the SRC Task showed significant improvement with age (r = − .40, p < .02), while this was not the case for RTs in the SRC Task or for performance in the TD task. Significant age effects were found for accuracy during compatible trials of high task difficulty (r = .46, p < .01) and during incompatible trials in both high (r = .56, p < .00) and low difficulty conditions (r = .59, p < .00) of the SRC Task. An extreme group comparison confirmed significant group differences between younger and older subjects only with regard to accuracy of response incompatibility performance (pextrem < .03). In the TD task, RTs correlated with age only in the condition of high difficulty (rt ⁎ age: rTD_high = − .46, p < .01; rTD_low = − .16, p = .40; accuracy⁎age: rTD_high = .26, p = .15; rTD_low = .04, p = .81).

Additional group comparisons were conducted in order to control for possible gender differences: Boys and girls did not differ significantly from each other with regard to task performance (with p > .05 for all comparisons). In addition no significant interaction effects were found between sex and task (accuracy: Ftask⁎sex = 1.0, n.s., reaction times: Ftask⁎sex = 1.6, n.s.), sex and task difficulty (accuracy: FTaskDiff⁎sex = 0.6, n.s., reaction times: FTaskDiff⁎sex = 0.1, n.s.), and sex and trial type in the SRC task (accuracy: FTrialType⁎sex = 2.0, n.s., reaction times: FTrialType⁎sex = 0.1, n.s.). In addition, we did not find any significant effect between age and sex (Fage⁎sex = 1.8, n.s). The same picture emerged within the analyses of extreme groups. Extreme groups did not differ with respect to gender contribution (p > .12) and no significant interaction effects were found between group and sex for any task parameter (accuracy: Fsex⁎extreme = 1.3, n.s.; reaction times: Fsex⁎extreme = 1.6, n.s.).

Across all subjects, no evidence was found for speed-accuracy trade-offs in any conditions (p > .5). There were no significant correlations between IQ and reaction times or accuracy, neither for the TD task (racc = .26, rrt = .28, n. s.) nor for the SRC task (racc = .01, rrt = .07, n. s.). Likewise IQ did not differ between extreme groups (Mchildren = 100 ± 13.4; Madolescents = 104.6 ± 15.8, TIQ = .8, n. s.). Working memory performance as measured with the Digit-Span test of the WISC-III was significantly associated with RTs in the SCR Task (p < .01), which increased with task difficulty (rSRC_high = − .44, p < .01, rSRC_low = − .40, p < .04) and trial type (rcompTrials = − .45, p < .01; rincompTrials = − .41, p < .02). In the TD Task, working memory performance did not impact significantly on task performance.

Brain regions involved in time perception and cognitive control

Separate random-effects analyses for response incompatibility and for time discrimination (compared against the baseline condition) revealed large networks of neural activity in fronto-parietal and cerebellar regions during both cognitive tasks. A conjunction analysis testing for a logical AND (Nichols et al., 2005) confirmed a large overlap of activated regions, in particular within bilateral fronto-parieto-cerebellar areas as well as the left insular cortex (for localization see Table 1 and Fig. 2). However, few differential neural correlates were found that were specifically associated with one but not the other task: in the SRC Task, there was stronger left-hemispheric activation in fronto-parietal areas, namely in the middle frontal gyrus and precuneus. In contrast, subjects activated the medial frontal gyrus bilaterally during the TD task (for details see also Table 1). On the neural level, no significant effect was found for task difficulty, neither for the SRC Task nor for the TD Task. Likewise, there were no significant effects of IQ on neural activation in any of the two tasks, analyzed via simple regression analyses. Although behaviourally there was a significant correlation between WM and RTs in the SRC task, neurally no significant effect of WM performance was found on brain activation patterns during the SRC task. No significant difference in task-specific activation patterns were found between boys and girls, analyzed via two-sample t-tests.

Discussion

In our study, we examined the developmental changes in the fronto-parieto-cerebellar circuit by simultaneously using tasks of cognitive control and time perception. We aimed to address developmental changes in task-specific neural activation maps as well as in patterns of psychophysiological interaction underlying both functions. Therefore, we identified common and task-specific activation patterns and focused on the prefrontal cortex activation as a key region in our subsequent connectivity analyses. Finally, results of the conjunction analysis (common activation), the random-effects analysis (task-specific) and the PPI analysis (connectivity) were analysed according to developmental changes by linear regression analyses and extreme group comparisons.

Overlapping and task-specific brain activation patterns

As hypothesized, the conjunction analysis revealed a bilateral fronto-parieto-cerebellar network covering the regions pre- and postcentral gyrus, inferior parietal regions and the cerebellum, as well as unilateral activation in the right insula and the right inferior frontal gyrus. Increased neural activity in the inferior frontal gyrus has been previously described in a number of studies on cognitive control (Rubia et al., 2007, Crone et al., 2006 and Rubia et al., 1999b) and time perception (Livesey et al., 2007) and has been associated with holding online either the rule (SRC Task: same symbols, compatible response/different symbols— incompatible) or the duration of the presented symbol.

Task-specific neural activation was found in the left middle frontal gyrus and the left precuneus for the SRC Task and in the orbito-frontal gyrus for the TD task.

The higher activation in left-hemispheric fronto-parietal activation in the SRC task might reflect a greater amount of spatial processing during the SRC than in the TD task. While in the TD Task the temporal processing determines the correct responses, in the SRC Task, the spatial information plays a crucial role: first, the information where the two consecutively presented stimuli appeared has to be encoded, followed by a judgment based on the previously learned rule. Although the exact role of the left parietal cortex in tasks of spatial WM is still unclear (Finke et al., 2006), findings from other imaging studies suggest significant associations between both spatial attention and spatial working memory and a left-hemispheric network covering, among other regions, the middle frontal gyrus and precuneus (Hahn et al., 2006 and Ruff et al., 2003). By contrast, the higher orbito-frontal activation observed in the TD task is in line recent suggestions that the orbito-frontal cortex is important for processing temporal information (Ferstl and von Cramon, 2007).

The connectivity analysis revealed the expected task-specific positive correlations between the right IFG, the cerebellum and the thalamus in the TD Task and between the right IFG and superior parietal areas, the contralateral left IFG and the thalamus in the SRC Task. Numerous human and non-human studies have provided evidence for structural connectivity between these structures. For example, Middleton and Strick (1994) showed that the cerebellum seemed to project to the prefrontal cortex via the thalamus and back to the cerebellum via the pontine nuclei. In humans, the close correspondence between the fronto-parietal cortices and the cerebellum was also demonstrated by fiber tracking in diffusion tensor imaging (Leh et al., 2007) and functional connectivity analyses (Allen et al., 2005). The right inferior fronto-striato-cerebellar network was recently described as mediating an inhibitory capacity (Rubia et al., 2007), with the right IFG being implicated in motor response inhibition and the cerebellum assisting in both the preparation and inhibition of movements (Luna et al., 2001 and Mostofsky et al., 2003). In a study by Klingberg (2006), it was demonstrated that during a visuo-spatial working memory task, frontal areas were found to be directly connected with parietal cortices (Klingberg, 2006) and seemed to constitute a functional unit with the parietal region storing the spatial representation, while frontal regions were associated with maintaining the representation as well as manipulating items in working memory (Crone et al., 2006).

One might argue that the use of alternating runs in our experimental paradigm induced cognitive control and working memory demands across both task conditions and, that this might have contributed to largely overlapping brain activation patterns. Note however, that simple regression analysis did not reveal a significant impact of working memory on neural activation patterns and that no association was observed between task performance in the TD task and working memory performance. Thus, it seems unlikely that it was the experimental manipulation that primarily accounts for the shared brain activation patterns across tasks.

We only found a significant impact of working memory on SRC processing: subjects with higher working memory capacity had shorter RTs. Furthermore, working memory capacity interacted with trial type and task difficulty, showing that processing of spatially incompatible information was more demanding than compatible information, particularly if the frequency of incompatible trials was low (higher task difficulty). Thus, the correlation of neural activity in the superior parietal cortex by the right IFG during the SRC task might reflect a stronger recruitment of brain areas associated with working memory functions. Consistently, our results hint at a stronger impact of working memory processes on task performance in the SRC than in the TD task: the correlations between working memory and parameters of the TD Task were non-significant, and parietal activation was down-modulated in the TD Task. The most robust effect found in the PPI analysis of the TD Task was the coupling between neural activity in the right ifg and the cerebellum bilaterally, reflecting the described neural basis of time perception (Rubia and Smith, 2004) via the fronto-cerebellar pathways (Middleton and Strick, 1994).The role of the IFG as a top-down modulator is also supported by a recent study on effective connectivity during ‘spelling’ and ‘rhyming’ (Bitan et al., 2006). Similarly to our study, the authors found task-specific modulatory effects of the left inferior frontal gyrus on neural activation patterns in task-relevant structures. Of special interest is the lateralization of the connectivity patterns: the spatial tasks we used in our study revealed a right-hemispheric modulation in contrast to the left lateralized top-down modulation during the processing of language tasks observed in the study by Bitan et al. (2006).

Our results, however, showed that activation of the right IFG was not only positively correlated with activation in task-relevant regions, but also negatively correlated with the task-irrelevant regions. This correlation pattern was found in all brain regions except for the thalamus, which was positively correlated with neural activity in the IFG during both tasks. A similar pattern of effective connectivity has been described in a study on interhemispheric control mechanisms during the processing of spatial and letter tasks (Stephan et al., 2003). Here, a double dissociation was found between neural activation of the left ACC and the left-hemispheric letter-task-specific neural activation and the right ACC and the spatial-task specific brain activation in the right hemisphere. Taken together, these findings suggest that the right IFG is a crucial brain region for top-down control mechanisms within the network of spatial incompatibility and time perception.

However, Bitan et al. (2006) found that the modulatory effects of the IFG on other brain regions involved in the phonological decision were stronger in adults than in children aged 9 to 12 years. By contrast, in our study, fronto-cerebellar connectivity patterns did not show changes with age, neither linearly nor non-linearly. The lack of a significant age effect in our study might be due to the still ongoing maturation between the ages of 8 to 15, and thus, the difference between 8–11 year olds and 14 and 15 years olds might have been too small to be detected. Thus, comparing functional connectivity patterns in our sample with adults would be of high interest for future studies. However, in line with the results in the study by Bitan and colleagues, our data suggest that even at the age of 8 years, a basic neural implementation of top-down control already exists.

Developmental effects

As hypothesized, developmental changes were observed in particular with regard to cognitive control processes during the incompatibility task. Here, we found a linear increase of task accuracy with age (Casey et al., 2002 and Davidson et al., 2006). This developmental effect became even more obvious within the extreme group comparison. By contrast, accuracy in the TD task did not change with age, which is in line with previous behavioral studies (McCormack et al., 2005 and Clement and Droit-Volet, 2006). Task-specific brain activation patterns also showed non-linear developmental changes: in the SRC Task, increased neural activity was found in the left inferior parietal gyrus in adolescents as compared to younger children. This fits well with previous findings demonstrating left-hemispheric parietal activation patterns during a go/nogo paradigm (Rushworth et al., 1997), which has been associated with higher-level motor planning and response selection (Rubia et al., 2001 and Rushworth et al., 1998). The non-linear developmental increase in parietal activation in our study is also in line with a recent study by Brahmbhatt et al. (2008) who found age-related differences in the left parietal cortex activation for WM of word and face stimuli in adolescents compared to adults. In addition, our PPI analyses revealed that the activation in the left inferior parietal gyrus and the left thalamus was positively correlated with activation in the right IFG in the adolescents’ group only. As previously reported by Edin et al. (2007) for a spatial working memory task, this age-dependent increase in connectivity between fronto-parietal regions might help to integrate converging information relevant to a specific task and might explain the lower accuracy in our younger subjects who processed the task on the basis of a more immature neural network. Interestingly, a recent study by Grieve et al. (2007) suggested an association between neuropsychological measures of executive functions and fractional anisotropy in bilateral pathways from the prefrontal cortex to the parietal lobe, with projections to the anterior portions of the thalamus during cognitive aging. Thus, one may speculate that functional and structural connectivity in this particular brain network might be associated with both increase and decrease of executive functions across the life-span.

Finally, it should be noted that in line with many other developmental fMRI studies, we did not find any linear age effects on brain activation patterns across the age range of 8 to 15 years. Comparisons between the youngest and oldest subjects, however, showed significant group differences (see also Fair et al., 2006). Maybe physical and psychological changes might have raised the intersubject variance so that it was not possible to measure a continuous development. In summary, we found shared neural activation in a fronto-parieto-cerebellar network associated with both cognitive tasks. By contrast, task-specific patterns of functional connectivity could be observed. In the age range from 8 to 15 years, developmental changes in task performance and brain activation patterns were only observed during the interference control task, but not for the time discrimination task. While top-down modulation processes during time discrimination, such as coupling between the right IFG and cerebellar activity, were already fully established in this age range, developmental changes in functional connectivity could be observed during inference control with age-dependent increases in the fronto-thalamic and fronto-parietal interactions.
