Effects of age and sex on developmental neural networks of visual–spatial attention allocation

  • Katya Rubiaa, , ,
  • Zoe Hydea,
  • Rozmin Halaria,
  • Vincent Giampietrob,
  • Anna Smitha
  • a Department of Child and Adolescent Psychiatry, Institute of Psychiatry, King's College London, UK
  • b Department of Biostatistics and Computing and Neuroimaging, Institute of Psychiatry, King's College London, UK
Received 2 September 2009, Revised 11 February 2010, Accepted 19 February 2010, Available online 25 February 2010

Abstract

Compared to our understanding of the functional maturation of brain networks underlying complex cognitive abilities, hardly anything is known of the neurofunctional development of simpler cognitive abilities such as visuo-spatial attention allocation. Furthermore, nothing is known on the effect of gender on the functional development of attention allocation. This study employed event related functional magnetic resonance imaging to investigate effects of age, sex, and sex by age interactions on the brain activation of 63 males and females, between 13 to 38 years, during a visual–spatial oddball task. Behaviourally, with increasing age, speed was traded for accuracy, indicative of a less impulsive performance style in older subjects. Increasing age was associated with progressively increased activation in typical areas of selective attention of lateral fronto-striatal and temporo-parietal brain regions. Sex difference analysis showed enhanced activation in right-hemispheric inferior frontal and superior temporal regions in females, and in left-hemispheric inferior temporo-parietal regions in males. Importantly, the age by sex interaction findings showed that these sex-dimorphic patterns of brain activation may be the result of underlying sex differences in the functional maturation of these brain regions, as females had sex-specific progressive age-correlations in the same right inferior fronto-striato-temporal areas, while male-specific age-correlations were in left medial temporal and parietal areas.

The findings demonstrate progressive functional maturation of fronto-striato-parieto-temporal networks of the relatively simple function of attention allocation between early adolescence and mid-adulthood. They furthermore show that sex-dimorphic patterns of enhanced reliance on right inferior frontal, striatal and superior temporal regions in females and of left temporo-parietal regions in males during attention allocation may be the result of underlying sex differences in the functional maturation of these brain regions.

Introduction

Complex cognitive functions, such as interference inhibition, performance monitoring and cognitive flexibility, develop throughout adolescence and into adulthood (Brown et al., 2005, Davidson et al., 2006 and Levin et al., 1991). Simpler cognitive abilities such as selective attention and visuo-spatial attention are also thought to be progressively refined during this age range (Brodeur et al., 1997, Karatekin, 2004, Kwon et al., 2002, Plude et al., 1994 and Ross et al., 1994). Sex differences become more pronounced during this period of adolescence, concomitant with the hormonal changes of puberty (Sisk and Zehr, 2005) with females being more efficient than males in tasks of selective attention, verbal fluency and conductive reasoning (Anderson et al., 2001, Anderson, 2001, Klenberg et al., 2001 and Schaie, 1994), and males outperforming females in cognitive functions that rely on visual–spatial processing, especially mental rotation (Astur et al., 1998, Collins and Kimura, 1997, Dabbs et al., 1998, De Luca et al., 2003 and Weiss et al., 2003b).

During this time, and presumably underlying these changes, the brain continues to mature via processes such as synaptic remodelling and competitive elimination, programmed cell death and myelination (de Graaf-Peters and Hadders-Algra, 2006, Huttenlocher, 1994 and Liston et al., 2006). Structural imaging studies demonstrate a linear increase with age in white matter, presumably reflecting myelination, that peaks at around age 50, and a non-linear decrease in grey matter density, presumably reflecting synaptic pruning and myelination, up to age 40 (Sowell et al., 2003 and Sowell et al., 2007). These processes are heterochronous and heterogeneous with higher association areas in frontal, parietal and temporal regions maturing latest and primary sensory areas maturing earliest (Gogtay et al., 2004, Sowell et al., 2002, Sowell et al., 2003 and Sowell et al., 2004). Gender differences show that males exhibit steeper developmental slopes in grey matter reduction and white matter increase than females (De Bellis et al., 2001, Giedd et al., 1999 and Giedd et al., 2006), partly explained by earlier maturation peaks in females in frontal, striatal, temporal and parietal areas (Giedd et al., 1999 and Giedd et al., 2006). Gender differences in cognitive abilities may at least in part be explained by these sex differences in brain structure and its development (Gur et al., 1999).

In line with structural development, functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI) studies show progressive linear increases with age between childhood and adulthood in fronto-striatal and temporo-parietal activation, well into the third and fourth decade of life, during late developing cognitive functions such as cognitive control and attention (Adleman et al., 2002, Christakou et al., 2009, Crone et al., 2006, Marsh et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2007a and Smith et al., in press).

Relatively little, however, is known about the progressive functional development of simpler cognitive functions such as visual–spatial attention allocation. Also, hardly anything is known on gender differences on this brain function or its functional development. Attention is involuntarily captured by changes in the sensory environment and the ability to detect novel or unusual events in the environment is essential to survival (Downar et al., 2002). The detection of infrequent stimuli embedded in a train of frequently occurring standard stimuli can be investigated with the oddball task (Downar et al., 2000).

Attention allocation to infrequent compared to frequent visual target stimuli, whether targets exclusively require a motor response (Clark et al., 2000, Gur et al., 2007, Kiehl et al., 2001 and Kirino et al., 2000), a different motor response to frequent stimuli (Madden et al., 2004) or are mentally counted (Ardekani et al., 2002 and Kirino et al., 2000) activates a distributed neural network of prefrontal, inferior parietal, and temporo-occipital areas, as well as insula and cingulate. Studies of attention allocation that require a different response to infrequent than to frequent target trials, however, co-measure cognitive and motor switching (Rubia et al., 2007b). One way to address this is to measure the passive observation of infrequent compared to frequent stimuli, without requiring a motor response, which activates more restricted networks of inferior frontal, temporo-parietal, insula, anterior cingulate and supplementary motor areas (Downar et al., 2000 and Downar et al., 2002). However, the participants' behaviour is uncontrolled during such passive oddball tasks (Brinkman and Stauder, 2008 and Rubia et al., 2007b) and there may be reduced task engagement, especially in children and adolescents. An alternative to control for the motor response is to ask subjects to respond with the same motor response to both frequent and infrequent stimuli. To our knowledge, fMRI studies using such a paradigm have so far only been used in our lab, in children, finding similar activations as in passive oddball tasks, in dorsolateral and inferior prefrontal cortices, cingulate, basal ganglia, temporal and parieto-occipital regions (Lawrence et al., 2009, Rubia et al., 2007b and Rubia et al., 2009).

To our knowledge, however, there is no published developmental fMRI study on the oddball task. A study using evoked response potentials (ERP) during a passive auditory oddball task showed developmental changes in frontal and central regions from childhood to adulthood (Mueller et al., 2008). Also, no fMRI studies have investigated gender effects on brain activation or its maturation during oddball tasks.

Relatively few fMRI studies have studied sex differences in cognitive functions, finding increased prefrontal activation in females and increased parietal activation in males during tasks of working memory, mental rotation, cognitive switching and interference inhibition (Bell et al., 2006, Christakou et al., 2009, Garavan et al., 2006, Goldstein et al., 2005, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003a). To our knowledge, however, only one fMRI study has investigated sex differences in relation to the relatively simpler visual oddball task in healthy adults, finding no effects (Gur et al., 2007). ERP studies, however, have found a more negative central N2 component, associated with the orienting response to salient stimuli in men during a passive auditory oddball task (Nagy et al., 2003), and larger centrofrontal N2 and frontal P2 ERPs, associated with early perceptual processing, in females in a visual oddball task (Yuan et al., 2008).

To our knowledge, only three developmental imaging studies have investigated sex by age interactions on brain activation during cognitive tasks, finding no effects during a reversal task (Crone et al., 2006), exclusively female contributions to age-related increases in lenticular nucleus activation during interference inhibition, (Marsh et al., 2006) and exclusive female-specific age-correlations in inferior and medial prefrontal brain regions during interference inhibition and cognitive switching, and exclusive male age-correlations in superior parietal regions during switching and in temporal regions during interference inhibition (Christakou et al., 2009). These findings suggest that gender differences in frontal and parietal recruitment may be related to gender differences in the underlying neurofunctional maturation of these brain regions.

In this fMRI study we aimed to investigate effects of age, sex, and sex by age interaction on neurofunctional activation in a large sample of sixty-three adolescents and adults during a visual oddball task that controls for motor response-related brain activation, thus measuring pure orienting and allocation of attention to rare infrequent stimuli (Rubia et al., 2007b). Given evidence for a linear age-correlated activation increase in fronto-striatal regions during complex attention-demanding tasks well into the 40s (Adleman et al., 2002, Christakou et al., 2009, Crone et al., 2006, Marsh et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2006 and Rubia et al., 2007a), we hypothesised that there would be increased activation with age in late developing frontal, striatal and parieto-temporal areas that mediate simple attention allocation (Downar et al., 2000, Downar et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2007a and Rubia et al., 2007b).

Given previous evidence for increased frontal activation in females and increased parietal activation in males during more complex cognitive tasks (Bell et al., 2006, Christakou et al., 2009, Garavan et al., 2006, Goldstein et al., 2005, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003a) and ERP evidence for enhanced frontal activation in females during oddball tasks, we expected to observe enhanced frontal activation in females and enhanced parietal activation in males during visuo-spatial attention allocation. Furthermore, in line with our previous age by gender interaction findings during higher cognitive control tasks (Christakou et al., 2009), we hypothesised that brain regions that differed in activation between males and females would be related to underlying sex differences in the neurofunctional maturation of these brain regions, with more pronounced maturation of parietal regions in males and frontal regions in females.

Methods and materials

Participants

Sixty-three right-handed healthy adolescents and adults, 38 males and 25 females, ranging in age from 13 to 38 (mean age in years (SD) = 21 (6)) were recruited through an advertisement. Exclusion criteria were current or past substance abuse, head injury, mental retardation, or mental, endocrine or neurological disorders. All participants scored above the 10th percentile (IQ estimate over 80) on the Raven's Standard Progressive Matrices test (Raven, 1960) of performance intelligence quotient (IQ) (Mean Performance IQ estimate (SD) = 107 (12); male IQ estimate = 108 (12); female IQ estimate = 106 (13)).

The 38 males (mean age (SD) = 20 (7) and 25 females (mean age (SD) = 22 (6)) did not differ significantly in their ages (t = − 1; df = 61; p = n.s.). There were no significant sex differences in converted performance IQ estimates (t = − 0.7; df = 34, p = n.s.). The study was approved by the local Ethics Committee and all participants gave informed consent and received £30 for their participation.

Oddball paradigm

Task administration

A video projector was used to present the task, which participants viewed on a mirror inside the MRI scanner. Behavioural response data were recorded through a key pad onto a time-locked automated PC system. All participants were trained once on the task. A randomised presentation, rapid, event related fMRI design was used with jittered inter-trial intervals as well as random events with randomised inter-target intervals to optimise statistical efficiency (Dale, 1999).

Oddball task

The oddball task has been described in detail elsewhere (Rubia et al., 2007b and Rubia et al., 2009). It consisted of 208 horizontal arrows that are presented on the right or left side of the screen for 600 ms, followed by a blank screen for an average inter-trial-interval (ITI) of 1.8 s (jittered between 1.6 and 2.4 s). In 160 trials the standard stimuli were horizontal arrows pointing either right or left with equal probability and participants were instructed to press the right or left button that corresponded to the arrow direction with their right or left thumb on a gamepad. In 24 pseudo-randomly interspersed trials, oddball stimuli occurred unpredictably (randomised after either 5.4 s, 7.2 s or of 9 s, including the small jitter of the ITI) consisting of slightly tilted arrows (at a 23° angle) that appeared on the right or left side of the screen, and pointed right or left like the standard stimuli for 600 ms. Participants were instructed to press the right or left button according to the arrow direction, as with the standard stimuli. All oddball stimuli were at least 3 repetition times (TR) apart from each other to allow adequate separation of the hemodynamic response. There were also 24 incongruent stimuli, measuring interference inhibition, the analysis of which was published elsewhere (Christakou et al., 2009).

Behavioural variables were the mean reaction times (MRT) and number of errors on both congruent and oddball trials and the oddball effect on speed (MRTODDBALL − MRTCONGRUENT).

For fMRI data analysis, the standard frequent trials were used as an implicit un-modelled baseline that was used in the modelling of the BOLD response of the oddball and incongruent interference trials. Only results from the oddball condition of the experiment are reported here.

Performance data analysis

The effect of sex on the dependent variables was analysed using multivariate analysis of variance (MANOVA) with sex as the independent factor. Linear effects of age on behavioural performance variables were investigated using two tailed Pearson correlation analyses.

Functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI)

Image acquisition

Gradient echo-planar magnetic resonance (MR) imaging data were acquired on a GE Signa 1.5 T Horizon LX System (General Electric, Milwaukee, WI, USA) at the Maudsley Hospital, London. Reliable image quality was obtained by using a semi-automated quality control procedure. A quadrature birdcage headcoil was used for radiofrequency transmission and reception. In each of 16 non-contiguous planes parallel to the anterior–posterior commissure, 208 T2*-weighted MR images depicting blood oxygen level dependent (BOLD) contrast covering the whole brain were acquired with echo time (TE) = 40 ms, TR = 1.8 s, flip angle = 90°, in-plane resolution = 3.1 mm, slice thickness = 7 mm and slice-skip = 0.7 mm.

At the same time, a high-resolution inversion recovery echo-planar image of the whole brain was acquired in the inter-commissural plane with TE = 40 ms, TI = 180 ms, TR = 16,000 ms, in-plane resolution = 1.5 mm, slice thickness = 3 mm, slice-skip = 0.3 mm. This EPI dataset provided almost complete brain coverage.

fMRI data analysis

XBAM (Brammer et al., 1997), the method of fMRI analysis used, makes no assumptions of normality, which are often violated in fMRI data, but instead uses median statistics to control outlier effects and permutation rather than normal theory based inference. Furthermore the most common test statistic is computed by first standardising for individual difference in residual noise, before embarking on second level, multi-subject testing using robust permutation-based methods. This allows for a mixed effects approach to analysis, which has been recommended after an analysis of the validity and impact of normal theory based inference in fMRI analysis in group studies (Thirion et al., 2007).

Individual analysis

Firstly, fMRI data were realigned in order to minimise movement artefacts (Bullmore et al., 1999a) and smoothed using a Gaussian filter (full-width half maximum, 7.2 mm). Time-series analysis of individual subject activation was performed using XBAM, with a wavelet-based re-sampling method (described in Bullmore et al., 2001). Briefly, we first convolved the two experimental conditions (i.e. the 24 congruent oddball trials as well as the 24 incongruent trials) with two Poisson model functions (delays of 4 and 8 s). Standard congruent stimuli were not convolved with the Poisson functions, but they formed a saturated baseline condition used in the modelling of the two experimental conditions. The 24 tilted oddball stimuli (as well as the 24 incongruent stimuli) are thus convoluted against un-modelled frequent standard stimuli that form the implicit baseline. The use of frequent standard stimuli as implicit baseline was done to control for motor responses (that are made both in response to the frequent baseline stimuli and in response to the oddball stimuli) and hence to test for the effect of visual–spatial attention to saliency independently of the confound of a motor response. The interference trials were not further analysed in this study as we have already published the findings of this part of the experiment previously (Christakou et al., 2009). We then calculated the weighted sum of these two convolutions that gave the best fit (least-squares) to the time series at each voxel. A goodness-of-fit statistic (the SSQ-ratio) was then computed at each voxel consisting of the ratio of the sum of squares of deviations from the mean intensity value due to the model (fitted time series) divided by the sum of squares due to the residuals (original time series minus model time series). The appropriate null distribution for assessing significance of any given SSQ-ratio was established using the wavelet-based data re-sampling method (Bullmore et al., 2001) and applying the model-fitting process to the re-sampled data. This process was repeated 20 times at each voxel and the data combined over all voxels, resulting in 20 null parametric maps of SSQ-ratio for each subject, which were combined to give the overall null distribution of SSQ-ratio. The same permutation strategy was applied at each voxel to preserve spatial correlation structure in the data. The null distribution can then be used to threshold the activation maps at any desired type I error rate. The resulting activation map hence reflects activation associated with oddball trials relative to standard frequent trials that formed the implicit baseline, measuring visual spatial attention to rare stimuli controlling for activation associated with motor response.

Given the way in which we modelled our single-subject fMRI data, it is not possible to conclusively know whether or not age has affected the brain's response to standard stimuli, which we are using as an implicitly-defined baseline against which we model oddball brain response. Several factors argue against this being a study confound. First, our primary study analyses looks at age-related change to a single-task condition (infrequent oddball stimuli). The effectively “saturated” hemodynamic response to standard stimuli in this implicit baseline means that hemodynamic change cannot be clearly or accurately resolved. However, it is improbable that the frequent, overlapping hemodynamic response represents anything other than an average signal, or that different brain regions will show evidence for specific age-related effects. Second, numerous previous oddball fMRI studies have shown that the majority of brain BOLD signal changes to infrequent oddball stimuli are positive (including in our hypothesised regions). “Deactivations” in these paradigms occur only in a few reasonably-well described regions. Our analyses are careful to account for the possibility of negative signal change across different brain regions, so we do not run the risk of falsely interpreting our results. Finally, comparison of alternative regression models for this type of paradigm suggests that this approach can accurately identify and quantify hemodynamic change to infrequent stimuli.

Group analysis

The individual maps were registered into standard Talairach space, beginning with rigid body transformation of the fMRI data into an inversion recovery image of the same subject and followed by affine transformation onto a Talairach template (Talairach and Tournoux, 1988). A group brain activation map (GBAM) for the task was then produced by calculating the median observed SSQ-ratio over all subjects at each voxel in standard space and testing them against the null distribution of median SSQ-ratios computed from the identically transformed wavelet re-sampled data (Brammer et al., 1997). The voxel-level threshold was first set to 0.05 to give maximum sensitivity and to avoid type II errors. Next, a cluster-level threshold was computed for the resulting 3D voxel clusters such that the final expected number of type I error clusters was < 1 per whole brain. The necessary combination of voxel and cluster-level thresholds was not assumed from theory but rather was determined by direct permutation for each data set, giving excellent Type II error control (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Cluster mass rather than a cluster extent threshold was used, to minimise discrimination against possible small, strongly responding foci of activation (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Briefly, a voxel-wide significance threshold was set (p < 0.05), and surviving voxels were assembled into 3-D clusters using a contiguity criterion. The mass of each cluster was calculated by adding the statistical values of all cluster members and thresholded at p < 0.01. For this group activation analysis, less than one false activated cluster was expected at a p-value of < 0.05 at the voxel level and p < 0.01 for cluster comparisons.

ANOVA for sex differences in brain activation

Analysis of variance was conducted for between-group differences using a randomisation-based test for voxel- and cluster-wise activation differences (Bullmore et al., 1999b). Less than one false positive activation was expected at p < 0.05 at voxel level and p < 0.01 at cluster level.

First, the difference between the mean SSQ-ratio values in each group was calculated at each voxel. The mean ratio was then recalculated 1000 times at each voxel following random permutation of group membership and the difference in SSQ-ratios was calculated after each permutation. The same set of random numbers was employed for the permutation at each voxel to preserve spatial correlations in each permuted map. The probability of the original SSQ-ratio difference under the null hypothesis of no effect of group membership is the number of times we observed an SSQ-ratio difference as large as or larger than the original difference during the permutation process, divided by the total number of permutations. If this value exceeded our threshold for voxel-level activation, activated voxels were used to identify activation clusters, which were subjected to cluster analysis.

Whole-brain correlations between brain activation and age across all subjects

To test for a linear correlation between whole-brain activation and age, the Pearson product–moment correlation coefficient was first computed at each voxel in standard space between age data and signal change over all subjects. The correlation coefficients were recalculated after randomly permuting the ages but not the fMRI data. Repeating the second step many times (1000 times per voxel, then combining over all voxels) gives the distribution of correlation coefficients under the null hypothesis that there is no association between specific ages and specific BOLD effects. This null distribution can then be used to assess the probability of any particular correlation coefficient under the null hypothesis. The critical value of the correlation coefficient at any desired type 1 error level in the original (non-permuted) data could be determined by reference to this distribution. In this data, less than one error cluster was observed at a p-value of p < 0.05 for voxel and p < 0.03 for cluster analyses. Statistical analysis was extended to cluster level as described previously ( Bullmore et al., 1999b).

Age by sex interaction effects: sex differences in whole-brain correlations between brain activation and age

In order to test whether gender had differential effects on the linear age-correlations across the whole brain, sex differences were examined in the correlation coefficients of brain activation with age. For each group independently, the average Pearson product moment correlation coefficient between subject age and fMRI response was computed, and the difference in correlation between the two groups calculated. To determine the significance of this difference, the appropriate null distribution was generated by randomly permuting subjects and their ages between the groups (without replacement), thus scrambling any group differences. For each of the permutations the difference in correlation between the scrambled groups was calculated and the resulting values were combined over all voxels to produce a whole-brain null distribution of differences in correlation. Testing was then extended to cluster level, with the cluster probability under the null hypothesis chosen to set the level of expected type I error clusters at less than one. Less than one error cluster was observed with a p-value of p < 0.05 for voxel and p < 0.008 for cluster analyses. Areas where either sex showed exclusive significant progressive or regressive changes are reported.

For all experimental contrasts, information can be obtained about the size, and also the direction of the activation from the general linear model fit to the time series of activation. The sign of the BOLD response can either be positive or negative with respect to the regressor (the implicit baseline in this case). We were careful to consider the possibility of negative BOLD signal change by examining the sign of the signal change relative to the regressor, particularly in brain regions known from prior research to be areas that “deactivate” relative to various baseline conditions. For all analyses only BOLD responses were considered where the average SSQ-ratio in response to the activation condition was positive.

For all analyses the brain extraction tool from the FSL (FMRIB Software Library) software package (Smith, 2002) was used to create a grey matter mask of the Talairach template used for normalisation. This mask was then subsequently used to restrict the analysis to those voxels lying within the grey matter mask.

Results

Behavioural performance

Across all subjects there was an inverse relationship between mean response time (MRT) and number of errors. An increased MRT was negatively correlated with a decreased number of errors for both congruent (r = −.6, p = 0.0001) and oddball trials (r = −.4, p = 0.002).

No sex differences were observed in any of the behavioural variables, with the exception of the oddball MRT effect that was significantly larger in females compared to males (see Table 1).

Activation in right posterior cingulate that was increased with increasing age, showed a significant negative correlation with the oddball reaction time effect (r = −.3, p < 0.007).

Activation in the right rostromedial frontal gyrus, that was negatively correlated with age, was positively correlated with errors on the oddball trials (r = 0.3, p < 0.04). Some of the negatively age-correlated regions were also negatively correlated with the oddball effect, including right middle temporal (r = −0.3, p < .007), right mesial frontal (r = −0.3, p < 04) and posterior cingulate gyri (r = − 0.3, p = 0.01), suggesting that as activation decreased in this region with increasing age, the oddball effect progressively increased. It thus appears that these activation clusters that were increased in activation in younger subjects, are related to the more immature adolescent performance pattern of faster Oddball RT effects and consequently enhanced inaccuracy.

Age by sex interaction analyses showed that there was a significant positive correlation between age and activation during oddball trials in females compared to males in right-hemispheric regions of inferior frontal cortex reaching into the insula, putamen and thalamus, superior temporal lobe and precuneus as well as in left premotor cortex (Table 5, Fig. 4). Conversely, males compared to females, demonstrated increased activation with age in left medial frontal, left medial temporal and inferior parietal regions and left posterior cingulate as well as in mid-precuneus and bilateral occipital cortices (Table 5, Fig. 4). The cluster in posterior cingulate correlated negatively with the oddball RT effect which was smaller in males than females (r = −0.3, p < 0.01).

Discussion

This study investigated the effects of age, sex, and age by sex interaction on brain activation during a visual oddball task that controlled for motor response-related brain activation and measured visual attention allocation to saliency, involving several processes such as target discrimination and evaluation, attention orienting and selective and sustained attention. Increasing age was associated with a more reflective performance strategy, where speed was traded for accuracy, and paralleled by linear progressive activation increases with age in bilateral frontal, striatal, temporal and superior and inferior parietal cortices. Decreasing activation with age was observed in right medial frontal gyri, mid-cingulate/SMA and cerebellum. Gender differences showed enhanced right-hemispheric inferior frontal, striatal and superior temporal activation in females and enhanced left inferior and superior (precuneus) parietal activation and posterior cingulate in males. Most importantly, the sex by age interaction analyses revealed that these sex-dimorphic activation clusters were positively-age-correlated in a sex-specific manner and thus the result of underlying sex differences in the functional progressive development of these areas.

Age effects

Across all subjects, visual oddball stimuli elicited activation in a predominantly bilateral network of superior and inferior frontal gyri, striatum, posterior cingulate/precuneus and medial temporal cortices, in line with previous fMRI findings (Ardekani et al., 2002, Clark et al., 2000, Downar et al., 2000, Downar et al., 2002, Gur et al., 2007, Kiehl et al., 2001, Lawrence et al., 2009, Linden et al., 1999, Madden et al., 2004, Rubia et al., 2007b and Rubia et al., 2009).

Increasing age resulted in a more reflective, less impulsive performance style, favouring accuracy by sacrificing speed, a typical developmental effect previously observed in higher cognitive switching and interference inhibition tasks (Christakou et al., 2009 and Davidson et al., 2006).

Whole-brain analyses revealed progressive age-correlated activation in areas crucial to saliency detection, namely inferior and dorsolateral prefrontal, temporal and inferior and superior parietal cortices, caudate, insula and posterior cingulate. The inferior prefrontal cortex (Clark et al., 2000, Clark et al., 2002, Downar et al., 2002, Kiehl et al., 2001, Kiehl et al., 2005, Kirino et al., 2000 and Derrfuss et al., 2005) and the caudate (Davidson et al., 2006, Gur et al., 2007, Madden et al., 2004, Zink et al., 2003, Zink et al., 2006, Brazdil et al., 2005, Kiehl et al., 2001, Koski et al., 1999, Rubia et al., 2007b and Stevens et al., 2005) are crucial for the updating of information and form a network of executive control of detecting and responding to targets. The temporo-parietal junction mediates the identification of change and saliency in the environment (Downar et al., 2000, Downar et al., 2002, Brazdil et al., 2007, Corbetta et al., 2000 and Corbetta and Shulman, 2002), presumably due to the integration of information about an object's location or form by the superior temporal lobe (Karnath, 2001, Op de Beeck et al., 2000 and Marois et al., 2000) and the role of the inferior parietal lobe in visual–spatial selective attention (Ardekani et al., 2002, Clark et al., 2000, Gur et al., 2007 and Kiehl et al., 2001). Posterior cingulate and precuneus are connected to the limbic system and visuo-motor pathways and mediate the dynamic allocation of visual–spatial attention to salience (Ardekani et al., 2002, Clark et al., 2000, Gur et al., 2007, Kiehl et al., 2001, Mesulam et al., 2001, Mitelman et al., 2005, Mohanty et al., 2008 and Small et al., 2003). Our findings of progressive functional maturation of prefrontal, temporo-parietal and striatal regions between childhood and adulthood during a relatively simple task of attention allocation extend previous developmental fMRI findings of progressive age-correlated activation in these regions during more complex, higher-level inhibition, attention and working memory tasks (Adleman et al., 2002, Booth et al., 2003, Bunge et al., 2002, Christakou et al., 2009, Klingberg et al., 2002, Konrad et al., 2005, Kwon et al., 2002, Marsh et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2007a and Smith et al., in press) and hence demonstrate that functional activation increase in these regions is already underlying the cognitive development of relatively simple perceptive attention allocation functions.

Regressive changes with age were observed in ventromedial and rostromesial frontal, as well as mid-cingulate, middle temporal and occipito-cerebellar brain regions. Several of these brain regions (right mesial frontal, middle temporal and mid-cingulate) were associated with the more impulsive performance pattern typical for younger subjects. Mesial frontal cortex, including anterior cingulate mediates error processing and performance monitoring (Ridderinkhof et al., 2004, Ullsperger and von Cramon, 2001, Rubia et al., 2003, Rubia et al., 2006 and Rubia et al., 2007a) and may have been triggered by the larger amount of errors in younger subjects, demanding increased cognitive control. The findings are in line with evidence for enhanced medial frontal ERP activation in children compared to adults during an auditory oddball task (Mueller et al., 2008). The regressive age-correlated activation changes extend fMRI findings of enhanced activation in these posterior regions in children compared to adults during more complex cognitive inhibitory (Booth et al., 2003, Bunge et al., 2002, Casey et al., 1997, Durston et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2006 and Rubia et al., 2007a), switching (Christakou et al., 2009 and Rubia et al., 2006), and sustained attention functions (Smith et al., in press).

The increased activation in younger subjects in these earlier maturing brain areas (Gogtay et al., 2004 and Sowell et al., 2004) is likely a compensation for the reduced activation in the more task-relevant, but still structurally (Gogtay et al., 2004) and functionally (Adleman et al., 2002, Christakou et al., 2009, Konrad et al., 2005, Kwon et al., 2002, Rubia et al., 2000, Rubia et al., 2006, Rubia et al., 2007a and Smith et al., in press) maturing fronto-striatal, parietal and temporal cortices. It may reflect enhanced reliance on primary sensory functions mediated by occipital and inferior temporal regions (Corbetta and Shulman, 2002) rather than on later maturing fronto-striatal and temporo-parietal selective attention networks causing more immature performance patterns of enhanced errors and reduced oddball reaction times.

Taken together, the increased activation with age in inferior fronto-striatal, and superior temporo-parietal areas, as well as the attenuation of activation with age in earlier developing medial frontal, mid-cingulate and inferior temporo-occipital cortices, appears to constitute the neural correlates of the performance style of older subjects, where speed is progressively traded for accuracy.

Sex differences and sex by age interaction effects

Females had a larger oddball reaction time effect, in line with evidence for poorer visual–spatial selective attention in females (Feng et al., 2007). Females, compared to males, had increased activation in right-hemispheric inferior frontal, insula, putamen and superior temporal regions, while males had increased activation in left-hemispheric medial temporal/inferior parietal lobes and mid-precuneus. Enhanced frontal and/or superior temporal activation in females compared to enhanced parietal activation in males is in line with ERP findings using similar oddball tasks (Yuan et al., 2008 and Williams et al., 2005) as well as with fMRI findings during basic visuo-spatial (Clements-Stephens et al., 2009 and Gur et al., 2007) and more complex tasks such as mental rotation (Hugdahl et al., 2006, Thomsen et al., 2000 and Weiss et al., 2003a), working memory (Bell et al., 2006 and Goldstein et al., 2005) and interference inhibition (Christakou et al., 2009).

Given that all these brain regions are part of a network for attention allocation to oddball stimuli (Ardekani et al., 2002, Clark et al., 2000, Downar et al., 2000, Gur et al., 2007, Kiehl et al., 2001, Madden et al., 2004 and Rubia et al., 2007b), females and males appeared to differ in their respective recruitment of these task-relevant brain regions. The inferior frontal and superior temporal areas are important for stimulus evaluation, the update of information and its contextual, cognitive integration, and top-down executive control of selective attention and response selection (Derrfuss et al., 2005, Downar et al., 2002, Kiehl et al., 2001 and Peers et al., 2005), while medial temporal and parietal regions including posterior cingulate and precuneus are crucial for visual–spatial sensory saliency detection due to their role in the encoding of stimulus properties such as location (tilted stimuli in this task) (Brazdil et al., 2007 and Corbetta et al., 2000). The inferior parietal lobe in particular is crucial for selective spatial attention (Yantis and Serences, 2003 and Serences and Yantis, 2006) and attention orientation and shifting (Lepsien and Nobre, 2006 and Kiehl et al., 2001), basic functions underlying oddball detection (Kiehl et al., 2001). The enhanced activation in males in parietal areas that are crucial for spatial orientation and selective attention could explain their performance superiority in the oddball reaction time effect. Males seem thus to have relied more on bottom-up parietal visual–spatial perception mechanisms for stimulus saliency processing, while females appear to have relied more on top-down fronto-striato-temporal executive control of selective attention, which may be slower and less effective than more automatic bottom-up processes used by males. This is in line with the hypothesis that during similar perceptive flexibility tasks (e.g. mental rotation), men utilise a parietal lobe mediated gestalt strategy in contrast to women who use a more frontal analytic/serial strategy (Thomsen et al., 2000). It is also supported by evidence from electrophysiological studies of enhanced synchrony of high-frequency oscillations, thought to underlie functional network integration, in females in frontal, and males in parieto-occipital regions during perceptual search in an auditory oddball task (Williams et al., 2005). The laterality effects of enhanced right fronto-temporal recruitment in females and enhanced left parietal and posterior cingulate recruitment in males have previously been observed in other cognitive contexts (Clements-Stephens et al., 2009, Gur et al., 2000, Gur et al., 2007 and Hugdahl et al., 2006) and are in line with evidence for more bilateral parietal activation in males during visual–spatial tasks that are more right-hemispheric in females (Gur et al., 2000 and Gur et al., 2007). This stronger bilateral parietal recruitment in males could potentially also be related to male superiority in visual–spatial processes.

The age by sex interaction effects provide developmental underpinnings of the sex-dimorphic brain activation differences. As hypothesised, the same brain regions that differed between the sexes also differed in their sex-specific functional maturation, with exclusively female activation increases with age in right inferior frontal, putamen and superior temporal regions, and exclusively male age-correlated activation increases in left medial temporal/inferior parietal lobes, precuneus and posterior cingulate. Thus, with increasing age these cortical areas progressively take over function in a sex-specific manner.

Right inferior frontal and superior temporal lobes were positively-age-correlated across all subjects, suggesting that females had the more mature activation patterns in these regions, in line with evidence of earlier structural development of these regions in females (Campbell et al., 2005, Giedd et al., 1999, Giedd et al., 2006 and Lenroot et al., 2007). Conversely, the sex by age interaction effects for males in the parietal activation clusters, appear to be male-specific, more mature activation patterns, since these areas were age-correlated in all subjects. Structurally, the parietal lobe is known to be larger in grey matter volume in males (Frederikse et al., 1999). The steeper functional maturation of left parietal and cingulate brain regions in males appears to be related to the superior performance in the oddball reaction time effect, which was negatively correlated with posterior cingulate activation. This is in line with evidence that activation in this region promotes rapid target detection in spatial attention tasks (Mesulam et al., 2001). The male-specific enhanced parietal and cingulate maturation may thus underlie the superior performance in males in this simple task as well as in more complex visual–spatial information processing and selective attention functions (Astur et al., 1998, Collins and Kimura, 1997, Dabbs et al., 1998, De Luca et al., 2003 and Weiss et al., 2003b) that are mediated by these regions (Coull and Frith, 1998, Yantis and Serences, 2003, Lepsien and Nobre, 2006 and Kiehl et al., 2001).

Overall, the sex by age interaction effects indicate that sex-specific differences in brain activation may be the result of underlying sex-dimorphic functional maturation processes. Strikingly similar sex-specific activation increases with age were observed in the same regions during a more difficult visual–spatial switching task, presumably related to similarly different performance strategies of enhanced top-down frontal inhibitory control in females and enhanced visual–spatial parietal switching abilities in males (Christakou et al., 2009). Together, these developmental imaging findings demonstrate that sex differences observed in task-related brain activation appear to be related to sex differences in the underlying progressive functional development of these brain regions, stressing the importance of including developmental data when investigating gender effects on neural brain activation.

Limitations

The strength of this study is the relatively large sample size, the use of permutation fMRI data analyses across the whole brain, and the sex by age interaction analysis of whole-brain activation. A limitation is the relatively small number of trials of interest which may have limited the statistical power of the analyses. Oddball trials, however, are by nature of the task demands relatively low in number in order to serve as “odd” trials compared to the high-frequency standard trials. A further limitation of the design is the modelling of the frequent stimuli as implicit baseline, which makes it not possible to analyse age-related effects to the standard stimuli compared to a resting baseline. While we consider it unlikely to be a study confound, future study designs should replicate and extend these results by using separately modelled standard and oddball stimuli.

Also, in this study we primarily explored linear correlations between age and brain activation and gender effects. However, non-linear developmental changes have been observed in functional imaging (Brown et al., 2005), and should be investigated in future studies of larger sample sizes. Furthermore, cross-sectional studies are confounded by cohort effects and gender effects on functional brain development could be more thoroughly investigated using longitudinal imaging studies.

Development of brain function is likely to be closely linked to structural brain development (Olesen et al., 2003). To what extent the age-correlated BOLD changes in this and other developmental functional imaging studies are related to age-correlated grey matter changes needs to be established in future developmental imaging studies that combine functional and structural imaging data.
